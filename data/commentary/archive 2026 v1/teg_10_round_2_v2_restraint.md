# Personal Bests, Personal Worsts: Palmares Sorts the Field {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 10 | Round 2 | 01/10/2017 | Palmares - Praia / Alvor</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> GW 48 | AB 45 | JB 45 | DM 42 | SN 37 | JP 35</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> JB +10 | DM +11 | GW +14 | AB +23 | JP +25 | SN +33</p>

Palmares, on its TEG debut, did what a Robert Trent Jones Jr. layout is supposed to do: it separated people. At one end of the field, two players posted the best rounds of their TEG lives. At the other, John Patterson opened with a 10 and Stuart Neumann closed with one. And somewhere in the middle, calmly making pars after par, was David Mullin — who simply kept doing what he has been doing all week, while everyone else either flew or burned.

**Gregg Williams** had the round. A personal-best **48 points**, gross +14, and comfortably the best raw scoring of the day. The engine room was a five-hole burst from the 8th to the 12th worth 18 Stableford points: par at the 8th, par at the par-5 9th, a birdie at the par-4 10th, par at the par-3 11th, bogey at the 12th. He didn't then let it drift either, closing with 12 points across the last four — pars at 15 and 16, bogeys at the par-5 17th and the par-4 18th. Tidy at both ends, devastating in the middle. Williams climbs into joint second on the Trophy.

**Alex Baker** matched him for personal-best status, if not for total: a PB **45 points**, gross +23. He was out of the gate quickly, picking up 9 points across the opening three holes (bogey, bogey, par at the par-4 1st, par-5 2nd and par-3 3rd), and then collected 10 more across 5-7 in the same bogey-bogey-par rhythm. The turn into the back nine was less kind — a three-hole wobble at 12, 13 and 14 produced doubles at each and cost six shots in a hurry — but the damage was contained rather than terminal. Enough to draw level with Williams on 86, the two now joint second on the Trophy.

**Jon Baker** played a properly stylish front nine. He opened with 10 points across the first three holes — par, birdie at the par-5 2nd, par — before a double-bogey 7 at the par-5 4th briefly broke the spell. He then responded with six consecutive pars from the 5th through the 10th, 18 points in a row. Forty points all told, fourth on the Trophy, and still the clear runner-up to Mullin in the Jacket race.

Which brings us to **Mullin**. The metronome did not stop. From the 4th he reeled off five straight pars — par-5, par-3, par-4, par-4, par-3 — for 15 points, the kind of unhurried stretch that explains why nobody in this field ever quite catches him even on days they outscore him. A double-bogey 7 at the par-5 9th closed the run and trimmed the gloss, but only slightly. He held both leads, as he was always going to.

**Stuart Neumann** played, essentially, two rounds in one. From the 9th to the 12th he was thoroughly composed: par at the par-5 9th, bogey at the par-4 10th, par at the par-3 11th, par at the par-4 12th — 14 points and a man going about his business. Then the 13th tee, and a back nine that became its own kind of folklore. A 10 at the par-5 13th (+5). A 9 at the par-4 14th (+5). A triple-bogey 7 at the par-4 15th (+3). Thirteen shots dropped over par in three holes. For good measure he signed off with a sextuple-bogey 10 at the 18th. The day's best front-nine recovery and worst back-nine collapse, packed into the same scorecard.

**Patterson**, meanwhile, was effectively done before some of the field had reached the 2nd tee. A sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 1st is not the sort of opening from which the rest of an afternoon recovers, and his didn't. He remains bottom of the Trophy and — despite Neumann offering every conceivable assistance over those 13th, 14th, 15th and 18th holes — the gap to safety has somehow widened.

The Trophy gap halved-ish — from 8 to 5 — as Williams and Baker piled into joint second, but nobody actually overtook Mullin and the Jacket lead remains a 13-shot chasm. At the bottom, the Spoon margin perversely *grew* from 3 to 5: Neumann's back-nine bonfire was eye-watering, but Patterson's opening 10 was simply more damaging than even a quintuple-quintuple-triple finish at 13-15. Palmares sorted the field; it did not reorder it.
