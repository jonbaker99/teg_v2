# The King of Kent: Mullin's Coronation by the Sea

## The 10th tee, Prince's, Sunday afternoon

Picture the scene. David Mullin stands on the 10th tee on the final day at Prince's, nine points clear in the Trophy, twelve ahead on gross, the salt air thick with the scent of inevitability and a Green Jacket already half-fitted across his shoulders. Three rounds of links golf along the Kent coast have been a procession. The chasers have been politely waved through to fight for the minor places. The leaderboard reads less like a contest than a coronation timetable.

He then makes an 8.

For roughly forty-five minutes thereafter — as Alex Baker stitched together pars at Prince's and the cushion shrank, and shrank again — TEG's second pilgrimage to Kent threatened, very briefly, to become a story rather than a stroll. It didn't. But the fact that we have to ask the question at all is the only smudge on what was otherwise a magnificently controlled four-day demolition.

This is how Mullin made Kent his own — and how he very nearly mislaid it on the way home.

## Round 1 — Deal Me In: Mullin Seizes Cinque Ports

Royal Cinque Ports, two-time Open host and not a course that suffers the loose swing gladly, did what Deal always does: it sorted people out. The wind came in off the Channel and the field came apart at the seams. Mullin alone seemed to have read the right memo.

The decisive sequence came in a four-hole burst from the 9th. A par at the 9th drew him level for the gross lead — the only time anyone got close to him on strokes all week. Then a birdie at the par-4 10th, a par at the par-4 11th to square the Stableford race, and a par at the par-5 16th to nudge outright in front. Thirteen points in four holes, both leads taken, and we were essentially done. He signed for 40, three clear on net and five on gross.

Behind him, the carnage was already being itemised. Alex Baker — destined to be both Mullin's most credible threat and his most generous accomplice — coughed up a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 11th and then, with rather more flair, a quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 16th. File that 16th away; it will be back. Gregg Williams contributed a 9 at the par-5 3rd to set the tone of his quietly bruised week.

And then there was Jon Baker. A bogey at the par-3 4th dropped him to the bottom of the Spoon table. He would not budge from there for the next 68 holes. One imagines him glancing up from the scorecard at the 5th tee and finding, with mild surprise, that the cellar door had locked behind him.

## Round 2 — Littlestone, Big Lead

If Deal sorted people out, Littlestone settled the argument. On the Romney Marshes — Purves, Braid and MacKenzie's pedigree quietly underwriting the test — Mullin produced the round of the tournament, a 41-pointer in which his bad holes were merely the runway for his good ones.

Twice he started to bleed and twice he reached for the tourniquet himself. After a triple at the par-4 4th, a double at the par-5 5th and a bogey at the par-3 6th had threatened to derail things, he birdied the par-5 7th. Steady. Later, when bogeys at the 15th and 16th and a double at the par-3 17th began to look ominous, he birdied the par-5 18th to close. The man simply refused to let a wobble become anything more.

That he could do this while Alex Baker was busily handing back 12 shots in four holes — triple, double, triple, and a quad 9 at the par-5 5th, with a further quadruple 8 thrown in at the par-4 8th — turned a three-shot Stableford cushion into nine. The gross gap stretched to twelve. By Saturday night the Trophy and Jacket conversations were both, in any meaningful sense, over.

## Round 3 — Prince's of the Realm

The first of two days on Prince's Shore/Dunes loop produced the only genuine evidence that Mullin was mortal. A quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 8th was an actual mistake — the sort that, on a normal week, would be hauled up and prodded.

But because the cushion was nine, and because Mullin had decided that the way to make a long lead feel comfortable was to close like a man who hadn't noticed it, he answered with a four-hole flourish from 15 to 18: par, par, par at the par-3 17th, birdie at the par-4 18th. Thirteen points home. A 36-point round; the Stableford lead held at eight, the gross margin at twelve, and any sense that the door might be ajar firmly resealed.

Everyone else was busy posting evidence for the defence. Alex Baker shed six shots in three holes from the 1st (double, double, double — admirably symmetrical), then added a quad 8 at the par-4 10th. Gregg Williams strung three pars together from 9 to 11 — a brief flowering of competence — before a triple-bogey 8 at the par-5 12th cut him back down. Jon Baker, meanwhile, was busy compounding his Spoon claim with a quad 8 at the par-4 13th and a triple 6 at the par-3 17th, all the while looking solid through 14, 15 and 16 in the way a man does when there's nothing left to play for but the cutlery.

## Round 4 — The 16th Bites Back

Which brings us back to the 10th tee.

The 8 at the par-4 10th was Mullin's second quad bogey in two days at Prince's, and unlike the 9 at the previous day's 8th, this one had consequences. Because behind him, Alex Baker was finally putting together the round he had been threatening all week.

He'd opened with three pars worth ten points. A double at the par-4 4th tried to nudge him back into character, but he steadied, and then — picking his moment with the precision of a man who has spotted the leader's wheels coming off — strung 11 points across holes 13 to 15 with three pars in a row. The nine-shot cushion of Friday night was now, suddenly, alarmingly thin. For the first time since the 9th at Deal on day one, Mullin was being asked actual questions.

And then Alex Baker arrived at the 16th.

The same hole — the par-4 16th at Prince's, two days running — where his round-one 10 had been the most expensive single error of the opening day. He proceeded to make another. A sextuple-bogey 10. His second 10 at a 16th in four days, and his very particular gift to David Mullin's afternoon. The door, briefly ajar, was slammed shut by the man trying to walk through it.

The day's late grace note belonged, against all expectation, to Jon Baker, who produced a 14-point sprint from 12 to 15 — birdies at the par-5 12th and par-4 13th, pars at 14 and 15 — and briefly looked like a man playing a different tournament. Then a double 6 at the 16th and a quad 8 at the par-4 18th confirmed what we had all known since the 5th tee on Friday: 39 points, last for the fourth time, Spoon secured.

Mullin signed for 37, and a double.

## How the silverware was distributed

**The Trophy.** Mullin led from the par-5 16th at Royal Cinque Ports onwards and never gave the advantage back — a stewardship of 65 holes. The margins along the way tell the story better than any prose: +3 after R1, +9 after R2, +8 after R3, +2 at the line. The decisive moment was the takeover at Deal; the decisive *survival* was the steadying after the 10th at Prince's on Sunday, helped considerably by Alex Baker's parting gift at the 16th. **154 plays 152.**

**The Green Jacket.** Briefly a contest. Alex Baker drew level for the gross lead at the par-4 6th at Deal in round one; Mullin matched him back at the 9th and was gone. Gross margins thereafter of 5, 12, 12 and 7. Mullin's +68 finished seven clear of Jon Baker, the runner-up on strokes — a small consolation prize at the bottom end of the leaderboard.

**The Wooden Spoon.** Settled in the time it took Jon Baker to walk off the 4th green at Royal Cinque Ports on Friday morning. A bogey at the par-3 4th dropped him last; 68 holes later he was still there. Rounds of 29, 29 and 28 had effectively booked the cutlery before the final day's 39 could mount a defence. **125 all in.**

## The men, in brief

- **David Mullin** — Wire-to-wire double champion. Met two of his own quad bogeys at Prince's with the shrug of a man who had built the cushion precisely so he wouldn't have to care.
- **Alex Baker** — The chief chaser and chief author of his own undoing. Capable of three pars on the spin; equally capable of a 10. The 16th at Prince's now has his name carved into it twice.
- **Jon Baker** — Anchored to the bottom from the 4th hole of the week, but signed off with a flash of what might have been in a R4 birdie burst between 12 and 15. The 8 at the last was, in the end, only punctuation.
- **Gregg Williams** — A quiet mid-pack week, bookended by a 9 at Deal's 3rd and an 8 at Prince's 12th, the latter arriving rudely after three pars in succession.

Kent, second time around, belonged to one man. He did try, on Sunday afternoon, to make a contest of it. To his credit, and rather more to Alex Baker's, he failed.
