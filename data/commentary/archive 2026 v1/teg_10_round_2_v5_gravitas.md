## Palmares Personal Bests, But Mullin Still in Front

The Algarve for a fourth time, but Palmares for the first — Robert Trent Jones Jr.'s splice of the parkland Alvor and the seaside Praia laid out for inspection — and the paradox of the day arrived before anyone had reached the clubhouse. Two personal bests had been posted behind David Mullin. The chase pack had, by any reasonable measurement, played the golf of their lives. And Mullin still walked off the 18th in front of both competitions. The field came at him; the field bounced off.

It is worth dwelling on how he did it, because nothing about Mullin's 42 points was theatrical. The spine of the round was a five-hole par streak from the 4th through the 8th — the par-5 4th, the par-3 5th, the par-4 6th, the par-4 7th, the par-3 8th, fifteen Stableford points accrued without so much as a raised eyebrow. The metronome only stopped at the par-5 9th, where a double-bogey seven punctuated the sequence. Gross +15, 42 points, both leads intact. A round that wins nothing in the heart and everything on the card.

The headline act was **Gregg Williams**, and the headline was a 48-point personal best, gross +14, the day's standout effort by a clear margin. The engine room ran from the 8th to the 12th: par at the par-3 8th, par at the par-5 9th, birdie at the par-4 10th, par at the par-3 11th, bogey at the par-4 12th — eighteen points in five holes, the kind of stretch most TEG cards never see. He then refused to let the closing holes undo him, gathering twelve points across the last four — par, par, bogey at the par-5 17th, bogey at the par-4 18th — and the score, when it was finally tallied, was the best of his TEG life. It hauled him level with Alex Baker on 86 and put him squarely in the Trophy conversation.

**Alex Baker** will, in calmer moments, recognise that he also produced a career round: 45 Stableford points, the finest he has ever signed for, gross +23. He opened bogey-bogey-par for nine, added another ten across the 5th to 7th (bogey, bogey, par), and looked set fair. Then the middle section bared its teeth: double bogeys in succession at the par-4 12th, the par-5 13th and the par-4 14th, six shots gone in three holes. That he still got home in 45 says something about the rest of the card. That he still trails Mullin by five says something about the size of the hill.

**Jon Baker** played, by the only metric the Green Jacket cares about, the finest golf of the afternoon: gross +10, also worth 45 points. He banked ten across the first three (par, birdie at the par-5 2nd, par), absorbed a double-bogey seven at the par-5 4th without visible distress, and then assembled the afternoon's quiet masterpiece — six consecutive pars from the 5th through the 10th, eighteen unflashy points, the par-3 5th, the par-4 6th, the par-4 7th, the par-3 8th, the par-5 9th and the par-4 10th. He stands 82 on Stableford and remains the only credible threat to Mullin's gross lead.

Then there is **Stuart Neumann**, and the kindest way to describe Stuart's showing is as two distinct rounds bolted clumsily together. Around the turn he banked fourteen points in four holes — par at the par-5 9th, bogey at the par-4 10th, par at the par-3 11th, par at the par-4 12th — pleasant, ambling, the golf of a man on holiday. The next three holes cost him thirteen shots. A quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 13th. A quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 14th. A triple-bogey 7 at the par-4 15th. Three holes, zero points, the round in flames. He signed off with a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 18th for good measure, and somehow — somehow — still totted up 37 points, which tells you how good the other bits were.

**John Patterson**, by contrast, had his disaster early and was made to live with it. The very opening hole, the par-4 1st, yielded a sextuple-bogey 10 — six shots gone before anyone had really settled into the day, before the coffee had quite worn off. There is no recovering from that kind of start, and Patterson did not. He finished on 35 points, the lowest return of the round, and watched the Wooden Spoon deficit stretch beneath him.

The Trophy gap closed but did not break: Mullin's 42 was beaten by both Alex Baker (45) and Williams (48), and the lead came in from eight to five, with Williams now level with Alex Baker on 86 and Jon Baker four further back on 82 — a three-man chase where there was barely one before. The Green Jacket margin barely budged, fourteen becoming thirteen, Jon Baker confirmed as the only realistic pursuer. And at the bottom, the Spoon race moved the other way: Patterson's 35 against Neumann's 37 stretched the deficit from three to five, despite Neumann's two double-figure holes — the sort of accountancy only TEG produces.
