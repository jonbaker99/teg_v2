# Mullin's Kent Coronation

*TEG returns to Kent, November 2021 — four rounds, three competitions, one procession with a late wobble.*

## Prologue: A Ten at the Sixteenth

Picture Alex Baker on the 16th tee at Prince's, late on Sunday afternoon. He has just played the previous three holes in level par for eleven Stableford points. David Mullin's eight-point Trophy cushion — a thing that had felt, for three and a half rounds, less like a lead and more like a geographical fact — has been trimmed to something distinctly nervy. The Shore/Dunes is doing its work. The wind is up. The chase is real.

Baker then makes 10 on the par four. A sextuple bogey. No points. The card is wiped, the contest with it.

It is a moment that requires several layers of context to appreciate properly, not least because it isn't even the first 10 Baker has signed for at a 16th this week. To understand how Mullin had built that cushion in the first place — and quite how Alex Baker came to be the man trying to chip it down — we need to rewind four days, head up the coast, and start at Deal.

## Round 1 — Mullin Plants the Flag at Deal

Royal Cinque Ports: a championship links of genuine Open pedigree, and a stern way to open a tournament. Mullin posted 40 Stableford points on it, which tells you most of what you need to know.

The lead changes that mattered — and on a links of this character there are always positional shifts early, while everyone is finding their feet on the bumps — both arrived on the back nine. The Trophy first: Mullin drew level at the 11th (b41) in the middle of a thirteen-point burst across holes 9 to 12, par–birdie–par–par, the sort of stretch that announces intent. Then at the par-five 16th he took outright lead (b38). It would prove the only lead change in the Trophy race that ever mattered. The Jacket followed a similar shape: Alex Baker had nudged briefly level at the 6th (b42); Mullin drew level at the 9th (b40) and was gone.

By that 16th tee, Alex Baker's round was already in some bother. Three successive doubles at 3-5 had bled six shots, a quadruple-bogey 8 at the 11th had bled four more, and then at the par-five 16th — yes, hold that hole number in your mind for later — he wrote down a 10 for no points (b04). Gregg Williams, meanwhile, had managed a 9 at the par-five 3rd (b15), the sort of opening that tends to define a round's ceiling. And Jon Baker, with a bogey at the par-three 4th, dropped to last in the Spoon race (b31). The next 68 holes would not move him from it.

After Day One: Mullin led the Trophy by 3, the Jacket by 5. The flag was planted.

## Round 2 — Littlestone Becomes Mullin Country

To Littlestone, on the remote, wind-scoured Romney Marshes — laid out by Purves and refined later by Braid and MacKenzie, which is the kind of CV that earns respect. Mullin shot 41 points, his best of the week. By dusk, his Trophy advantage had stretched to 9 and his Jacket margin to 12. The race for both, in any honest accounting, was already done.

Alex Baker did the hard work of making it so. He shipped twelve shots across the 2-5 stretch, including a quad 9 at the par-five 5th (b10), and then added another quadruple at the 8th (b11). Mullin was not flawless — there was a triple-then-double patch at 4-6, mopped up by a birdie at the par-five 7th (b20); and another rough sequence at 15-17, closed out by a birdie at the par-five 18th (b21). Twice he wobbled. Twice he reached for a birdie at a reachable par five and steadied the ship. This is what winning rounds look like.

## Round 3 — Prince's, Princely Lead

Onwards to Prince's Shore/Dunes for the weekend double-header — the routing widely regarded as the club's strongest, and a course TEG has now visited more than any other.

Mullin shot 36. The advantage held at 8. Within the round, though, there was a small confession: a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-five 8th (b07), the kind of hole that, in a different week against a different field, might have signalled the wheels coming off. Instead he was simply far better on the back nine — a ten-point split front-to-back (b18), capped by a thirteen-point flourish across the closing four (b17). The champion, leading.

Around him, others were doing the things that explained why he was leading. Alex Baker doubled the opening three for another six dropped, then ran up a quad 8 at the 10th (b12). Jon Baker, by now wearing the Spoon like an old coat, took a quad 8 at the 13th (b13). Gregg Williams briefly threatened to do something useful — nine points across 9-11 — before triple-bogeying the par-five 12th (b28) and remembering himself.

## Round 4 — A Ten at the Sixteenth

Which brings us back where we began.

Mullin began eight clear and, for a long while, looked like a man who would simply run the clock down. Then came his quadruple bogey 8 at the par-four 10th (b08) — and at the very moment the cushion needed replenishing, Alex Baker pressed. He had already opened with ten points across the first three holes (b22). Now he tacked on eleven points across 13-15 (b19): a level-par stretch on a course in November that, in context, represented the round of someone who suddenly believed.

He arrived at the 16th tee within range. Then he made 10.

That, really, is the report. The pattern — quads at the 11th in R1, the 5th and 8th in R2, the 10th in R3, and now the showpiece at the 16th — had finally caught up with him at the worst possible moment. There is a neatness to Baker bookending his tournament with 10s at the 16th holes of two different courses (Royal Cinque Ports on Thursday, Prince's on Sunday — different holes, different layouts, identical scribbled number on the card).

Jon Baker, of all people, had the round's other narrative arc: a fourteen-point burst across 12-15 — birdie, birdie, par, par (b06) — on his way to 39 points, comfortably his best of the week. But there was a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-four 18th (b14) waiting at the end of it; the final shot of his tournament, fittingly, was a sort of last rite. Mullin signed for 37. Trophy by 2.

## How the silverware was settled

**The Trophy (Stableford).** Mullin led after every round — by 3, 9, 8, 2 — which is the shape of a wire-to-wire victory that flatters to deceive only at the end. The decisive act was the outright takeover at Royal Cinque Ports' 16th in Round 1 (b38). Everything after that was defence: the procession through Littlestone, the back-nine reassertion at Prince's on Saturday, and on Sunday a genuine working margin when his own quad at the 10th and Baker's hot stretches (b22, b19) made it briefly interesting. Baker's 10 at the 16th (b02) ended it. Margin: two points.

**The Green Jacket (Gross).** Effectively over by Saturday night. Rounds of 15, 14, 20 and 19 — a total of +68 — won Mullin the Jacket by seven from, of all people, Jon Baker on +75 (b03). The advantage reached 12 after R2 and never came under credible threat.

**The Wooden Spoon.** Decided on Thursday, fourth hole. Jon Baker bogeyed the par-three 4th at Deal, dropped to last (b31), and stayed there for 68 holes. Stableford rounds of 29, 29, 28 told one story; the 39 in Round 4 told another — but 125 points total (b05) and a closing quad at the 18th (b14) confirmed which story the record book would carry.

## The men, in brief

- **David Mullin** — Wire-to-wire victor of both Trophy and Jacket; took outright Trophy lead at Deal's 16th in Round 1 and never surrendered it. Weathered his own quads at the par-five 8th (R3) and par-four 10th (R4) with the equanimity of a man who knew the cushion would hold. Two trophies, no panic.
- **Alex Baker** — The nearly-man. 10s at the 16th in both Round 1 and Round 4, quads at the 11th, 5th, 8th and 10th, and yet a closing round capable of clawing the deficit down to a working margin before the decisive breakdown. Runner-up for the Trophy and the most compelling supporting actor of the week.
- **Jon Baker** — A scorecard at war with itself: Wooden Spoon from Thursday lunchtime to Sunday teatime, and yet runner-up for the Green Jacket. A fourteen-point burst at 12-15 in R4 and a 39-point closing effort couldn't quite outweigh the openers of 29, 29, 28 or the quads at the 13th (R3) and 18th (R4). A unique tournament.
- **Gregg Williams** — Mid-pack throughout, in the way only Gregg can be. A 9 at the par-five 3rd at Deal capped the week's ceiling early; a promising nine-point stretch across 9-11 at Prince's was instantly undone by a triple at the par-five 12th. Steady at being neither.
