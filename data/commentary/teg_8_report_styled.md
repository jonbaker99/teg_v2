There are tournaments where the destiny of the silverware turns on a chip-in at the 71st hole, a missed three-footer in the dusk, a sudden change of weather. And then there is TEG's second pilgrimage to the Lisbon Coast, where the Trophy was effectively over by Sunday afternoon and the only competition with a heartbeat had its mild fibrillation corrected by the second hole of the final round.

The Estoril coast served up a handsome architectural sampler — Robert Trent Jones Sr.'s parkland at Quinta da Marinha, his son's mountain layout in the Sintra hills at Penha Longa, a return to Quinta for the third act, and Arthur Hills's Atlantic links at Oitavos Dunes to close. Three new courses for TEG, one repeat. Pine forests, ocean views, the lot. And, threading through all of it, Gregg Williams marching at the front of the Stableford parade with the unhurried air of a man who knew, somewhere around the 10th green at Quinta on day one, that this was already done.

## The kill shot, delivered in pars

Begin at the end. Oitavos Dunes, Round 4. Gregg Williams stands on the par-3 12th with a Trophy lead that has, depending on which round you snapshot, been four, eleven, five — and now needs only defending for an hour or so against an Atlantic breeze and his own capacity for self-sabotage. What follows is the least dramatic championship-clinching sequence imaginable: par at the 12th, bogey at the par-5 13th, par at the par-3 14th, par at the par-3 15th. Twelve Stableford points in four holes. The Trophy, formally, sealed.

The man had, by then, been leading for three and a half rounds. To understand why nobody ever quite laid a glove on him, we need to rewind to a Tuesday afternoon at Quinta da Marinha.

## Round 1 — Williams Plants the Flag at Quinta {.round1 .round}

Trent Jones Sr.'s parkland was new ground for TEG, and the field treated it accordingly: some politely, some less so. Gregg, after pottering around for seven holes, found a gear. Across holes 8–11 he assembled the burst that would define the week — bogey at the par-4 8th, par at the par-3 9th, birdie at the par-5 10th, bogey at the par-5 11th. Fourteen Stableford points in four holes. The birdie at the 10th nudged him into outright lead, and that is the last sentence of this report in which the phrase "outright Trophy lead" will change hands.

He finished on 38, four clear, having had the cheek to drop an 8 at the par-4 15th along the way. David Mullin's +20 took the Gross by a single shot. Up at the sharp end, the day was orderly.

Down at the other end, it was something else. Stuart Neumann reached the 7th tee a member of polite society and emerged from the 15th green having dropped twenty-nine shots across nine holes — double, double, triple at 7–9; then a quadruple at the par-5 10th, a quadruple at the par-5 11th, a quintuple at the par-4 12th; a double at 13, a quintuple at the par-3 14th, a double at 15th. He signed for 17 points and had, in the bargain, been bottom of the Stableford from the 4th hole onwards. Templates were being set in both directions.

Elsewhere: Alex Baker began with five straight bogeys at 1–5 for 15 — the first instalment of a recurring relationship with catastrophe that would not, in the end, prevent him finishing second. Jon Baker pencilled in a 9 at the par-4 13th.

## Round 2 — Penha Longa, Lead Longer {.round2 .round}

Up into the Sintra hills, onto Trent Jones Jr.'s mountain layout, and the question was whether anyone in the chasing pack fancied making a fight of it. They did not. Gregg shot 41, the best round of the day, and walked off with an eleven-point cushion. The remarkable thing is that his card included a 10 at the par-5 12th — a quintuple bogey of the sort that, for anyone else, might have been the round's defining moment. For Gregg it was a footnote, absorbed and forgotten by the time he reached the clubhouse.

Mullin posted +14 to push his Gross advantage out to six. Henry Meller contributed a 9 at the par-4 16th, Alex Baker an 8 at the same hole; Neumann, gamely battling, found 31 points and was *still* bottom, having decorated the back nine with quadruples at the par-4 10th and par-5 12th and a 9 at the par-4 16th. (The 16th, you sense, was where Penha Longa kept its teeth.)

By Wednesday night the Trophy looked like a procession and the Jacket appeared secure. The Spoon, it scarcely needed saying, had its occupant.

## Round 3 — Return to Quinta, Return of the Wobble {.round3 .round}

This was the round in which the tournament briefly remembered it was supposed to be a competition.

David Mullin had been imperious for two days. On TEG's second crack at Quinta da Marinha he was nothing of the sort. A 26-point, +26 round — comfortably his worst of the week — included a quadruple at the par-4 12th, and Jon Baker, who had been stalking patiently, drew level on the Gross at that same 12th hole and then nudged into outright lead at the 15th. By the end of the day Baker had a two-shot Jacket advantage and the only genuine pulse of the week was beating.

What it was *not* doing, however, was threatening the Trophy. Gregg had his own struggles going — 29 points, the worst of his week, with quadruples at the par-5 10th and the par-4 13th — and yet still walked off five clear. When the leader has his off-day and his nearest pursuer cannot eat into the margin, the tournament is telling you something.

Down at the bottom, Neumann was once again writing a stretch of golf you would not wish on your worst playing partner. The opening five holes at Quinta cost him seventeen shots: quintuple at the par-5 1st, double at the par-3 2nd, double at the par-5 3rd, quintuple at the par-4 4th, triple at the par-3 5th. Later he found an 11 — a sextuple — at the par-5 10th. He signed for 20 and remained, as he had been since the 4th hole of the week, sixth. Alex Baker, for company, dropped a 10 at the par-4 8th.

## Round 4 — Mullin Reclaims at the 2nd, Williams Seals at the 12th {.round4 .round}

Arthur Hills's Atlantic links is Portugal's premier course and, on the evidence of this Sunday, a place that sorts the unsettled from the unbothered with brisk efficiency.

For Henry Meller it was the unsettled. Across the opening six holes he shed twenty-two shots: double at the par-4 1st, quadruple at the par-4 2nd, triple at the par-3 3rd, a sextuple 10 at the par-4 4th, quintuple at the par-4 5th, double at the par-4 6th. By the time he reached the 7th tee the day was lost; what remained was scenery.

For Mullin, by contrast, links air evidently agreed. The Jacket advantage, lost only the previous afternoon, was reclaimed at the second hole he played on Sunday — a bogey at the par-4 2nd was enough to nose ahead of Jon Baker, and from there a +17 closing round carried him home by six at +77. Order, in other words, was restored more or less at the first opportunity Mullin was given to restore it. The wobble had lasted exactly one round.

And up ahead of him, Gregg — back to his Penha Longa tempo at 41 points — sealed the Trophy with that procession of pars from the 12th. Twelve clear of Alex Baker on 149 to 137; the margin not so much defended as confirmed.

Neumann's 32 at Oitavos was, in fact, a perfectly respectable day. It made not a dent. 100 points all in, sixth after every round, a residency rather than a finish.

## How the silverware was settled

**Trophy (Stableford).** Williams led by 4 after Quinta, by 11 after Penha Longa, by 5 after the return visit, by 12 at close — 38, 41, 29, 41 — outright advantage taken at the par-5 10th in Round 1 and never surrendered. Alex Baker, for all his blow-ups, was clear in second.

**Green Jacket (Gross).** Mullin +20, +14, +26, +17 for +77, with Jon Baker briefly in front after the third round before being deposed at Oitavos's 2nd. Won by 6.

**Wooden Spoon.** Neumann, sixth from the 4th hole of the week to the 72nd. The Quinta H7–15 carnage in Round 1 set the tone; the Quinta H1–5 leak in Round 3 underlined it; no closing 32 was ever going to be enough. 100 points.

## The men, in brief

- **Gregg Williams** — Wire-to-wire Trophy winner by twelve. Took the lead at Quinta's 10th in Round 1, kept it through a 10 at Penha Longa's 12th and quadruples at Quinta's 10th and 13th in Round 3, sealed it with four serene holes from Oitavos's 12th. The least flustered champion you could wish to see.
- **David Mullin** — Led the Jacket all week bar one round, surrendered it briefly to Jon Baker after a +26 Sunday at Quinta featuring a quadruple at the 12th, retook outright advantage at Oitavos's 2nd and closed by 6 at +77. The wobble was real; the response was instant.
- **Jon Baker** — Patient stalker, owner of the Jacket lead for precisely one round, runner-up by six. A 9 at Quinta's 13th in Round 1 was his only real blot. So near, and yet, in the end, six away.
- **Alex Baker** — Trophy runner-up despite a highlights reel of horrors: five opening bogeys at Quinta, a 10 at Quinta's 8th in Round 3, an 8 at Penha Longa's 16th, quadruples or worse in every round. Twelve adrift of Gregg and miles ahead of everyone else — a curious sort of consistency.
- **Stuart Neumann** — Sixth after every round, Spoon on 100. The H7–15 stretch at Quinta on day one — twenty-nine shots dropped in nine holes — was the week in miniature; the Round 3 opening five at Quinta its sequel; the 11 at the par-5 10th its punctuation. A closing 32 at Oitavos hinted at what might have been, and arrived a day too late.
- **Henry Meller** — Specialist in the horror opening. A 10 at Quinta's 3rd in Round 1, a 9 at Penha Longa's 16th in Round 2, and the showpiece — twenty-two shots gone across Oitavos's first six holes on Sunday, including a 10 at the par-4 4th. The week's chief supplier of colour, if not of contention.
