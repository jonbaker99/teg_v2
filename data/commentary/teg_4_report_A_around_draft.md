# Mullin's Forest

The 8th green at Bletchingley, mid-afternoon on the final day. Jon Baker, who had spent three and a half rounds doing impressionist work as the second-best player in the field, holes out from somewhere off the dance floor for an eagle two on the par-4. He has, in that moment, the Trophy lead. For the first time since Lingfield he is the man to beat. The standings, briefly, are upended.

He will not lead another hole this week.

What followed across the next eight holes — 27 shots conceded to par, including two separate sextuple bogeys — is one of the more thorough acts of self-immolation TEG has yet witnessed. But it is also the moment that confirmed what the Gross column had been quietly insisting since the 11th tee at Royal Ashdown: this was David Mullin's tournament, and had been for some time.

A word on the geography. TEG had never been to East Sussex before, nor for that matter to Surrey, and the four-course menu was a debut on every card: Royal Ashdown's bunkerless heath, Lingfield's parkland and water, MacKenzie's masterpiece on Crowborough Beacon above the South Downs, and the fast modern parkland at Bletchingley. Five men, four virgin courses, and one quiet coronation.

## Round 1 — A Forest with No Bunkers, and Meller Finds the Only Trouble

Royal Ashdown Forest is famous for not having any bunkers. This did not prevent Henry Meller from finding trouble at the par-3 11th, where he carded a 12 — a nine-over par that one assumes involved heather, more heather, and a brief existential pause. It was the kind of number you don't so much write on a card as confess to.

At the top, Baker was the early Trophy leader, three clear of Mullin. Beneath that surface, however, Mullin had quietly seized the Gross lead at the 11th and would not yield it again for the remaining 63 holes of the tournament. It is the sort of thing that only becomes interesting in retrospect, which is to say: now.

The day's signature horror, though, belonged not to Meller but to Gregg Williams, who played the closing nine in 22 over par. The damage, for the connoisseur: double at the 10th, triple at the 11th, doubles at the 12th and 13th, triple at the 14th, doubles at the 15th and 16th, and triples at the 17th and 18th. A nine-hole stretch with no par or better in it — a sustained, almost ceremonial leak. Williams was not yet at the bottom of the Spoon race. He was, however, beginning to audition.

## Round 2 — Mullin Takes the Trophy Lead at Lingfield

Lingfield Park, with its mature trees and its water, did what parkland with water tends to do. Mullin overhauled Baker on the Trophy and finished the day two clear. His Gross lead stretched to 7. The numbers thicken; the story does not yet. Sometimes a round's job is simply to confirm the direction of travel.

## Round 3 — Mullin's +17 at Crowborough: A Round for the Record Books

High on the heath above the South Downs, on Alister MacKenzie's contribution to the week, David Mullin produced the round of his TEG life and one of the best rounds in TEG history. Seventeen over par gross — at the time, the second-best Gross round ever played at TEG.

The Trophy lead, which had been 2, became 16. The Gross lead, which had been 7, also became 16. Mullin took the Trophy lead outright with a par at the 10th and proceeded methodically through the back nine like a man tidying up after a dinner party. There is, in tournament golf, a particular kind of round that doesn't just lead — it announces that leading is now the natural order of things, and that any challenge to it will be regarded as bad manners. This was that round.

Behind him, the field was reduced to managing its own pulse.

## Round 4 — The Eagle, the Avalanche, and the Coronation

Which brings us, properly, to Bletchingley.

Baker, to his eternal credit, did not arrive at the final round to make up the numbers. At the par-3 2nd he birdied to take the Trophy lead outright. Mullin drew him level at the 3rd. Then came the 8th — the par-4, the holed approach, the eagle two — and Baker was, against all the gathering evidence of the preceding three days, in front.

The next eight holes are best presented in sequence, because their cumulative effect is the story. Double at the par-5 9th. Triple at the par-4 10th. Triple at the par-5 11th. At the par-3 12th, a sextuple bogey 9 — and, on the same green, Mullin's tidy par to reclaim the Trophy lead outright. The decisive moment of the week, delivered as politely as possible. Double at the 13th. Triple at the par-3 14th. At the par-5 15th, a second sextuple — an 11 this time. Double at the 16th. Twenty-seven shots, eight holes. The eagle was 22 holes ago and felt like a different sport.

Mullin walked it home.

Down at the other end, the Spoon was changing hands in a sort of mournful relay. Williams had dropped to last at the par-5 6th with a quadruple bogey 9. Meller obligingly took it back at the par-5 11th with a quintuple 10 — his second 10 of the week, on a different course, both on par-5s if not parallel ones. Then Williams sank to the bottom for good at the par-4 13th with a triple 7, and finished the job with a closing six holes of 16 over par: triple, quadruple, triple, double, double, double. A personal-worst round of +15. A personal-worst gross of 43-over for the day. He could not be caught, even by Meller, who had every reason to want to catch him.

## How It Was Won

**The Trophy** went to Mullin at +9 — the lowest winning Trophy score in TEG history. Margin: 8. He was behind only after Round 1 and during the small handful of holes at Bletchingley when Baker was holing out from the fairway. The rest of the week, he was in front.

**The Green Jacket** was wire-to-wire from the 11th hole of the opening round. Round-end margins of 3, 7, 16, 19 tell the story of a contest that was never quite close enough to be called one. Mullin's +81 finished 19 clear of Stuart Neumann's +100 — both personal bests, and both achieved on courses none of them had ever seen before, which is the sort of detail you might expect to soften the rout. It did not. Personal-best gross scores also for Baker (+113), Meller (+107) and Williams (+152), which is its own quiet commentary on a benign set of courses and a top of the leaderboard operating in a different weather system.

**The Wooden Spoon** went to Williams at +40, despite his never having been bottom of the pile through three rounds (Meller after R1, Neumann after R2, Meller again after R3). His three signature haemorrhages — 22 over the closing nine at Ashdown, 18 over the opening six at Crowborough, 16 over the closing six at Bletchingley — were sufficient, in aggregate, to overhaul a man who had carded a 12 and two 10s. It takes commitment.

## The men, in brief

- **David Mullin** — Behind by 3 after Ashdown, in front from Lingfield onwards. The Trophy at +9 (a TEG record) and the Jacket by 19, with the second-best Gross round in TEG history as the centrepiece. A coronation in everything but the ceremony.
- **Jon Baker** — Led the Trophy after R1, runner-up throughout, briefly retook the lead at Bletchingley's 8th with an eagle and proceeded to give back 27 shots in eight holes. A Gross PB of +113 the consolation; the sextuples at the 12th and 15th the abiding image.
- **Stuart Neumann** — Quiet, consistent, and runner-up for the Jacket at +100. A Gross personal best, never in the Trophy hunt, never in the Spoon conversation. The week's least dramatic story, gracefully told.
- **Henry Meller** — Opened with a 12 at Ashdown's 11th, added a 10 at Bletchingley's 11th, and held last place outright on the final afternoon. A Gross PB of +107 and Williams's late unravelling spared him the Spoon by the narrowest of margins.
- **Gregg Williams** — Bled in stretches: 22, 18, 16. Inherited the Spoon at +40 on the 13th at Bletchingley and never threatened to give it back. Somehow also a Gross PB of +152, which is the kind of statistic that requires the rest of the field to have had a very good week.
