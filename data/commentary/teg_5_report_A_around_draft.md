# TEG 5 — The Mullin Paradox

There is a moment, somewhere in the late morning of the third day at Boavista, when David Mullin stands on the 3rd green knowing two things at once. He is going to win the Green Jacket by a distance that will require fresh adjectives. And he has just, quietly, lost the Trophy.

Gregg Williams has rolled in a par at the par-5 3rd for four Stableford points, and with that small, blameless act has moved into outright lead of the net competition for the first time all week. He will not be displaced. The man wearing — figuratively, prematurely, but indisputably — the Green Jacket is about to spend the rest of the week sliding all the way to the bottom of the other leaderboard.

This is the shape of TEG 5. Two competitions, one man at the top of one and the bottom of the other, and a third party walking off with the silverware that actually counts. To understand it, we have to rewind forty-eight hours, to a Tuesday in June at Boavista when David Mullin started rewriting the record book.

## Round 1 — +12, and the Book Reopens

The 8th TEG round at Boavista began like any of the previous seven, and ended unlike any round TEG had previously witnessed. Mullin signed for +12 gross. The best round, gross, in TEG history (b01).

He took outright lead of the Green Jacket at the 3rd and was never headed — a sentence that will, over the next three days, acquire an increasingly load-bearing weight. The net competition, by contrast, behaved the way net competitions tend to behave on a Tuesday: Mullin, Baker and Williams traded the lead in polite increments — Mullin out at 3, Baker level at 4, level again at 16, Mullin retaking outright at the last to finish the day one clear at +1. Williams sat third, five back, the kind of position from which one is supposed to applaud politely and order another beer.

Nothing about the evening suggested that the bunched net field at the top of the board mattered less than the eight-shot gap at the top of the gross one. Nothing yet suggested that those two facts were the entire story of the week.

## Round 2 — Boavista's Day of Records

If Round 1 was a record, Round 2 was a small genre.

Mullin came back out and shot +11 gross, equalling the TEG record he had set the previous afternoon (b02). For good measure he posted a personal best of -4 net (b06). This would, on almost any other day in TEG history, be the headline. On this day it was arguably the third item on the bulletin.

Because Stuart Neumann shot -9 net. The best round in TEG history to date (b03). And, on the same card, +11 gross — tying Mullin's twenty-four-hour-old gross record (b04). Two men holding the gross record jointly by sundown, one of them also holding the best net round ever recorded. Boavista, eighth and ninth TEG visits, was simply handing out plaques.

The quietest record of the three belonged to Williams, who personal-bested at -6 net (b07) — and did so on a card that came in at +27 gross. The handicap engine, humming beautifully in the background, doing the work that would eventually win him the Trophy while nobody was looking. State the implication: the man who would lift the Trophy was, on the day of his personal-best net round, sixteen shots worse on raw strokes than the man who would lift the Jacket. This is not a paradox. It is the system functioning as designed.

Henry Meller, meanwhile, made a 10 at the par-5 16th (b25), which under the circumstances felt almost companionable.

Mullin led the Trophy by three. Williams was second. The Jacket race was, in any meaningful sense, over.

## Round 3 — Williams Walks Through the Door

We return to where we began. The 10th TEG round at Boavista. The 3rd green. Williams' par, four Stableford points, outright lead (b21). It is the only lead change in the Trophy race that will matter.

What made the door open was Mullin walking through it backwards. His net game, which had spent two days quietly being good while his gross game was being historic, came apart entirely. He posted +12 net — his worst TEG round to date (b20) — on a gross card of +27. The wheels did not so much come off as politely detach themselves and roll into the Atlantic.

By close of play Williams led the Trophy by five. Mullin had fallen to fourth in the net standings. The Green Jacket lead, untouched by any of this, held at fourteen, because of course it did — Mullin's +27 gross was, by the standards of his own week, a bad day, and by the standards of everyone else's a perfectly defensible afternoon.

Two competitions, two directions, one man.

## Round 4 — Palmares Holds, Williams Wins, Mullin Spoons

Round 4 took everyone to Palmares — a Robert Trent Jones Jr. design new to TEG, blending parkland with genuine links, a change of surface for the closing act. It mattered.

Williams' overnight five-shot cushion looked, for a stretch on the back nine, like the kind of cushion that gets thrown out of an upstairs window. He bled ten shots across four holes: triple at the par-4 10th, double at the par-5 11th, double at the par-3 12th, triple at the par-5 13th (b46). Then, just to keep things interesting, a 9 at the par-4 16th (b13). Most overnight leaders, given a links debut and that particular run of numbers, would be shaking hands with the runner-up by the 17th tee.

Williams held. He won the Trophy by two from Baker at -6 (b05). His first.

Mullin completed his Jacket procession with +22 gross — a final margin of twelve over Baker at +72 (b11). The lead by round, for the record-keepers: eight, fourteen, fourteen, twelve. One lead change all week, at the 3rd hole of the first morning, and then nothing.

And then there was the Spoon. Mullin had begun the day fourth on net, already a striking position for a man about to be fitted for a Green Jacket. At the par-5 6th he made a quadruple-bogey 9 (b22, b30) and slid to the bottom. Meller — who had been propping up the table for two rounds — looked like he might be granted a reprieve when he doubled the par-4 15th (b23) and briefly took the wooden one back. Mullin parred the par-3 17th (b24). That was that. Bottom for good at +12 net (b19).

The same man wearing the Jacket carrying the Spoon home. The dry facts: Jacket by twelve, Spoon by one.

The closing day's other business was tidier. Baker posted a personal-best +18 gross at Palmares (b08), the runner-up's runner-up reward. Meller, who had spent the week in the basement, signed off with a personal-best -3 net and a personal-best +17 gross (b09, b10) — the links, apparently, suiting him in a way that the parkland never had.

## How the three were decided

**The Trophy.** Mullin led after R1 and stretched it to three after R2. Williams' -6 net personal best in R2 (b07) parked him second. He took outright lead at R3's 3rd (b21), got the five-shot overnight cushion when Mullin posted +12 (b20), and survived the Palmares wobble (b46, b13) to win by two (b05).

**The Green Jacket.** Mullin, hole 3 of R1 onwards. Wire-to-wire. Twelve clear (b11). Records at b01, b02, b04.

**The Wooden Spoon.** Mullin, via his R3 +12 (b20), the quad at Palmares' 6th (b22) and the par at the 17th (b24, b19). Net leader through 36, last through 72.

## The men, in brief

- **Gregg Williams** — A maiden Trophy, assembled so quietly that one wonders if he meant to. The R2 personal-best -6 net closed the gap; the par at R3's 3rd opened the door; the Palmares back nine, ten shots gone in four holes and a 9 at the 16th, was the bit where he held his nerve when there was no obvious reason to.
- **David Mullin** — Two of the greatest gross rounds in TEG history, back to back at Boavista, and a Green Jacket won by twelve. Also: his worst-ever TEG net round in between, a quadruple at Palmares' 6th, and a Spoon to carry home alongside the Jacket. A week that will require its own shelf.
- **Jon Baker** — Runner-up in both Trophy and Jacket, which is the kind of double that earns sympathy rather than headlines. Personal-best +18 gross at Palmares (b08) was the highlight of a week spent two paces behind two different men.
- **Stuart Neumann** — Posted -9 net in R2, the best round in TEG history (b03), and tied the gross record at +11 the same afternoon (b04). Did not seriously threaten the lead of either competition. A reminder that records and trophies are not, in TEG, the same currency.
- **Henry Meller** — A 10 at Boavista's 16th in R2 (b25), large stretches of the week at the bottom of the net table, and then a Palmares farewell of -3 net and +17 gross (b09, b10), both personal bests. The links, in the end, were kinder than the parkland.
