# Dunes, Doom and Coronation: TEG 8 Decided at Oitavos {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 8 | Round 4 | 28/11/2015 | Oitavos Dunes</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> GW 41 | AB 39 | DM 34 | SN 32 | JB 28 | HM 21</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> DM +17 | GW +22 | SN +24 | JB +25 | AB +33 | HM +40</p>

So here we were, at last, on the Atlantic. Oitavos Dunes — Arthur Hills's links at Cascais, TEG's first visit, the tour's second crack at the Lisbon Coast — and you could not have asked for a more honest closing stage. Wind, dunes, a scorecard with room for some genuinely outrageous numbers, and at the end of the afternoon two trophies handed out: one held, one taken. Gregg Williams kept what he came with. David Mullin left with Jon Baker's. And down the bottom, Stuart Neumann did the necessary admin to confirm what most of us had suspected since about Tuesday.

It is worth saying up front that none of this was particularly *serene*. The two men who walked off as champions both had moments on the back of the card that ought, in a kinder competition, to have cost them dearly. They didn't. That is what makes it a story.

**Gregg Williams** arrived five clear in the Trophy and left twelve ahead, which sounds like a procession and absolutely wasn't. The procession bit got interrupted at the par-4 5th, where a quadruple-bogey 8 opened a trapdoor that didn't fully close until the 9th. Across holes 5 to 8 he bled eleven shots to par — quad, double, double, triple — the kind of stretch where you start glancing back over your shoulder to see who's coming. Mercifully for Gregg, on this particular afternoon nobody was. He gathered himself and then, in the most Gregg-Williams way imaginable, simply put the Trophy to bed with a four-hole burst across 12 to 15: par at the par-3 12th, bogey at the par-5 13th, par at the par-3 14th, par at the par-3 15th. Twelve Stableford points in four holes. Thank you, goodnight, and the silver stays where it is.

**David Mullin**, meanwhile, had spent the first three rounds two gross shots adrift of Jon Baker and clearly decided that today was the moment. The Jacket changed hands at the par-4 2nd — a bogey 5 from Mullin, and the lead he had spent three rounds chasing was suddenly his to defend. Which he then had to do for sixteen more holes, including one in particular that very nearly handed it all back. The par-4 11th. An 8. A quadruple bogey, in other words, in the middle of a gross shootout he was leading by the thinnest of margins, on a links he'd never seen before. This was the wobble. This was the moment Jon, somewhere on the course, could reasonably have allowed himself a small hopeful exhalation. Mullin's response was immediate and emphatic: a birdie 4 at the par-5 13th to stop the bleeding, then — after bogeys at 14 and 15 threatened to start it up again — a second birdie at the par-5 16th to arrest it once more. Six clear at the close. The best Stableford return of the afternoon, the Green Jacket, and the considerable satisfaction of having grabbed it on hole two and refused to let go.

**Jon Baker** is the man in the unlucky chair here. Overnight Jacket leader, the lead surrendered before he'd properly warmed up, and despite a perfectly respectable round he simply could not get back at Mullin. Worse, the afternoon cost him on two fronts: second on Gross by six, and overtaken on the Trophy too, slipping to third behind his brother. Sometimes the round just goes like that.

**Alex Baker** is the brother in question, and his climb to Trophy runner-up came with the kind of scorecard that tells you everything about Oitavos in a breeze. A quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 15th. A closing quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 18th — a genuine final-hole horror — and yet, when the dust settled, neither blemish had altered the colour of his medal. Silver. He'd done enough elsewhere, and Jon had not done quite enough.

Which brings us, with some trepidation, to **Henry Meller**. There is no gentle way to write this up. Across the first six holes Henry shed twenty-two shots to par. Twenty-two. The damage: a double at the 1st, a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 2nd, a triple at the par-3 3rd, a sextuple-bogey *10* at the par-4 4th, a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 5th, and a double at the 6th to round off the carnage. Six holes, twenty-two over. He then mislaid a further six across 11–13 — three double bogeys in a tidy little row — and closed with another nine-shot collapse at 16, 17 and 18, the 18th being a quadruple-bogey 8. It was the round of the week, in the worst possible sense, and the only silver lining is that it briefly flickered hope at the foot of the leaderboard for about forty minutes.

That hope, of course, belonged to **Stuart Neumann**, who had begun the morning twenty-two shots clear at the foot of the Trophy table and could in theory have been caught if Henry produced something miraculous and Stuart produced something dire. Henry obliged on his side of the bargain; Stuart, with quadruple-bogey 8s at the par-4 5th and the par-4 17th, more or less obliged on his. The cushion shrank to eleven, which is the kind of margin that looks tight on paper and isn't tight at all in practice. The Spoon was always going to Stuart. It went to Stuart.

The shape of the afternoon, then: the Trophy gap stretched from five to twelve as Williams steadied himself and Alex Baker climbed past Jon for second; the Green Jacket changed hands at the 2nd hole and stayed changed, a two-shot deficit overturned into a six-shot victory; and the Wooden Spoon was formally collected, the margin slimming from 22 to 11 only because Meller's round was the more calamitous of the two.
