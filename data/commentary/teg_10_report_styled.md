Look at the final scoreboard and the symmetry does the work for you. At the top, Alex Baker — runner-up in TEG 8, runner-up in TEG 9, and a man for whom the silver salver had begun to feel like a personal effect. At the bottom, John Patterson, who twelve months earlier had been the Trophy champion and now found himself contemplating a Wooden Spoon. One man's breakthrough, another's downfall; one promotion, one demotion, the Algarve sun shining impartially on both.

In between, David Mullin won the Green Jacket so completely it almost felt rude, and somehow still lost the Trophy by 11.

This is the story of how that inversion was built.

## The shape of the week

Four rounds, two courses, one of them effectively a private fiefdom. Boavista bookended the trip — the 11th and 12th TEG visits to a venue where Mullin, Jon Baker, Williams and Neumann are now into double figures apiece — with two days of fresh ground at Palmares in between, Praia/Alvor on the Sunday and Alvor/Lagos on the Monday. The new course did what new courses do: it produced personal bests for those who took to it and 10s for those who didn't.

The records, though, all came at Boavista. They tend to.

## How the Jacket was won, before lunch

The Green Jacket was decided, in any meaningful sense, on the opening Saturday. David Mullin arrived in the Algarve on a run of four straight Jackets and on what amounts to his home course, and proceeded to remove any remaining suspense with a 75 gross — +4 at Boavista, a new course record by seven shots across 50 prior visits, and the best gross round in TEG history. The previous Boavista mark had been 82. Mullin took seven shots off it on a Saturday morning.

Alongside the 75 came 49 Stableford points, at the time the second-best round TEG had ever seen. By the close of day one he led the Jacket by 14 and the Trophy by 8. The leads by round thereafter ran 14, 13, 10, 12. He never relinquished the front, never looked like relinquishing it, and finished at +51, twelve clear of Jon Baker — who, with grim consistency, took up the runner-up position he has now occupied in three of the last four Jackets, and did so via a personal-best gross total of +63.

An eighth Jacket, then, secured roughly by elevenses on day one.

## How the Trophy was won, slowly

The other story took longer.

Through Saturday and into Sunday, Mullin's Stableford advantage looked unassailable in the way most early leads do. Eight points after R1, five after R2. The chasers were pursuing a man who does not, historically, fold.

Alex Baker opened with 41 points on the record-breaking Saturday and was already second, eight back. On the Sunday at Palmares he posted a personal-best 45 — and a personal-best +23 gross alongside it — and trimmed the deficit to five. Gregg Williams, on the same fresh ground, played the round of his TEG life: 48 Stableford, +14 gross, both personal bests. Jon Baker, not to be left out of the new-course party, shot +10 gross — the third-best gross round in TEG history.

The Sunday at Palmares was, by some distance, the most generous afternoon TEG has put on in years. Three personal-best Stableford rounds. The third-best gross round ever recorded. Also a sextuple-bogey 10 at the 1st from John Patterson, and another 10 at the 18th from Stuart Neumann, but we'll get to them.

Monday, Alvor/Lagos, more new ground. Alex Baker posted a third consecutive personal best: 47 points, +22 gross. And on the 6th hole of that round, with no fanfare and no obvious tilt of the earth's axis, he took outright lead of the Trophy for the first time. The two-time bridesmaid was, briefly, the bride.

Williams was not done. On the par-5 13th he made a bogey 6 and that was enough — the lead changed hands again. For two holes Williams held the advantage, and Baker was the chaser. Then Baker reclaimed it on the 15th and pushed on, and by the time the cards were signed the gap was 11 points.

A margin he then proceeded to match exactly on the Tuesday — 11 points, to the point — by means of the highest-scoring round in TEG history.

## Tuesday at Boavista: fifty-one

Fifty-one Stableford points. Read it again. The previous best was the 49 Mullin had posted on the opening morning, sitting fresh in the record book and looking durable for at least, you'd have thought, longer than 72 hours.

The round's foundation was a six-hole stretch from the 5th to the 10th, in which Alex Baker went bogey, par, par, bogey, par, par — three pars and three bogeys, +2 across the run, 21 Stableford points harvested. The bogey on the 8th is worth pausing on: a par-5, stroke index 2, one of the hardest holes on the course, and a hole on which, on the same Tuesday afternoon, John Patterson made an 11. The par on the 9th was a par-4 at stroke index 18 — the easiest hole — and is the only entirely ordinary thing in the sequence.

Personal-best gross total too, at +90. A first Trophy. After two runner-up finishes, after the suspicion that he might simply be one of those people for whom golf tournaments are something that happens to other people on the final afternoon, Alex Baker won the Trophy by 11 with the best round anyone has ever played on a TEG.

The nearly-man, having been nearly twice, was finally.

## How the Spoon was lost

John Patterson arrived in the Algarve as the holder. Twelve months earlier he had lifted the Trophy. He had never finished bottom of a TEG. By the 4th hole of the Saturday at Boavista, he was last, and by the time he signed for his closing 21 on the Tuesday — the worst round he has put his name to — he had occupied the bottom position at the close of all four rounds. Bottom from R1 H4, bottom forever after.

The damage came in identifiable lumps. A quintuple-bogey 10 on the 8th at Boavista on Saturday. A sextuple-bogey 10 on the opening hole at Palmares on Sunday. Another quintuple at the Boavista 8th — sorry, the Palmares-Alvor 8th — on Monday, and then the Tuesday's sextuple 11 at the Boavista 8th, a par-5 that finally finished him having been responsible for three blow-ups across four rounds.

The Monday produced something even worse than any of those individual numbers. Across holes 11 to 15 at Alvor/Lagos, Patterson went triple, quintuple, triple, quadruple, quadruple — 19 shots dropped in five holes. The quintuple came at the 12th, a par-3 of stroke index 18, the easiest hole on the course. Around all of this the Stableford columns ran 29, 35, 27, 21.

Final total: 112. The Spoon, inherited from the man who had been holding the Trophy.

## The Neumann question

It is worth recording, briefly, that Stuart Neumann arrived in the Algarve holding back-to-back Spoons and looking the obvious favourite for a third. He escaped only because Patterson collapsed harder, and he did not exactly escape cleanly. A sextuple-bogey 10 at the 18th on Saturday. A sextuple-bogey 10 at the 18th on Sunday — consecutive 10s at consecutive closing holes, on different courses, a kind of grim symmetry of his own. A quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 13th in the same Sunday round. And across holes 13 to 15 that afternoon, scores of 10, 9, 7 against pars of 5, 4, 4 — 13 shots dropped in three holes.

The Spoon went elsewhere only because elsewhere went harder.

## The men, in brief

- **Alex Baker** — Bridesmaid in TEG 8, bridesmaid in TEG 9, champion in TEG 10. Rounds of 41, 45, 47, 51 — three consecutive personal-best Stableford rounds, and then the highest-scoring round in TEG history to close. Personal-best gross totals at +90. Took outright Trophy lead on the 6th hole of Monday and was never headed across the 30 holes that followed.
- **David Mullin** — An eighth Jacket, won by 12, led wire-to-wire. The R1 75 was a new Boavista course record by seven shots over 50 prior visits and the best gross round TEG has ever recorded. He posted the second-best Stableford round in TEG history on the same afternoon and still lost the Trophy by 11, which is what handicaps are for and is in no sense a paradox.
- **Gregg Williams** — Trophy runner-up, the closest he has come without winning. Personal-best Stableford 48 and personal-best +14 gross on the Sunday at Palmares, a personal-best gross total of +76 across the week, and a two-hole spell on Monday afternoon as the outright Trophy leader. A near-miss, but the most authoritative near-miss of his career.
- **Jon Baker** — Jacket runner-up again, for the third time in four TEGs, with a personal-best gross total of +63 and the third-best gross round in TEG history (+10) on the Sunday. Bridesmaid is becoming a difficult term to use in this account.
- **John Patterson** — From Trophy holder to Spoon in twelve months. Bottom from the 4th hole of the opening morning and bottom at every subsequent count. Four blow-ups of quintuple bogey or worse, the 19-shot stretch across the Monday's 11th to 15th, and a closing 21 — his worst round on record.
- **Stuart Neumann** — Escaped a third straight Spoon by the thinnest of margins and the unhappiest of routes. Two 10s at two different closing holes, a third 10 at a par-5 13th, and 13 shots gone in three holes on the Sunday. A reprieve, of sorts.
