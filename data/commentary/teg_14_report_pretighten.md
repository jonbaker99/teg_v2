# Mullin's Reversal

There is a hole at Prince's, sixteenth on the card, that will be remembered with a small private shudder by Alex Baker for some time. Late on Sunday afternoon he stood on its tee two shots off David Mullin's Trophy lead, having spent the previous three holes — par, par, par — reducing what had been an eight-point cushion to something that could plausibly be called a contest. He walked off it having taken 10. The chase, which had been the only live story on the leaderboard for ninety minutes, was over in the time it took a ball to find whatever it found.

The detail that elevates this from a private misfortune to something approaching design is that the same player had taken a 10 on the 16th on Thursday at Royal Cinque Ports, twenty miles up the coast — the very hole, as it happened, at which David Mullin had assumed outright lead of the Trophy he would not relinquish. Two 16ths. Two 10s. One man. Three days apart. File it under things that probably mean something.

This was a tournament of inversions. Mullin arrived in Kent as the reigning Wooden Spoon holder, having collected the cutlery in three consecutive TEGs — a competitor, by any honest reading of the form book, who came to the first tee primarily to participate. He departed with the Trophy (154 points, by two) and his ninth Green Jacket (+68 gross, by seven), and along the way set new personal bests at all three Kent courses he visited. Jon Baker, meanwhile, arrived as defending Trophy champion and back-to-back Jacket winner. He was bottom of the Spoon race by the fourth hole of Thursday afternoon and was never seen above it again — 68 holes anchored to last place, the longest unbroken occupancy of the cellar this trip has produced. He, too, set a record before the end. We will come to it.

## Round 1 — Mullin's 40 and Baker's 10

The Thursday round at Royal Cinque Ports was where everything that would matter was, in retrospect, already in place. Mullin posted 40 points for an 87 gross — seven shots better than his visit a year earlier, a personal best at the venue, and an opening statement that nobody quite registered as one. He drew level on the Trophy at the 11th and claimed outright lead at the par-5 16th. That advantage would not change hands again for four days.

On gross, the Jacket flickered briefly. Alex Baker drew level at the par-4 6th — the kind of early-round jockey that means nothing, except that on this occasion it was the last time anyone other than Mullin would top either leaderboard. Mullin retook it at the 9th and from then on the chart was a line with one bend in it.

Baker's 10 came at the par-5 16th. There was also a quadruple-bogey 8 at the 11th, one of the harder holes on the course. By close of play the Trophy gap was three and the Jacket gap was five — small numbers, with quite large implications buried in them.

And at the par-3 4th — the easiest hole at Royal Cinque Ports — Jon Baker made bogey. It dropped him into last place in the Spoon standings. He would remain there for the next 68 holes. The defending Trophy champion, the back-to-back Jacket holder, signed for 29 points.

## Round 2 — Mullin's Cushion Reaches Nine

The Friday round at Littlestone produced another 7-shot course PB for Mullin: 41 points, 85 gross. The Trophy cushion grew from three to nine. The Jacket advantage expanded from five to twelve. The competitor who had arrived with three Spoons in his luggage was now playing the kind of golf that requires the rest of the field to do something quite specific to catch him, and the rest of the field was not doing it.

Alex Baker, in particular, was not doing it. He dropped twelve shots across four holes — triple at the 2nd, double at the 3rd, triple at the 4th (one of the hardest on the course), quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 5th — and added a further quad at the 8th for completeness. Twelve shots, in four holes, on a links he had presumably walked round in roughly the right order. It is the kind of stretch that ends most Trophy challenges. His did not end. It did, however, slip further out of reach.

Jon Baker shot another 29 — thirteen shots worse than he had managed at Littlestone the year before, which is the sort of margin that one does not always wish to dwell on. He remained, of course, last.

## Round 3 — Lead Steady, Damage Spread Around

The Saturday round at Prince's was the day on which everyone offered a contribution to the broader project of damage. Mullin himself opened proceedings with a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 8th — a moment which, in another tournament, in another mood, might have been the start of something. It was not. He closed with thirteen points across the final four holes (par, par, par, birdie), shot 36 for the day, and the Trophy lead held at eight while the Jacket advantage held at twelve. The numbers refused to move.

Around him, the catalogue grew. Gregg Williams's tidy run of pars came to an end with a triple bogey at the par-5 12th — the first crack in a round that had threatened, briefly, to be something. Jon Baker took a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 13th, the hardest hole at Prince's, in a tally of 28 points that did the bulk of the work in confirming what had already been confirmed at the 4th on Thursday. And Alex Baker, the man who would not stop being interesting, dropped six shots in his opening three holes — three doubles — and added a quad at the par-4 10th, one of the harder holes on the course.

It was, as a round, an unusually democratic distribution of error, and it changed almost nothing.

## Round 4 — Baker Charges, Then the Sixteenth Happens Again

For a stretch of Sunday afternoon at Prince's, the tournament briefly became one. Alex Baker, eight points back at the start of the day, played the opening three in 10 Stableford points — three pars — and then, having absorbed a quiet section, found another gear at the 13th. Par at the par-4 13th (the hardest hole on the course). Par at the 14th. Par at the par-5 15th. Eleven points in three holes. The Trophy margin, eight at breakfast, was two.

He walked onto the 16th tee.

The 16th at Prince's is a par-4. It is stroke index 17 — the easiest hole on the course. Baker took 10. Sextuple bogey. The same hole, in the sense that matters, as Thursday's 16th at Royal Cinque Ports; a different hole, in the sense that they are twenty miles and three days apart. The same score, exactly. The chase ended on the green.

Mullin, for his part, found his own moment of mortality at the par-4 10th — a quadruple-bogey 8 that trimmed the Jacket advantage from twelve to seven. Nobody was within striking distance by then. He shot 37 points, won the Trophy by two and the Jacket by seven, and joined a small private club of players who have arrived holding the Spoon and departed holding both of the things one would rather hold.

And at the back of the field, on a card that no longer mattered to anyone but the man writing it, Jon Baker shot 39 points and signed for 83 gross — a new course record at Prince's, beating the previous best across five visits. There were birdies at the par-5 12th and at the par-4 13th, the hardest hole on the course, inside a 14-point burst from the 12th to the 15th. There was also a double at the 16th and a quad at the 18th, because this is still a Jon Baker scorecard and certain conventions must be respected. The Spoon was already his. The course record was, in the circumstances, the consoling artefact of the most ornate Wooden Spoon collection in living memory.

## How the three were decided

**The Trophy** was decided at the 16th at Prince's on Sunday, when Alex Baker's 10 ended a charge that had reduced an eight-shot margin to two. Mullin had led from the 16th at Royal Cinque Ports on Thursday and was never headed. The round-by-round Trophy cushions read 3, 9, 8, 2 — a number that suggests a much closer tournament than the leaderboard, taken as a whole, actually was, and a much more comfortable one than the closing forty minutes felt.

**The Green Jacket** was decided at the 9th at Royal Cinque Ports on Thursday, when Mullin retook the gross lead from Alex Baker and kept it. The Round 2 +14 at Littlestone opened the margin to twelve, where it sat through the weekend; Mullin's Round 4 quad at the 10th brought it back to seven without ever threatening to do more. Ninth Jacket.

**The Wooden Spoon** was decided at the 4th at Royal Cinque Ports on Thursday afternoon, when Jon Baker bogeyed the easiest hole on the course and dropped to last place. Three Stableford rounds of 29, 29 and 28 dug the grave; the Round 4 39, with the course record inside it, was the floral arrangement.

## The men, in brief

- **David Mullin.** Arrived with three Spoons in three TEGs and departed with both of the trophies that aren't the Spoon. Wire-to-wire on the Trophy from Thursday's 16th; never headed on the Jacket from Thursday's 9th. New personal bests at all three Kent courses — 87 at Royal Cinque Ports, 85 at Littlestone, 91 at Prince's, each a meaningful margin on his previous visit. Ninth Green Jacket. The most extreme inversion the records will admit to.

- **Jon Baker.** Defending Trophy champion, back-to-back Jacket holder, Wooden Spoon by close of play on Thursday. Last place from the 4th hole of the tournament to the 18th, uninterrupted. Seven shots worse than last year at Royal Cinque Ports; thirteen shots worse at Littlestone. And then, at Prince's on Sunday, a course-record 83 — the most consoling object ever handed to a Spoon winner, and the kind of round that makes the rest of the week harder, not easier, to explain.

- **Alex Baker.** Trophy runner-up by two, and a one-man catalogue of the routes by which one comes second. Two 10s, both on a 16th; four quads scattered across the four rounds; cold stretches of six shots in three holes (Round 1) and twelve shots in four (Round 2); hot stretches of 10 points in three holes (Round 4) and 11 points in three (Round 4). A new personal best at Royal Cinque Ports (98, four better). The most productive disaster in TEG's books, and if the 16th at Prince's had been any other hole on the property he might have been the one in green.

- **Gregg Williams.** A quieter week, in the manner he has perhaps grown tired of. A personal-best gross total of +76, course PBs at Royal Cinque Ports (93) and Prince's (86), and eleven shots worse than his last visit at Littlestone for variety. The pars finally stopped at the par-5 12th on Saturday — a triple bogey, and the closest thing his day had to an event. Mid-table on the Trophy, which is starting to sound familiar.
