There is a particular silence that descends when a man is doing something nobody in the room has ever seen done. On the back nine of the third round at Boavista, with the Atlantic glinting somewhere off to the west and three other competitors trudging through their own private weather systems, David Mullin signed for a net round of -2. Twenty over the card in gross terms, and the finest round ever struck in the brief, scarred history of TEG.

It was, by then, an act of vandalism against a contest already over. Mullin had led the Green Jacket from the fourth hole of the opening round and never been headed. The Trophy had wobbled, briefly, into Jon Baker's hands overnight, but Mullin had taken it back by the third hole of the final day and was now beating the field about the head with arithmetic. He won the Trophy by seven. He won the Jacket by eleven. He set the record for the best gross round in TEG history on day one, broke his own record on day three, and bookended the week with the two best net rounds anyone has ever produced. The story of TEG 2 is, in the end, the story of a man arriving at a course nobody had played before and behaving as though he had grown up on it.

There were, in fairness, three other competitors present. We should hear from them too.

## Round 1 — Mullin Sets a Marker, Boavista Sets a Trap {.round1 .round}

Boavista — Howard Swan parkland above Lagos, with the Atlantic doing scenic work in the middle distance — was new ground. TEG's first sortie to the Algarve and a venue the field would have to learn in real time over three consecutive days. By teatime on the first, it was clear participants would be learning faster than Boavista was forgiving.

Mullin's opening +0 net, off a gross +20, was the best net round and the best gross round in TEG history. Baker's +4 net, off +32, was the second-best net round and the third-best gross round in TEG history. Neumann's +11 net, off +29, slotted in as the third-best net round and the second-best gross round in TEG history. Three men, three of the finest rounds ever played, all on the same afternoon. It was not so much a tournament opening as a benchmark-setting exercise.

It was also, for everyone else, a warning.

The back nine — and the par-3 14th in particular — emerged early as the place where TEG cards came to die. Gregg Williams arrived on the 12th tee with his round still intact and left the 18th green having shed twenty-two shots in seven holes: double, double, a 9 at the 14th, double, triple at the 16th, a 9 at the 17th, double home. A list so relentless it stops sounding like golf and starts sounding like inventory damage from a small fire.

Henry Meller's own bonfire began one hole earlier. Triple at the 11th, quadruple at the 12th, quadruple at the 13th, an 8 at the 14th, quadruple at the 15th: twenty shots dropped over five holes. The Wooden Spoon position would change hands precisely once across the entire tournament. It changed at the third hole of the first round, when Meller dropped to the bottom and made himself comfortable there for the duration.

Mullin closed the day four clear in the Trophy and nine clear in the Jacket. The leaderboard had a tidy little shape to it, which is generally the last moment such things remain tidy.

## Round 2 — Baker's Day, Mullin's Stumble {.round2 .round}

For most of the second afternoon, Jon Baker was the best golfer in the Algarve. His +1 net round — gross +29 — was the second-best net round in TEG history, eclipsed only by Mullin's own opener. Around him, Neumann was assembling a gross +27 (then the second-best gross round in TEG history) and Mullin a gross +28 (the third-best). On any other week, in any other company, these would be the headlines. Here they were the support act.

The Trophy lead, which had sat with Mullin since the opening tee shot, began to move. Baker took it outright with a par at the 4th. Mullin reclaimed it at the 5th with a bogey. Baker drew level again at the 6th and then, across the 6th, 7th and 8th, banked ten Stableford points — bogey, par, bogey — to edge meaningfully ahead. For the first time all week, the leaderboard had a contest on it.

Then Boavista's closing stretch went to work on both of them.

Mullin bled ten shots across 16, 17 and 18: triple, triple, an 8 at the last. A man in the act of handing back a tournament. Except Baker, having watched all this unfold from a position of fragile advantage, contrived to lose thirteen shots over the final four holes himself: triple at the 15th, double at the 16th, 8 at the 17th, 8 at the 18th. Two men, side by side, dismantling their cards in synchrony, like a pair of cellists who have lost the sheet music but kept playing out of politeness.

Baker, on the math, came out ahead. He carried a three-shot Trophy lead into the final day. The Jacket lead never moved: Mullin by eight.

Meller, meanwhile, dropped eleven shots from the 13th to the 16th — double, triple, triple, triple — in the way one might continue paying a direct debit one no longer remembers setting up.

## Round 3 — Mullin's Masterpiece {.round3 .round}

The contest, such as it was, lasted five holes.

Baker, three clear and needing only to play golf of the standard he had played for two days, instead dropped twelve shots in the opening five: double at the 1st, double at the 2nd, triple at the 3rd, triple at the 4th, double at the 5th. There is no kinder way to phrase it. The man holding the Trophy lead arrived at the 6th tee no longer holding that advantage, and Mullin — who had retaken it outright at the 3rd with a bogey at the par-5, the decisive takeover of the entire tournament — was already gone.

What Mullin then did over the remaining thirteen holes was the round itself: -2 net, +18 gross, both records, both his own. The best gross round in TEG history. The finest net round in TEG history. Set on a course he had first seen on Monday morning.

Behind him, Neumann assembled a gross round of quiet, unfussy quality — a bogey, a double and a bogey through 8, 9 and 10, then a birdie at the par-3 11th to break the cycle — and locked up the Green Jacket runner-up spot at +77.

And Meller, anchored to the Spoon since the third hole of the week, produced a gross +20: the second-best gross round in TEG history at the time, and the cruellest piece of timing in the field. A man who had spent two days as the worst golfer in the Algarve delivered, on the final afternoon, a round that on any other day would have been the story. Instead it was a footnote on a +39 net Spoon total he had effectively secured forty-eight hours earlier.

## How it was decided

The **Trophy** went to Mullin at +6, by seven from Baker at +13. The decisive moment was the third hole of the third round, where Mullin's bogey at the par-5 reclaimed the lead Baker had held overnight. Baker's opening-five bleed put it beyond reach.

The **Green Jacket** went to Mullin at +66, by eleven from Neumann. Mullin led from the fourth hole of the first round and was never headed. The round-by-round leads — nine, eight, eleven — describe a procession, not a contest.

The **Wooden Spoon** went to Meller at +39 net. He dropped to the bottom at the third hole of the first round, and the position never changed hands again. Twenty shots lost over 11-15 in the opening day, eleven more over 13-16 in the second, did the work. The record-equalling third round arrived too late to do anything but decorate a result already settled.

## The men, in brief

- **David Mullin** — A coronation. Opened with the finest round in TEG history, closed with a better one, and in between conceded ten shots over 16-18 in the second round as if to reassure the field he was, at some level, human. The Trophy and the Jacket, by daylight.
- **Jon Baker** — The only man who briefly looked capable of living with him. Two of the finest net rounds in TEG history, a thirty-six-hole Trophy lead, and then twelve shots dropped over the first five holes of the final day. A habit of finding 8s — at the 17th and 18th in the second round, with more besides — proved the difference.
- **Stuart Neumann** — Green Jacket runner-up on the strength of two top-three gross rounds, played with a quiet competence that the net column never quite rewarded. The 14th and a fourteen-shot wobble across 13-17 in the opening round, including his own 8 at the par-3, kept him out of the Trophy conversation.
- **Henry Meller** — Bottom from Tuesday afternoon onwards, his card shredded by a back nine he never solved. Posted the second-best gross round in TEG history on the final day, by which point the Spoon was a foregone conclusion he had spent two days authenticating.
- **Gregg Williams** — A connoisseur of the round-defining 9: one at the 14th in the opening round, another at the 17th the same afternoon, a third at the 12th in the third round. Twenty-two shots shed across the closing seven holes of day one set a tone he never quite escaped.
