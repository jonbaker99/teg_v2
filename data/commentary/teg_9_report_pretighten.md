# Patterson, by a Distance

There are tournaments that hinge on a putt, a gust, a moment of nerve at the death. TEG 9 was not one of them. TEG 9 hinged on a Sunday afternoon at Praia D'El Rey in which a man who had never previously laid eyes on the place produced the second-best round in the history of the competition, and then — with the courtesy of a guest who senses he has overstayed — politely played out the remaining 36 holes until the Trophy could be handed over.

John Patterson, Lisbon novice, finished R2 ten clear of the field. He had begun the morning one behind. The arithmetic of those eighteen holes — 49 points, +14 gross, a number we will return to with the reverence it deserves — settled the Trophy in a single afternoon and rendered the back half of the week a procedural exercise. Up at the front of the Jacket, David Mullin had effectively done the same job at Royal Óbidos the day before, and by the time the field reached R3 the only two competitions with any heat in them at all were "how much" and "by what margin". The answers turned out to be six and ten.

Down at the other end, Stuart Neumann discharged his duties as reigning Wooden Spoon holder with the quiet authority of a custodian who has memorised the keys. Bottom at the end of R1. Bottom at the end of R2. Bottom at the end of R3. Bottom at the end of R4. The man came to defend a title and protected it the way titles are properly protected: without ever once allowing doubt to enter the room.

This was TEG's third visit to the Lisbon Coast, alternating Seve Ballesteros's final design at Royal Óbidos — parkland, water, mischief — with Cabell Robinson's dunescape at Praia D'El Rey. Royal Óbidos played the wrecking ball. Praia handed out the points. The story is best told, then, not in strict order but in the shape that it actually took: three coronations, each effectively complete before the second course had been seen for the second time.

## The Sunday That Settled It

Patterson began R2 a point behind Jon Baker. He ended it ten clear. Inside that swing sit two stretches that deserve to be read out at the next AGM.

Holes 9 through 13 yielded seventeen points: bogey, par, par, birdie, par. Holes 15 through 18 added another fourteen: par, bogey, and — for the connoisseurs — a par at the par-5 17th, SI 1, the hardest hole on the course, followed by a closing bogey on the 18th. He birdied the par-5 12th. Across the whole of his 49-point afternoon, he shipped almost nothing one could classify as a mistake. The gross figure was +14. The Stableford was 49. Only one round in TEG history has ever been better.

The texture of the day around him was, of course, ordinary TEG. Gregg Williams ran up a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 9th. Alex Baker — and we will get to Alex Baker, who deserves a chapter of his own — assembled a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 10th and a quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 14th and *still* posted a personal-best 44 points, with a personal-best gross of +27, and *still* went home that evening a footnote. Mullin, meanwhile, watched his Jacket margin balloon from two to seven without seemingly being asked to do anything strenuous.

Patterson's lead at the close of play was, by the standards of a four-round Stableford, absurd. Briefly, fleetingly, at R2 H2, he had even ceded the lead to Mullin. He took it back at R2 H11 and the question from that point onward was whether he could lose it on purpose.

## Flashing Back: A Royal Carnage

Rewind a day. Royal Óbidos, Seve's final design, opened TEG 9 by hosting both ends of its scoring spectrum on the same morning — which is the kind of debut a course architect, in another world, might have requested.

David Mullin shot 87. Across seven visits to Royal Óbidos, no one had ever broken 90. He took the Jacket lead outright at R1 H18 and, though the dry record will show that Jon Baker drew level briefly at H6 and Mullin himself only drew level twice more before going clear, none of that is the story. The story is that the round was the Jacket in microcosm: the man at the front of the field never moved.

At the opposite pole, Alex Baker shot 115 — a new course-worst at Royal Óbidos, exceeding the prior worst of 108. The card included a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 2nd and a septuple-bogey 11 at the par-4 14th. To set a record on a course visited as often as this one requires a particular commitment, and Alex Baker is nothing if not committed.

Patterson, the visitor, ran up a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 4th, SI 1, and somehow still walked off with 38 points and a Trophy position one behind Jon Baker. Williams, defending the Trophy he had won at TEG 8, opened proceedings with a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 1st — the first hole of his title defence — and topped it off later with a sextuple-bogey 9 at the par-3 15th. Stuart Neumann posted a quintuple-bogey 9 at the same 4th that had bitten Patterson, and a quintuple-bogey 8 at the 15th. That 15th is logged as the moment he became Spoon holder for the duration. He arrived at the green inside the cut. He left it outside it. He never returned.

Jon Baker led the Trophy by one at the close of R1. It is a fact that, viewed with the benefit of what was coming, has a certain wistful quality.

## The Wobble That Wasn't

R3 was Patterson's only ragged afternoon, and even then "ragged" needs sourcing. Back at Royal Óbidos he posted 34 points, his weakest of the week, with a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 12th (SI 2, one of the hardest holes on the course) and a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 18th. The Trophy lead shrank from ten to three.

In any other tournament this would have been the headline. In this one, it was the chapter where the reader is briefly led to believe in jeopardy before being reassured. Mullin, going around the same course, ran up a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 10th. His Jacket lead remained at seven. Threat noted; threat not acted upon.

Neumann, for completeness, stayed bottom.

## R4: The Closing Formalities

Patterson answered the only remaining question with 47 points at Praia. He absorbed a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 5th (SI 2, one of the hardest holes on the course) the way a man absorbs a parking fine on the morning of his wedding. Alex Baker, who had spent the week alternating between brilliance and arson, matched both of his personal bests — 44 points, +27 gross — and at R4 H14 briefly drew level on the Trophy. It was the closest anyone came to Patterson after R2 H2. Patterson re-established the margin at six. Alex Baker took Trophy runner-up on 162. His personal-best gross total stood at +127.

Mullin opened R4 with a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 1st and won the Jacket by ten. There is something instructive about that sentence; he is a man for whom an opening quad does not constitute a crisis.

Williams ran up a sextuple-bogey 11 at the par-5 7th — his third sextuple of the week, joining the R1 9 at the 15th and the R2 10 at the 9th. The 9 at the 1st of R1 was merely a quintuple, and so does not count toward the collection. He nonetheless posted a personal-best gross total of +115. Handicap golf, ladies and gentlemen: a man can produce a sextuple bogey in three of four rounds and still file his neatest week's work.

Neumann, finally, produced a flicker of resistance. Across R4 holes 11–15 he posted 16 points: par at the par-3 11th, par at the par-5 12th, birdie at the par-4 13th, par at the par-3 14th (SI 17, one of the easiest holes on the course, but a par is a par), bogey at the par-4 15th. It was not a rescue. By that point the gap between rescue and a flicker had become an academic distinction. He finished the round, and the tournament, bottom of the Trophy table on 127.

## The Three Coronations

**The Trophy** was lost by Jon Baker on the morning of R2 and won by John Patterson on the afternoon of the same day. The margin was six, 168 to 162. The decisive moment was a 49-point round in which a man playing both courses for the first time in his life converted a one-shot deficit into a ten-shot lead. He also posted, en route, a personal-best gross total of +88. A maiden Trophy and a career-best gross in the same week is the kind of debut that gets noticed.

**The Green Jacket** was Mullin's seventh, and it was decided at R1 H18 of an opening round in which he set a new course record. Wire to wire. Round-end margins of +2, +7, +7, +10. Jon Baker took the runner-up spot for the fourth time in five TEGs and, like Patterson, did so while filing a personal-best gross total of +77. There is a particular cruelty in being the nearly-man on your best ever week.

**The Wooden Spoon** was Neumann's the moment his second ball found whatever it found at R1 H15. He arrived in Portugal holding the title. He departed Portugal holding the title. There are easier ways to defend a trophy than being bottom at the end of all four rounds, but it is difficult to think of any more thorough.

## The men, in brief

- **John Patterson** — Arrived having never set foot on either course; left with the Trophy by six and a personal-best gross of +88. The sextuple at Royal Óbidos's hardest hole in R1 turned out to be the only blemish of consequence. The 49 at Praia was the second-best round in TEG history. The conversion of a Lisbon debut into the second-best round ever played is the kind of fact that will outlive most of us.
- **David Mullin** — Seventh Jacket, won the way the other six were won: by being the best ball-striker in the room and refusing to surrender the lead from R1 H18 onwards. The 87 at Royal Óbidos was a new course record across seven visits. A quintuple at the 10th in R3 and a quadruple at the 1st of R4 were absorbed as routine maintenance.
- **Jon Baker** — Led the Trophy after R1, was buried by Patterson's Sunday, and settled into the role he has made his own: Jacket runner-up for the fourth time in five TEGs, with a personal-best gross total of +77 to show for the week's labour. Bridesmaid, with a polished new dress.
- **Alex Baker** — A study in non-overlapping halves. A course-worst 115 at Royal Óbidos in R1, then back-to-back 44s at Praia in R2 and R4 — each a personal best, each with a personal-best +27 gross — for Trophy runner-up on 162 and a personal-best gross total of +127. The man contains multitudes; some of them are quadruple bogeys.
- **Gregg Williams** — Opened his Trophy defence with a 9 at the 1st of R1, then proceeded to deliver a sextuple bogey in three of the four rounds. Filed a personal-best gross total of +115 anyway. The defending champion is not, this year, the champion. The defending champion's gross is, this year, the tidiest of his career. Both things are true.
- **Stuart Neumann** — Reigning Spoon holder, retiring Spoon holder. Bottom at the end of every round. The decisive drop is logged as R1 H15. The 16 points across R4 holes 11–15 demonstrated that the resources were there; they were merely deployed on a strict need-to-use basis. Title successfully protected.
