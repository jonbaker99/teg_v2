# TEG 11, Catalonia — The Bridesmaid and the King

The photograph, when it eventually surfaces, will show Jon Baker on the steps of the PGA Catalunya Stadium clubhouse holding a piece of silverware in each hand and the expression of a man who has just been told the joke about himself is no longer funny. Three Jackets-finished-second in the previous four TEGs. A bridesmaid in three different dresses. And now, at the end of a week in Catalonia, the full set: the Trophy by six, the Green Jacket by three, two course records sitting in the ledger under his name. The king, meanwhile — David Mullin, eight Jackets in the cabinet and a back-to-back run going in — was somewhere in the car park, holding a wooden spoon.

To understand how the throne tipped over, rewind to Tuesday morning.

## Round 1 — Baker Rewrites the Stadium

The Stadium course had played host to five previous TEG visits, accumulating in that time a course record of 94 and a tacit understanding that nobody was going to do much about it. On the first afternoon of TEG 11, Jon Baker shot 90. Nine shots better than he himself had managed on his last visit here in TEG 6; four shots better than anyone in TEG history had managed full stop. Thirty-five Stableford points. Top of the Trophy by three, top of the Jacket by two, and a faint sense in the group that the week had already declared itself.

Behind him, John Patterson — reigning Wooden Spoon holder, defending the title with the diligence of a man who has come to terms with his calling — was assembling something rather grand in the opposite direction. A 9 at the par-4 4th. A 9 at the par-4 13th. And, across holes 12 to 14, eleven shots dropped: triple, quintuple, triple, the middle one a quintuple bogey on a par-4 that has nothing in particular to recommend it. The triple at the 14th tipped him to the bottom of the Spoon race, where he settled in and made himself at home.

## Round 2 — Two Courses, Two Records

The field crossed the road to the Tour course, where the previous record was 89 across five visits and the prevailing wisdom was again that this was a number unlikely to trouble anyone. Jon Baker shot 84. Six shots better than his own previous best here, five shots better than the prior TEG benchmark, and a second course record in twenty-four hours. He posted 41 Stableford points, which is the sort of number that ends conversations. The Trophy lead stretched. The Jacket advantage stretched. The week, you felt, was approaching a particular shape.

The Tour course, however, had not finished with statistics. In the same group, on the par-5 15th, Patterson made an 11 — a sextuple bogey, his second course-defining hole of the week — and Gregg Williams made a 10 on the identical hole, which is the kind of thing groupmates discuss carefully or not at all. Williams replied with a birdie on the par-4 17th, the third-hardest on the course, an act of defiance from a man who would later finish fourteen shots better at the Stadium than he had on his last visit and yet somehow never quite escape the orbit of catastrophe. Patterson's afternoon totalled 103 gross — a new course-worst for the Tour, narrowly displacing the previous floor of 101. Two course records and a course-worst, all on the same afternoon, all in the same scoring tent.

## Round 3 — Alex Strikes Back

El Prat Azul, a Greg Norman routing making its TEG debut, served as the neutral middle ground on which the leaderboard briefly inverted. Alex Baker, defending Trophy champion and so far this week a man watching his namesake do unreasonable things from two seats down the table, finally landed a blow. A bogey at the par-4 1st — the third-hardest on the course — drew him level with Jon for the Trophy lead. A par at the par-4 13th pushed him outright in front. The defence of his title, abandoned as theoretical for two days, was suddenly material.

In the Jacket, David Mullin chose the same morning to mount his own counter-charge. A par at the par-4 3rd drew him level with Jon. The reign was being defended in person. Three holes later, on the par-3 6th, Jon Baker made a bogey — not a stroke of brilliance, just the right number on the right hole — and retook the outright Jacket lead. That, as it turned out, was the move that decided the gross. He was never headed again. Across the next three holes he posted par, par, birdie for ten Stableford points, which is the kind of stretch that quietly says: I heard you, but no.

And then, at the par-3 12th, the easiest hole on the course, David Mullin made a bogey. It does not, written down, sound like much. It dropped him below Patterson into last place on the Stableford for the first time all week. He would not climb out. The eight-time Jacket champion, the back-to-back holder, last on Stableford on the easiest hole on the course because of one shot too many. The decisive Spoon moment, in other words, looked precisely like nothing at all.

By the close, Alex Baker led the Trophy by two; Jon Baker led the Jacket by three; David Mullin was at the bottom of the pile.

## Round 4 — The Bridesmaid Gets the Lot

The final round returned to the Stadium, the stage that had opened Jon Baker's week and now had the option of closing it. Mullin made an early statement — a par at the par-5 3rd drew him level with Jon for the Jacket lead for the second time in two days, and the king's last credible attempt at a ninth coronation was, briefly, alive. At the par-4 6th, Jon Baker made a double bogey. It was enough to draw him level with Alex for the Trophy.

From there, Alex Baker came apart in instalments. Holes 12, 13 and 14 cost him eight shots — a triple on the par-5, a triple on the par-4 13th, a double on the 14th. Holes 16, 17 and 18 cost him another eight — a double on the par-3 16th, a quadruple on the par-4 17th, and a double on the par-4 18th, the second-hardest on the course, for good measure. Twenty-seven Stableford points, his worst round of TEG to date, posted at exactly the moment the title was sitting in his lap. The Trophy margin, level at the 6th, finished at six.

Mullin held on for the Jacket runner-up spot at three back. Patterson, by sheer momentum of arithmetic, finished clear of the bottom. And Jon Baker — bridesmaid in three of the last four, the nearly-man on first-name terms with the runner-up speech — walked off the Stadium with both.

## How it was won, and how it was lost

The Trophy was Jon Baker's from the opening tee shot to the closing handshake save for one round in the middle, when Alex Baker drew level at the El Prat 1st and went ahead at the 13th. Jon levelled it again at the Stadium 6th in Round 4. Alex's 27-point reply did the rest. The Jacket was led wire-to-wire on the round figures, but Mullin drew level twice — at the El Prat 3rd and at the Stadium 3rd — and the decisive moment, properly accounted, was Jon's bogey at the El Prat 6th, the takeover that stuck. The Spoon was Patterson's for two rounds and Mullin's for two, the handover located precisely on the par-3 12th at El Prat — one shot, easiest hole on the course, eight Jacket titles' worth of history rearranged around it.

## The men, in brief

- **Jon Baker** — The nearly-man arrives. Two course records on consecutive days (90 at the Stadium, 84 on the Tour, each a personal best by some distance over his TEG 6 efforts here), the Trophy by six, the Jacket by three, and the bridesmaid dress finally in a skip somewhere outside Girona.
- **David Mullin** — Jacket runner-up by three, last on Stableford on 125. A personal best of 91 at the Stadium in Round 4, three better than his TEG 6 mark here, that nobody is going to remember on account of where it was filed. The eight-time champion's first Spoon since his only previous one, collected the same week he came within three of his ninth Jacket.
- **Alex Baker** — Defending Trophy champion. Outright leader at the close of Round 3 after the move at the El Prat 13th; 27 points in Round 4 and a card pocked with triples and a quad on the 17th. Hands the title to his namesake by six, having held it outright for the better part of an afternoon.
- **Gregg Williams** — A 10 at the Tour 15th, a 9 at the Stadium 10th, a birdie at the third-hardest hole on the Tour by way of apology. Fourteen shots better at the Stadium than his previous visit here, which in any normal week earns a paragraph; this week it earned a line.
- **John Patterson** — Reigning Spoon holder, two 9s on the opening afternoon, an 11 at the Tour 15th, a 103 gross that displaced the Tour course-worst from five prior visits, and yet — by virtue of one Mullin bogey at the easiest hole on a course nobody had played before — escaped the bottom. Saved by the king's fall.
