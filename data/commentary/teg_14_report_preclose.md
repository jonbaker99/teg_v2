# From Spoon to Sceptre: Mullin's Kentish Redemption

The man who arrived in Kent clutching the Wooden Spoon — and the one before it, and the one before that — left holding both pots. David Mullin, three-time reigning embarrassment of TEG, walked off Prince's on Sunday afternoon with the Trophy under one arm, his ninth Green Jacket draped over the other, and the dignified bearing of a man who had simply mislaid the previous three years and now had them back.

The biography reconciles itself if you squint. Eight prior Jackets always suggested he could play. Three consecutive Spoons implied he no longer could. As it turns out: both true, and seemingly at the same time.

To find the moment it all turned, you go back to Royal Cinque Ports on the first afternoon — to the 16th green at Deal, where Mullin holed out for par on the par 5 and inched into the outright Trophy lead. The wind off the Channel did whatever it does. Nobody marked the occasion. He was not headed across the next 56 holes.

## The week, in shape

This was TEG's second full lap of the Kent rota — the same four courses, the same Romney-and-Sandwich tour as TEG 13, every per-course history line now comparable. Royal Cinque Ports is where the week was decided. Littlestone, with its Purves/Braid/MacKenzie pedigree and its great flat marsh, is where it was buried. Prince's Shore/Dunes — playing host on Saturday and Sunday — is where the course record was rewritten by, of all people, the man going home with the Spoon.

Three competitions, all effectively over by Sunday morning, none dull.

## How Mullin took the Trophy

The Trophy ledger is almost embarrassingly tidy. 40, 41, 36, 37. A 154 total. Leads after each round of 3, 9, 8, 2. Drew level at the 11th at Deal, took outright command at the 16th, and that was that for the headline competition until the closing holes on Sunday.

The wobble, when it came, was at Prince's on Saturday. A 9 at the par-5 8th — a quadruple bogey on a hole he'd been quite content with twenty minutes earlier — invited the question of whether Mullin might, in fact, be made of the same stuff as everyone else. He answered with 13 Stableford points across the closing four holes: par, par, par, birdie. The question was withdrawn.

Alex Baker, runner-up in everything that matters, made one genuine run at him. The Sunday card opened par-par-par on the first three — 10 points banked — and after a wobbling double at the 4th he produced par-par-par again across 13 to 15 for another 11. The 13th is the hardest hole on Prince's; he parred it under pressure. The Trophy was within a couple of points.

Then he stood on the 16th tee.

## Alex Baker and the 16th, a story in two parts

The 16th hole at Royal Cinque Ports is a par 5. The 16th at Prince's is a par 4. They are different holes, on different courses, separated by three days and several miles of Kent shingle. Alex Baker took 10 on both of them.

The first one, in Round 1, was a quintuple-bogey 10 that nobody much remarked on at the time, because Alex Baker also took an 8 at the 11th that afternoon and the round still came in at 98 — a personal best at Deal by four shots. The second one, on Sunday's 16th at Prince's, was a sextuple-bogey 10 that ended a Trophy chase two points short of its target. He had played the previous fifteen holes like a man who could still win this. He played one hole like a man who couldn't.

In between, at Littlestone, he had haemorrhaged 12 shots between the 2nd and 5th — triple, double, triple, quadruple — and added another quad at the 8th for good measure. Around all of which he assembled stretches of golf that were genuinely superb on the property. Two points short. Two 10s at two 16ths. The shape of his week.

## How Mullin took the Jacket

The Jacket was over by Sunday lunchtime, and arguably by Friday evening. Alex Baker had briefly drawn level on gross at the 6th at Deal; Mullin levelled again at the 9th and then, across holes 9 to 12, produced four holes for 13 Stableford points — par, birdie, par, par — and the field was watching his back.

The 87 he signed for at Royal Cinque Ports was a personal best at the course by seven. The 85 at Littlestone the next day was a personal best at that venue by seven. The 91 at Prince's on Sunday was a personal best at that location by one — completing personal bests at all three Kentish courses in a single week, which is the kind of thing that ought to be flagged by some kind of regulator.

The overnight Jacket lead went 5, 12, 12, 7. The quadruple bogeys at Prince's — the 8th on Saturday, the par-4 10th (the third-hardest hole on the course) on Sunday — trimmed the margin without ever genuinely threatening it. Final: +68 gross, seven clear of the runner-up. A ninth Jacket.

The runner-up was Jon Baker.

## How Jon Baker lost everything

Jon Baker arrived in Kent as the defending Trophy champion and back-to-back Jacket holder, with a clean column where the Wooden Spoon ought to live. He left with that column ruined. Thirteen TEGs in, his first.

He dropped to the foot of the Spoon table at the 4th at Deal — a bogey on a par 3, the easiest hole on the course — and he was bottom at every checkpoint thereafter. The 92 at Royal Cinque Ports was seven worse than his last visit. The 92 at Littlestone was thirteen worse than his last visit; on the same patch of marshland he had previously gone round in 79. Stableford rounds of 29, 29, 28. Numbers that do not lend themselves to recovery.

And then Sunday. An 83 at Prince's. A course record. Across six TEG visits to the place, no one had ever been round in fewer than 85. Jon Baker birdied the 12th, birdied the 13th — the hardest hole on the course, the same one that had cost him an 8 on Saturday — and parred the 14th and 15th: four holes for 14 Stableford points. A double at the 16th and an 8 at the 18th tidied the card a little, in the wrong direction, but 83 it was. The lowest gross score Prince's has ever shown a TEG.

He still finished bottom of the Stableford. 125 points, alone at the foot. The best round of golf the venue has ever seen, posted by the man going home with the Spoon. There is no obvious moral.

## Williams, the nearly-man, doing his thing

Gregg Williams shot a personal-best gross total of +76. His 86 at Prince's on Saturday was a course personal best by three. He has won three Trophies in his time. He finished third in this Trophy and third in this Jacket.

His last four Trophy finishes read 2-3-2-2. This one extends it to 2-3-2-2-3. Even his collapses are tidy: a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 3rd at Deal, a triple at the par-5 12th at Prince's after three pars in a row. He plays beautifully and arrives third. He executes career-best golf and arrives third. The chronic nearly-man, now with the gross numbers to match.

## The men, in brief

- **David Mullin** — Arrived as the reigning Spoon-holder for the third TEG running. Left with a third Trophy and a ninth Jacket. Wire-to-wire from the 16th green at Deal on day one. Personal bests at all three Kentish courses in a single visit. The 8 prior Jackets always hinted; the back-to-back Spoons distracted; both, it turns out, were the same player.
- **Jon Baker** — Defending Trophy champion, back-to-back Jacket holder, and now, thirteen TEGs in, the owner of his first Wooden Spoon. Bottom from the 4th hole on Thursday onwards. Posted the lowest round Prince's has ever seen on the Sunday afternoon and it didn't budge him an inch off the floor.
- **Alex Baker** — Runner-up in the Trophy by two; runner-up to the 16th hole twice. A quintuple at Deal's 16th in Round 1, a sextuple at Prince's 16th in Round 4 — bookends to a week of brilliant stretches and ruinous interruptions. The PB 98 at Deal was the kind of round that contained both halves of him.
- **Gregg Williams** — Career-best gross at +76, a personal best at Prince's, and third in both competitions. Last four Trophy finishes now read 2-3-2-2-3. Even the breakthroughs land him in the same place.
