# The Algarve Reshuffle {.report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 10 | Algarve, Portugal | 2017</p>

By the back end of Sunday afternoon at Boavista, Alex Baker was no longer playing golf in any recognisable sense. He was assembling a monument. Across holes 5 through 10 — bogey, par, par, bogey, par, par — he had banked 21 Stableford points in six holes. By the time he signed for 51, the highest single-round Stableford total TEG has ever recorded, his closing card carried with it a +17 gross, a personal best, and the most decisive coronation the competition has yet produced.

Which raises two questions, both of which had been hanging in the Algarve sun all week.

How does a man who finished second on the Trophy in each of the last two TEGs — bridesmaid in 8, bridesmaid in 9 — finally get over the line? And how does the man who walked off that R9 podium clutching the trophy itself end up watching the closing holes from outright last, where he had been since the 4th hole of the opening round?

The answers were written in three separate ledgers — Trophy, Jacket, Spoon — and on this trip they all moved at once.

## Round 1 — Mullin rewrites Boavista, and the record book {.round1 .round}

Boavista, in its eleventh TEG outing, had been quietly accumulating a course record across fifty rounds and four prior visits. The mark stood at 82. David Mullin, arriving on the back of consecutive Jackets and seven in the cabinet already, walked round it on the opening morning in 75. Plus four. The best Gross round in TEG history. The same card returned 49 Stableford points — the second-best Trophy round on record — and by the time he handed it in he was eight clear on the Trophy and fourteen clear on the Jacket. Before lunch on day one.

It was, in the strictest sense, never a fair fight thereafter. The Jacket lead would drift between ten and fourteen all week without once threatening to disappear. Mullin had moved to the front of the Gross at the 6th and would not be headed for the remaining sixty-six holes of the tournament.

At the other end of the card, the early signs were no kinder. John Patterson, defending Trophy champion, took outright last at the 4th. He added a 10 at the par-5 8th — the second-hardest hole on the course — a 7 at the short 6th and an 8 up the last. He would hold the place he took at the 4th for sixty-eight further holes, every one of them. Stuart Neumann, the reigning Spoon-holder going for three in a row, signed off with a sextuple 10 at the 18th, which felt at the time like an opening statement and turned out to be a recurring theme.

## Round 2 — Personal bests all round, and Patterson stumbles at the 1st {.round2 .round}

The middle two rounds were TEG's first look at Palmares, beginning on the Praia/Alvor pairing. New ground tends to flatter the field, and so it proved. Jon Baker posted +10 gross, the third-best Gross round in TEG history. Alex Baker set personal bests in both metrics — 45 points and +23. Gregg Williams went further: a personal-best 48 points and a personal-best +14. The newcomers' tariff, paid out generously.

Mullin's Trophy lead, eight after R1, was down to five.

John Patterson opened with a 10 at the par-4 1st. He had not yet completed a hole of the round, and the tournament had already, in any meaningful sense, finished with him. Stuart Neumann's contribution to the Algarve's middle act was a three-hole sequence around the 13th, 14th and 15th — 10, 9, 7 — which bled thirteen shots into the scorecard in roughly twenty minutes. He then closed with another sextuple 10 at the 18th, the second in two outings.

The trajectory was unchanged. Only the company was changing.

## Round 3 — The Trophy turns at the 6th {.round3 .round}

Palmares, this time on the Alvor/Lagos pairing, is where the Trophy quietly changed hands for good. Alex Baker took outright lead at the par-4 6th with a par. Gregg Williams, still riding the previous day's 48, took it off him briefly with a bogey at the par-5 13th. Baker retook it at the 15th. From there he extended.

He extended, it should be said, despite a quintuple-bogey 10 of his own at that same par-5 13th — a hole that nibbled at all three principals in different ways. The eventual Trophy winner, posting a personal-best 47 points, ran up a 10 in the middle of it and still led by eleven at the close. That is the shape of the week in one sentence.

It is also the round in which John Patterson's Spoon, already a near-certainty, became a mathematical formality. Holes 11 through 15: triple, quintuple, triple, quadruple, quadruple. Nineteen shots haemorrhaged across five holes. The quintuple came at the par-3 12th, which the card lists as the easiest on the course. He added a 10 at the par-5 8th for thematic consistency.

Baker took an eleven-point lead into the final round.

## Round 4 — 51, by way of coronation {.round4 .round}

Back to Boavista, and this is where we came in.

Baker's 51 points need no further architecture: the six-hole stretch from the 5th to the 10th — twenty-one points in itself — was the spine of the round, with a par at the par-3 6th, a par at the par-4 7th, a bogey at the second-hardest hole on the course and a par at the par-4 9th, which is listed as the easiest. By the close he had a personal-best +17 gross, a personal-best Gross total of +90, and a 184-point Trophy by eleven.

Mullin's eighth Jacket came in at +51 gross, by twelve. The pre-eminent gross machine of the modern TEG era did what he reliably does, and added a new Boavista course record — 75, seven better than his prior best on the course and twenty-three better than his last visit — to the trophy cabinet for good measure. Jon Baker, with a personal-best Gross total of +63, took the runner-up spot for the fourth time in five TEGs; the third-best Gross round in TEG history, posted in R2, was the engine. Gregg Williams, who briefly held the Trophy lead at R3 H13, signed off with a 7 at the par-3 6th and an 8 at the 18th, banked a personal-best Gross total of +76, and finished second on the Trophy for the third top-two in his last five.

And John Patterson, who had taken outright last at the 4th hole of Wednesday morning and held it for the remainder of the week, posted his worst round to date: 21 points, +40 gross, with an 11 at the par-5 8th — same hole where he had made 10 in R1, same hole where he had made 10 in R3. Four rounds, four entries on the par-5 8th: 10, —, 10, 11. A defending Trophy champion's first Wooden Spoon, sealed at the only hole on the property that seemed genuinely to know his name.

## How the three competitions were won

**The Trophy** was decided at the par-4 6th in R3, when Alex Baker made par to take outright lead. It moved once more in the round, briefly, when Williams nipped ahead at the par-5 13th, and Baker retook it at the 15th. Final margins: led by eleven at the end of R3, led by eleven at the end of R4. 184 points. Bridesmaid in 8, bridesmaid in 9, the dress is finally back in the wardrobe.

**The Green Jacket** was effectively over at the 6th hole of R1, when Mullin moved to the front of the Gross en route to a +4 that put him fourteen clear before lunch on day one. The only subsequent lead change was a brief share with Jon Baker at the 9th of the same round. The Jacket margin by round read 14, 13, 10, 12. Wire to wire.

**The Wooden Spoon** changed hands at the 4th hole of R1, when Patterson took outright last from Neumann, and then stayed put for the longest unbroken occupation of bottom place in recent memory — sixty-eight holes without movement.

## The men, in brief

- **Alex Baker** — Two runner-up finishes, then this: four personal-best Stableford rounds in four rounds, four personal-best Gross rounds in four rounds, a personal-best Gross total, the best round in TEG history to close, and a first Trophy by eleven. The 10 at the par-5 13th in R3 belongs in the file too, because it sums up the week: he made a quintuple bogey and led by more by sundown.

- **David Mullin** — Arrives on back-to-back Jackets, opens with the best Gross round TEG has seen and a new Boavista course record, leads the Jacket wire to wire and closes it out by twelve. Eighth Jacket. The Trophy that he led after R1 and R2 before letting go at the 6th in R3 is the only smudge on an otherwise immaculate week.

- **Jon Baker** — A personal-best Gross total of +63 and the third-best Gross round in TEG history in R2, and yet again the runner-up on the Gross — in contention for the fourth time in five TEGs. Briefly led the Trophy outright at the 9th of R1, which is now a piece of trivia rather than a story.

- **Gregg Williams** — A personal-best 48 points and a personal-best +14 in R2, a personal-best Gross total at week's end, and a forty-minute spell as outright Trophy leader at the 13th in R3. A quieter Sunday, with a 7 at the short 6th and an 8 up the last, settled him into the Trophy runner-up spot — his third top-two in five TEGs.

- **John Patterson** — Defending Trophy champion at the gate, outright last by the 4th, never anywhere else for sixty-eight further holes. A 10 at the par-4 1st in R2, a 10 at the par-5 8th in R3, an 11 at the par-5 8th in R4, nineteen shots given back over five holes in R3, and a closing 21 points that was his worst round to date. The most complete inversion the Algarve had to offer, and TEG's archive will have to find space for his first Spoon.

- **Stuart Neumann** — Spoon-holder going for a hat-trick; spared only because someone else took it. Sextuple-bogey 10s at the 18th in both R1 and R2, thirteen shots bled across the 13th, 14th and 15th in R2, and a best Boavista round of the week that matched, to the shot, his last visit there. The trajectory has not altered; only the company at the bottom has.
