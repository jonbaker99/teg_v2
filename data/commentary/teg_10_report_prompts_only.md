# Baker's Algarve Breakthrough

Halfway down the 10th fairway at Boavista on a Sunday afternoon, Alex Baker had just taken four at a par four for the third time in six holes. Behind him, a six-hole stretch read 21 points at +2 gross. Ahead of him lay eight more holes in which he could not, under any reasonable arithmetic, lose the thing he had spent two entire TEGs failing to win. The bridesmaid, finally, was the bride.

That he would end the afternoon with 51 Stableford points — the highest single round in TEG history — was, by then, almost incidental. The tournament had already been decided. What remained was the question of how big the trophy cabinet would need to be.

Rewind seven days, then, to the same fairway at Boavista, where the whole story begins with a different man entirely.

## Round 1 — Mullin Rewrites the Gross Record at Boavista

It is rare, in TEG, for a competition to be over by Friday evening. Rarer still for it to be over before anyone has eaten. David Mullin, on the 11th TEG visit to Howard Swan's Atlantic-view parkland, opened with a Gross round of +4 — the lowest single round ever recorded across the entire history of the tour. It was worth 49 Stableford points, which is itself the second-highest Stableford round TEG has ever seen. One card, two entries in the record book, and a 14-shot Green Jacket cushion banked at the first time of asking.

Around this seismic event the rest of the field arranged itself into the postures they would broadly maintain for the next 72 holes. Stuart Neumann, arriving as the reigning Spoon holder and looking to dispose of the thing rather than accept it for a third year, began his defence by carving a sextuple-bogey 10 at the 18th. John Patterson, the defending Trophy champion and theoretically the man to beat, dropped to outright last by the 4th. He would not climb out of that position at any point in the remaining 68 holes. A quintuple-bogey 10 at the 8th — SI 2, one of the hardest holes on the course — sealed the early tone.

Mullin led the Trophy by eight. Eight is a lot. Eight is not, however, fifteen.

## Round 2 — A Personal-Best Picnic at Palmares

Palmares, Robert Trent Jones Jr.'s newer routing along the Lagos bay, made its TEG debut over the Praia/Alvor loop, and the field responded by handing out personal bests like sun cream. Alex Baker posted a PB Stableford round of 45 points at a PB Gross of +23. Gregg Williams went one better on the Stableford ledger — a PB 48 — and a PB Gross of +14. Jon Baker, not to be left off the registry, returned +10 gross, the third-best Gross round in TEG history at that point and a single-shot reduction in his Green Jacket deficit. One shot. Against fourteen. The cavalry, such as it was, had brought a spoon.

Mullin, the man being chased on the Trophy, returned a card sufficient only to trim his lead from eight to five — which, against the personal bests sprouting up around him, felt rather like leaving the pub early.

At the bottom of the leaderboard, the comedy was darker. Patterson opened with a sextuple-bogey 10 at the 1st, which is the sort of start that suggests the man has already given up on the round and is now negotiating with the weekend. Neumann then produced one of the more remarkable three-hole stretches of the trip: 10 at the par-5 13th, 9 at the 14th, 7 at the 15th, for 13 dropped shots in twelve minutes of golf. He capped it with a second sextuple-bogey 10 at the 18th — SI 2 here, too — to match his Round 1 finale. The Spoon, mercifully for him, was being contested elsewhere.

## Round 3 — The Day the Trophy Changed Hands

The Alvor/Lagos loop is where the Trophy quietly, and then not so quietly, left David Mullin's possession. The lead changed hands like a passed sangria jug across the front nine: Baker drew level at the 4th, Williams drew level shortly after, Baker took outright at the 6th, Mullin drew level at the 7th, Williams went ahead at the 13th with a bogey at the par-5, Baker drew level again at the 15th. By the time the dust settled and the round was signed, Baker had a 47-point card — another personal best, on both Stableford and at +22 gross — and an 11-point Trophy lead. Eleven would, eerily, prove to be the exact final margin.

The moment that mattered was Baker's outright lead at the 6th. From there, with 30 holes left to play, he was never headed.

That he managed all this while taking a quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 13th himself is the sort of detail that makes the breakthrough feel earned rather than gifted. He simply outscored the consequences.

At the bottom of the leaderboard, Patterson's afternoon was the kind of thing a war correspondent files and then deletes for being too on-the-nose. A quintuple-bogey 10 at the 8th — his second in three rounds on a hole he had clearly identified as a personal nemesis — and then, on the back of it, a five-hole bleed at 11 through 15 that read 7, 8, 8, 7, 9 for a combined +19. The 12th, where he took a quintuple-bogey 8, is SI 18 — the easiest hole on the course. The 11th, where he took triple, is SI 2. He was being equitably destroyed by both ends of the difficulty curve.

## Round 4 — Baker Signs the Best Round Ever Played

Back at Boavista for the venue's 12th TEG round, Alex Baker — the man who had been second in TEG 8 and second in TEG 9, the perennial nearly-man with a CV of solid, unrewarded competence — went out on the Sunday and shot 51 Stableford points at +17 gross. The 51 is, full stop, the highest Stableford round in TEG history. The +17 was his third PB Gross outing in three rounds, and capped a tournament Gross PB of +90 across the four days.

The decisive stretch, if such a thing can be said of a coronation, ran from the 5th through the 10th: bogey 5, par 3, par 4, bogey 6 at the par-5 8th (SI 2, the hole that had cost Patterson his soul), par 4 at the SI 18 9th, par 4 at the 10th. Twenty-one points across six holes, at a combined +2. By the turn, the question was not whether Baker would lift his first Trophy but whether he would do so in record-breaking fashion. He would.

Mullin, meanwhile, processed in to a final Green Jacket margin of 12 — his eighth. He had led after Round 1 by 14, after Round 2 by 13, after Round 3 by 10, and after Round 4 by 12. At no point across 72 holes did anyone get closer than 10. Jon Baker took the runner-up slot with a tournament gross of +63, also a personal best, completing his now-third Jacket second place in the last four TEGs. The bridesmaid's bridesmaid.

At the very back of the procession, Patterson posted his worst round in TEG: 21 points, +40 gross, including a sextuple-bogey 11 at the 8th — the same 8th, the SI 2, the recurring villain. He had carded 29, 35, 27 and 21 across the four rounds and finished bottom in every one of them. The Spoon was sealed at 112.

## How the three were decided

**The Trophy** turned at the 6th of Round 3, where Baker took outright for the first time and was never headed across the remaining 30 holes. His PB 47 set the margin at 11; the record 51 preserved it; Gregg Williams's PB 48 in Round 2 and brief outright lead in Round 3 were the only serious resistance, and they yielded a runner-up finish at exactly that 11-shot gap. Williams's tournament gross of +76, also a personal best, deserves a sentence of its own, and now has one.

**The Green Jacket** was decided on Friday afternoon at Boavista, when Mullin's +4 banked 14 shots in a single sitting. The rest was admin. Eight Jackets.

**The Wooden Spoon** was decided at the 4th hole of the tournament. Patterson took outright last on Friday morning at Boavista and held it for 68 consecutive holes — past the 10 at the 8th in Round 1, past the 10 at the 1st in Round 2, past the 10 at the 8th in Round 3, past the 19-shot stretch at 11–15 the same afternoon, past the 11 at the 8th in Round 4, and across the finish line at 112. Neumann, the reigning Spoon, brought three of his own sextuple-or-quintuple bogey 10s and a 13-shot bleed in twelve minutes — and was spared, because Patterson, gallingly and definitively, outdid him.

## The men, in brief

- **Alex Baker** — Trophy at last, on 184 points, by 11. PB Stableford in R2 (45). PB Stableford in R3 (47). Highest round in TEG history in R4 (51). PB Gross in R2, R3 and R4; tournament Gross PB of +90. After second in TEG 8 and second in TEG 9, the breakthrough was so comprehensive it bent the record books on the way through.
- **David Mullin** — Eighth Green Jacket, by 12, on the back of the lowest Gross round TEG has ever seen. Led the Trophy by eight, then five, then watched it drift across the bay. Two days at the top of one ladder, two days atop the other, and only the cheaper one to take home.
- **Gregg Williams** — A PB Stableford 48 and PB Gross +14 at Palmares, an outright Trophy lead at the 13th of Round 3, a tournament Gross PB of +76, and second by 11. On any other weekend, the story of the trip.
- **Jon Baker** — Jacket runner-up for the third time in four TEGs, this time with a PB Gross tournament of +63 and a +10 at Palmares that was, briefly, the third-best Gross round in TEG history. The cavalry brought a spoon, etc.
- **John Patterson** — Defending Trophy champion to Wooden Spoon in 72 holes. Bottom from the 4th of Round 1, bottom in all four rounds, a 10 at the 8th in R1, a 10 at the 1st in R2, a 10 at the 8th in R3, an 11 at the 8th in R4, a 19-shot stretch in between, and a personal-worst 21-point closing round. 112.
- **Stuart Neumann** — Arrived as reigning Spoon holder with two on the bounce and the chance of a hat-trick. Produced two sextuple-bogey 10s at the 18th, a quintuple-bogey 10 at the 13th, and a 13-shot bleed across three holes at Palmares. Escaped. Patterson saw to that.
