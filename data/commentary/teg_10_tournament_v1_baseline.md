## In which a man eight points behind wins by eleven

Begin, as all good stories should, at the end. Or rather, six holes into the end. It is Round 4 at Boavista, the Atlantic doing its postcard impression beyond the parkland, and Alex Baker has just stood on the 5th tee and proceeded to play the following six holes: bogey, par, par, bogey, par, par. Twenty-one Stableford points. A man cannot, in any meaningful sense, score more points than that across holes 5 to 10 of a golf course. It is the heart of a round of **51 points** — the highest ever posted in TEG, full stop, end of argument, kindly update the honours board.

Now rewind. Because four days earlier the same Alex Baker had been **eight points** behind David Mullin and looking, frankly, like scenery.

This is the story of how that gap closed.

## Round 1 — Mullin Lights Up Boavista {.round1 .round}

The 11th TEG round at Boavista belonged, comprehensively and from quite early on, to David Mullin. He went round in **+4 gross for 49 Stableford points** — at the moment of signing, the **second-best round in TEG history**, behind only the sort of score people had stopped expecting to see. The engine room was a three-hole burst at 12, 13 and 14: par at the par-4 12th, birdie at the par-5 13th, birdie at the par-3 14th. Eleven points in a row. The Trophy lead at the close: eight.

Behind him, Alex Baker quietly posted 41 and went unnoticed, which suited him.

At the other end, two early signposts to the week ahead. Stuart Neumann, having negotiated seventeen perfectly civilised holes, arrived at the par-4 18th and made a **10** — a sextuple bogey to walk in on, and, as it would transpire, only the first instalment of a personal correspondence with that particular green. And John Patterson, who had taken sole possession of last place as early as the **4th hole**, decided to consolidate by carding a **10 at the par-5 8th**. He finished the day on 29, last, and would remain so for the rest of the week without ever really being challenged for the position.

## Round 2 — Palmares Debut, Patterson's Opening Disaster {.round2 .round}

TEG had never been to Palmares before. Robert Trent Jones Jr., the Praia and Alvor nines spliced together for the opening day — new ground, and as it turned out new ground that suited some men considerably better than others.

Patterson opened with a **10 at the par-4 1st**. There is, in a manner of speaking, no faster way to introduce yourself to a golf course.

Gregg Williams, by contrast, introduced himself rather beautifully: **48 points at +14 gross**, a personal best, and the sort of round that would have been the headline in almost any other week. Alex Baker, still going about his business with the air of a man pretending not to notice the leaderboard, also posted a **personal-best 45**, trimming Mullin's lead from eight to five.

And Stuart Neumann resumed his ongoing project. From holes 13 to 15 at Palmares he dropped **13 shots in three holes**: a 10 at the par-5 13th, a 9 at the par-4 14th, a 7 at the par-4 15th. He then walked to the 18th — a *different* 18th, this one on the Alvor nine — and made *another* **10**. Two rounds, two closing holes, two tens. Some men collect wine.

Mullin held the Trophy lead; Patterson, on 35, held everything else down.

## Round 3 — Baker Takes the Wheel {.round3 .round}

If Round 4 is where Alex Baker won the Trophy, Round 3 is where he decided to. Palmares again, this time the Alvor/Lagos combination, and a third successive personal best: **47 points at +22 gross**.

The leaderboard moved in a way it had not moved all week. At the **par-4 6th**, a par from Baker gave him outright possession of the Trophy lead for the first time. Mullin briefly clawed back a share at the 7th. Then, at the **par-5 13th**, the lead changed hands again — Gregg Williams made a bogey, took the outright lead, and would have been entitled to feel pretty good about it had he not been standing next to Alex Baker, who had just made a **10** at the same hole. By the **15th** Baker was level again, and from there he held a share of the top for the rest of the round and finished the day **eleven clear**.

The arithmetic of that swing was largely supplied, as it had been all week, by John Patterson. At the par-5 8th, a 10. Then, across holes 11 to 15, he shed shots in the following order: triple bogey, quintuple bogey, triple bogey, quadruple bogey, quadruple bogey. **Nineteen shots dropped in five holes.** Twenty-seven points for the round. Last, again, by some distance.

## Round 4 — The Greatest Round in TEG History {.round4 .round}

Back to Boavista for the 12th TEG round at the venue, and the day already described in the preamble. Williams, gamely, threw an early punch — a **birdie 3 at the par-4 5th** drew him momentarily level for the Trophy lead. Then Baker played those six holes, and the tournament was effectively over before lunch.

**Fifty-one points. +17 gross.** The best round ever played in TEG. Eleven-point Trophy victory, final tally **184**, with Williams home in second on **173** — a fine total in any normal week, and a normal week this was not.

While Baker was rewriting the record book, David Mullin was completing a piece of work that deserved rather more attention than it got. **+13 gross** on the final day, and the Green Jacket settled by **twelve clear strokes** at **+51** to Jon Baker's +63. Mullin had led the gross from R1 H6 — bar a fleeting share at R1 H9 to Jon Baker — and led at the close of each round by 14, 13, 10 and 12. Wire to wire is the technical term. Untroubled is the human one.

And Patterson? Patterson signed for a personal-worst **21 points**, including an **11 at the par-5 8th** — third time of asking at that hole this week, and by some margin his worst yet. Final position on the Stableford after every single round of the trip: last, last, last, last. Final aggregate: **112**. The Wooden Spoon, sealed without ever once being in doubt.

## How it was won (and lost)

- **The Trophy (Stableford).** Alex Baker — eight back after R1, five back after R2, ahead from the 6th hole of R3 (level briefly, again briefly behind, never properly headed thereafter), home by 11. Three escalating personal bests: 45, 47, 51. The 51 is now the best round in TEG history; the 47 and 45 are merely his two next-best ever.
- **The Green Jacket (Gross).** David Mullin, from R1 H6 to the final putt. Margin: 12.
- **The Wooden Spoon.** John Patterson, from R1 H4 to the final putt. Margin: not the point.

## The men, in brief

- **Alex Baker** — Wandered around in second place for a day, then quietly produced the three best rounds of his TEG life in succession, the last of them the best round of *anyone's* TEG life. Trophy by 11. A week that will be talked about for as long as anyone can be bothered to talk about anything.
- **David Mullin** — Opened with 49, briefly held the second-best round in TEG history, and was overtaken by exactly one man, three days later. Consolation: a Green Jacket led wire to wire and won by twelve. As consolations go, perfectly acceptable.
- **Gregg Williams** — A personal-best 48 at Palmares, a brief outright Trophy lead at R3 H13, a brief share again at R4 H5. The 10 at the par-5 13th in R3 was the moment the advantage came and went in the same breath; second on 173.
- **Jon Baker** — Held a share of the gross lead for one hole at Boavista on the opening day, then settled into a steady, almost invisible week to claim the Green Jacket runner-up spot at +63. Anonymous, by his standards, and effective.
- **John Patterson** — Two tens, an eleven, a personal-worst 21-point round to finish, and last place after every single round. The Spoon was effectively his by the 4th hole of the week and he never let anyone near it.
- **Stuart Neumann** — Conducted a private and one-sided feud with the 18th hole — a 10 there at Boavista in R1, another 10 at the (different) 18th at Palmares in R2 — and threw in a 13-shot collapse across three holes mid-R2 for good measure. The 18ths of the Algarve are pleased to see him go.
