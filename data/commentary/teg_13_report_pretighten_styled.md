There is a photograph, or there should be, of Jon Baker walking off the 18th at Prince's on the Sunday afternoon with a Trophy in each hand and an 18-shot Jacket lead in his pocket. TEG had never been to Kent before. It had certainly never witnessed anything like this. The Jacket margin was the largest in the competition's history, full stop, and the man holding it had led every one of the seventy-two holes that produced it.

So the question across four days on the Channel coast was never really *whether* Jon Baker would win. It was: by how much, and what would Kent take from everybody else in the meantime. Four debut links courses — Royal Cinque Ports, Prince's twice over (Dunes/Himalayas, then Shore/Dunes), and Littlestone in between — were lined up to find out. The answers, in order: by six and by eighteen; and quite a lot.

## Round 1 — Royal Cinque Ports, Royally Conceded {.round1 .round}

The Thursday round at Deal set the tone with disconcerting efficiency. Jon Baker went par, par, birdie at the par-5 3rd — ten Stableford points banked inside three holes — and from there the leaderboard arranged itself into the shape it would broadly keep for ninety-six hours: Baker on top, the rest beneath, and John Patterson somewhere quite specific and quite low. By the close, Baker had 39 points, a six-shot Trophy lead and a nine-shot Jacket cushion.

The Patterson catalogue, which will become a recurring feature of this report, opened at the 1st: a quadruple-bogey 8. He added a quintuple-bogey 9 at the 7th, another 8 at the 10th, and a 9 at the par-5 16th. Four blow-ups in eighteen holes, none of them costing fewer than four shots to par. The defending Trophy champion was, on paper, defending it from below.

Alex Baker, meanwhile, bled nine shots over the four-hole stretch from the 4th: a double at the par-3 4th (the easiest hole on the course), a triple at the 5th, doubles at the 6th and 7th. He found another quad-bogey 8 at the 11th for ballast.

At the bottom of the Wooden Spoon table, the chairs were already being rearranged with some enthusiasm. Last place changed hands six times in eighteen holes — Patterson, Williams, Mullin, Williams again, Alex Baker, and finally Patterson once more, who walked off the 18th with the seat warm beneath him. None of this, in the end, would matter, but it gave the round its texture: nobody was safe, and Patterson was least safe of all.

## Round 2 — The 18th-Hole Reply at Prince's {.round2 .round}

The Friday round on the Dunes/Himalayas was the only round in which the Trophy felt alive, and it didn't feel alive for long. Jon Baker scored 36, which was perfectly fine, but Gregg Williams played the round of a man who had read the leaderboard and concluded that something would have to be done. By the 15th, he had drawn level.

It is the only moment across four days when Jon Baker had a peer rather than a pursuer. He answered it three holes later with a birdie at the par-4 18th. Trophy lead restored, outright, by one. Williams had pulled level; Williams had not led. There is a difference, and the scorecard observed it.

The Patterson catalogue resumed in some style. He bled eleven shots across the opening four holes — an 8 at the 1st, a double at the 2nd, a triple at the par-5 3rd, a double at the 4th — and added another quadruple-bogey 8 at the 17th later on, for the collector's set. Alex Baker, having begun the previous round with a four-hole bleed, ended this one with a five-hole deterioration: double bogeys at the 14th, 15th, 16th, 17th and 18th, the closing stretch tidied away with the grim consistency of a man trying not to look at his card. David Mullin contributed his own quadruple-bogey 8 at the 9th — noted, filed, not yet decisive.

Patterson, as he had at Deal, finished the day in last.

## Round 3 — Patterson's 49, Baker's +8 {.round3 .round}

Saturday at Littlestone is the round this tournament will be remembered for, and it will be remembered for two scorecards, sitting side by side, both of them historic, both belonging to men who could not have been further apart in the standings.

John Patterson — yes, that John Patterson, the man with five 8s and a 9 in two rounds — posted a personal-best +12 gross and 49 Stableford points. It is the third-best round in TEG history. The defending champion, having spent two days demonstrating that links golf could be played in almost any number of strokes per hole, played it in twelve over par. He even managed a quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 17th on the way home, and it barely registered against the rest of the card.

The trouble for Patterson — and for everybody else with a horse in the Trophy race — was that Jon Baker chose the same afternoon to shoot +8 gross, the second-best Gross round in TEG history, for 44 points. Patterson's heroics were the third-best round ever recorded; Baker's were superior.

Williams again drew level with Baker for the Trophy lead, this time at the par-4 1st (the easiest hole on the course). It is the closest the competition came to actually changing hands. Baker then accumulated thirteen points across holes 8 to 11 — par, birdie at the par-3 9th, par at the par-4 10th (the hardest hole on the course), par — and the race was over. Williams was not idle: pars at six consecutive holes from the 6th, then three more at 12, 13 and 14, before a double at the par-4 15th finally interrupted the run. He played beautifully. He gained nothing.

Alex Baker found a quad-bogey 8 at the 2nd, and at the 9th slid into last place in the Wooden Spoon race, where he ended the day. Patterson, for the first time in three rounds, was not bottom. He was, instead, third in the history books.

## Round 4 — Mullin's Ninth at the Tenth {.round4 .round}

Sunday at Prince's — Shore/Dunes this time, a different routing for a different purpose — was a coronation in two parts. Jon Baker shepherded 39 points home, lengthened the Jacket lead from 14 to its final, record-setting 18, and at no point gave anybody cause to look up from their own card. Nine points across the 12th, 13th and 14th — three pars — was the stretch that turned the procession into a formality.

Patterson, having posted the third-best round in TEG history the day before, opened with an eagle at the par-5 2nd. This is the same player whose first hole at Deal had cost him four over par and whose first hole at Sandwich had cost him four over par. By the 15th — a par-5 — he was running up another 9. The card he submitted on the final day contained an eagle and a quadruple bogey, which is, in its way, a summary of his entire tournament.

Alex Baker, having begun the trip leaking shots in clusters, closed it with a personal-best +17 gross. Less a redemption than a steadying — but a steadying clearly worth noting.

And then there was David Mullin. He had drifted through three rounds in the comfortable anonymity of mid-pack, his eight Jackets in the cabinet at home and his two consecutive Wooden Spoons quietly unmentioned. At the 10th on Sunday — one of the hardest holes — he made a quintuple-bogey 9. It dropped him to the bottom of the Wooden Spoon race. He did not move from there. A third Spoon, and a third in a row, secured on a single hole.

## How it was decided

**Trophy.** Jon Baker led after R1 by six, after R2 by one, after R3 by five, after R4 by six. Williams drew level twice and led never. The birdie at the 18th at Prince's on the Friday closed the only real window; the +8 at Littlestone on the Saturday made sure it stayed closed. Margin: 6 points.

**Green Jacket.** Nine after R1, seven after R2, fourteen after R3, eighteen at the close. No lead changes across seventy-two holes. The Saturday round at Littlestone was the round it became absurd. The final margin of 18 is the largest in TEG history.

**Wooden Spoon.** Patterson held last after R1 and R2; Alex Baker after R3. Mullin took possession of it at the 10th on Sunday and refused to give it back. Final tally: 135 points.

## The men, in brief

- **Jon Baker** — Wire-to-wire double. Led every round of both competitions across seventy-two holes; the only flicker was Williams drawing level on the Friday 15th, answered with a birdie at the 18th. Trophy by six, Jacket by a record eighteen, and a third straight Jacket.
- **Gregg Williams** — Twice the best of the rest, twice not the man. Drew level for the Trophy lead at R2 H15 and again at R3 H1; led outright at neither. Six consecutive pars at Littlestone could not move a leaderboard that was being moved by someone else.
- **John Patterson** — The defending Trophy champion turned in six 8s-and-9s across the four rounds — at Deal's 1st, 7th, 10th and 16th, at Sandwich's 1st and 17th, at Littlestone's 17th, at Prince's 15th — and also the third-best round in TEG history, also an eagle at the par-5 2nd on the final day. A tournament that visited every available extreme and finished, somehow, in the middle.
- **David Mullin** — Eight-time Jacket winner; now three-time Wooden Spoon holder, three in a row. Spent three rounds in mid-pack, then made a single 9 at the 10th on Sunday and the sentence was written.
- **Alex Baker** — Nine shots gone over four holes at Deal, ten over five at Sandwich, last in the Wooden Spoon race by the 9th at Littlestone. Closed with a personal-best +17 gross at Prince's on the final day — not redemption exactly, but the right shape of card on the right day.
