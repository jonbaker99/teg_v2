# Carnage on Seve's Last Stand {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 9 | Round 3 | 02/10/2016 | Royal Óbidos</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> AB 42 | GW 38 | SN 37 | JB 34 | JP 34 | DM 33</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> DM +22 | JB +22 | GW +25 | SN +26 | JP +29 | AB +30</p>

The scoreboard, viewed coldly, says John Patterson still leads the TEG Trophy. Examine it warmly — with a glass of something and a wince — it reveals rather more than that. A ten-shot cushion has been hacked down to three. Every single one of the six cards returned at Royal Óbidos carries at least one quadruple bogey or worse. The aggregate tally of disasters unfolds like a wine list: a 9, a 9, a 9, an 8, an 8, an 8, an 8, a 7. Seve Ballesteros' final design, on TEG's third visit to the Lisbon coast, did not so much examine the field as frisk it at gunpoint.

That none of us escaped unmolested is, in its way, the point. Royal Óbidos is a proper Challenge Tour venue with proper water and proper teeth, and on a day when the Trophy race was supposed to be quietly fossilising into a Patterson coronation, it instead handed out blow-ups by the half-dozen and reshuffled the chasing pack. The headline goes to **Alex Baker**, who posted 42 Stableford points — the round of the day — and rose from joint third into clear second without ever quite looking like he was trying. The subhead belongs to Patterson, who survived. Just.

Take **Jon Baker** first, because he was off like a whippet. Sixteen points through the opening five — par, birdie at the par-5 2nd, par, bogey, par — is the sort of start that has a man mentally drafting an acceptance speech by the 6th tee. The acceptance speech did not survive contact with the 10th, 11th and 12th. A double-bogey 6, then a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 11th, then another double-bogey 6: eight shots gone in three holes, the cushion shredded, the front-nine glow extinguished. He limped home for 34 points, second in the Jacket race still, level with Mullin in the Trophy, and entitled to feel that the course had mugged him in broad daylight.

**John Patterson**, leader, started his own version of the same horror film three holes earlier. Holes 2 to 4 yielded a double-bogey 7 at the par-5 2nd, a triple-bogey 6 at the par-3 3rd, and a triple-bogey 7 at the 4th — eight shots to the bad before the round had really begun. He stabilised, as leaders do, and then unstabilised again: a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 12th and, for symmetry, a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 18th to sign off in style. Thirty-four points. Lead intact. Lead very much pruned. A round of damage limitation in which the damage, frankly, was not greatly limited.

The man making him uncomfortable is **Alex Baker**, whose 42-point effort is the more remarkable for not being a clean one. He shipped his own eight shots across 10–12 — a triple-bogey 7, a triple-bogey 8 at the par-5 11th, a double-bogey 6 at the 12th — and then took a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 14th for good measure. Absorbing a nine on your scorecard and still posting the day's best return is the kind of arithmetic that requires genuine scoring elsewhere, and Alex found it. Joint third to clear second, and now, with one round to play, the man Patterson actually has to watch.

**David Mullin** keeps the Green Jacket lead, and at exactly the same seven-shot margin he started the day with, which sounds tidier than it was. His nadir was a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 10th — neatly cancelled, as you'll have noticed, by Jon Baker's quadruple-bogey 9 at the 11th the following hole. The Jacket race is therefore preserved by mutual implosion, the golfing equivalent of two men falling down the same flight of stairs at the same speed. His 33-point Stableford return, however, was the day's quietest, and it cost him second place in the Trophy: Alex Baker came past him and Mullin slipped to third, level with Jon Baker on 110.

**Gregg Williams** had himself a tidy day, which on this evidence counts as larceny. Thirty-eight points, second-best of the field, with only a quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 15th to apologise for. He gained on everyone above him without ever threatening to actually catch them, and remains fourth in both races — a man going about his business while the leaderboard burns around him.

Which leaves **Stuart Neumann**, who, in a deeply Neumannish twist, posted 37 points and went *backwards*. There were flashes here that on a kinder day would have been a story: nine points across the par-3 6th, par-5 7th and par-3 8th (par, bogey, par), and then twelve points across 10–13 (bogey, par, bogey, par) — a stretch through which he was, briefly, the best player on the course. Sandwiching this, however, were a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 4th, another quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 16th, and a closing 8-6-7 in which eight shots vanished over the final three holes. Last in the Trophy, and — somehow — further adrift than he started.

The races, then, shifted but did not break. Patterson's Trophy lead has gone from a comfortable ten to a nervy three, with Alex Baker now the man in the next seat rather than two seats back. The Green Jacket margin sits stubbornly at seven, only because Mullin and Jon Baker traded nines on consecutive holes. And the Spoon gap, defying Neumann's best effort of the day, has actually stretched from 14 to 15 — proof, if any were needed, that on Seve's last stand a respectable score wasn't enough to outrun the catastrophe everyone else was also producing.
