# Baker's Best, Patterson's Prize

## Level at the 14th, Levelled by the 17th

Picture the scene on the 14th green at Praia D'El Rey. Alex Baker has just tapped in for a double-bogey 5 on a par three — the kind of score that, in almost any other circumstance, you would file under "minor accident" and walk briskly to the next tee. But here, on a Tuesday afternoon on the Lisbon Coast, that scruffy little five has done something extraordinary: it has drawn him level with John Patterson at the top of the Trophy. The whole tournament, four rounds in, and this is the closest anybody has come to JP. Baker is having the round of his life — a personal-best 44 Stableford points is already in the post — and the Trophy is, for the first time in a fortnight of golf, genuinely in play.

It lasts one hole.

To understand both how he got there and how it slipped away, you have to rewind to the front nine, which Baker played in the manner of a man wrestling a slightly oiled pig. The damage came in a clump: a triple at the 4th, a bogey at the 5th, a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 6th, and another bogey at the 7th. Four holes, eight shots dropped, Trophy push apparently in a ditch. And yet the very next hole — the par-3 8th — he holed for a birdie 2 to stop the bleeding, and from there set off on the long climb back. It was a wonderful, slightly mad piece of golf, and it ought to have been the story of his TEG 9.

It might still have been, had the dunes on the inward stretch not been waiting. From level pegging on the 14th green, Baker proceeded to play 14 through 17 in a cumulative eleven over par: the double at the 14th itself, then a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 15th, a triple-bogey 7 at the 16th, and a double-bogey 7 at the par-5 17th. Eleven shots. Four holes. Trophy gone. He still signed for 44 points, the best round of his TEG career, and on any other day that would be the headline. On this day it was a footnote to the title race.

Which brings us, of course, to **John Patterson**, who watched all of this unfold while quietly putting in one of the more underwhelming Trophy-clinching performances you'll ever see. Patterson's day had its own moment of horror — a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 5th, the kind of number that on a tighter leaderboard would be a tournament-ending injury. But JP had brought a three-point cushion into the round, and a three-point cushion turns out to be exactly the right amount of insurance for a nine. He absorbed the blow, watched Baker reel him in, watched Baker fall away again, and converted three points into six. Not pretty. Not memorable. Entirely sufficient. TEG 9 is his.

The Green Jacket race, meanwhile, threatened briefly to become interesting and then very firmly did not. **David Mullin** opened with a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 1st — a tee shot at dawn, basically, with the title on it — and for about twenty minutes the field could permit themselves a small flicker of hope. Mullin then steadied himself with four pars at 2 through 5 (a double-bogey 6 followed, which we'll quietly overlook on his behalf) and, crucially, stitched together three consecutive pars at the par-5 7th, par-3 8th and par-4 9th — nine Stableford points across the stretch and the wobble was over. The seven-shot overnight lead became ten by the time he holed out on 18. Green Jacket, sealed.

**Jon Baker** was the only man within plausible reach, and "plausible" was doing some heavy lifting even at the start of play. He went about his round in his usual quietly competent way, made precisely no inroads on Mullin, and then ran up a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 17th to confirm what everyone already suspected. Second on gross, ten back, and a perfectly respectable runner-up.

**Gregg Williams** produced the day's single most spectacular piece of carnage: an eleven — yes, an eleven, a sextuple bogey — at the par-5 7th. It is the kind of number you write on the card very neatly, in the hope that neatness might somehow lend it dignity. The rest of his round did what it could to forget that hole had ever happened, and he ends the week fifth in the Trophy.

And then, inevitably, **Stuart Neumann**, for whom the Spoon has been a slowly tightening certainty for some time and who duly delivered on the contract. He opened the front nine by bleeding nine shots across holes 2 to 5 — double, triple, double, double, a sort of metronomic disintegration. He then, in the manner of a man who has decided to enjoy the view on the way down, came briefly and brilliantly to life with 16 points across the 11–15 stretch, including a birdie at the par-4 13th and pars at the 11th, 12th and 14th. For five holes he looked like a man playing for the Trophy. Then the coastal dunes finished what the front nine had started: a quadruple-bogey 8 at the 16th, a double-bogey 7 at the par-5 17th and a double-bogey 6 at the 18th — eight shots dropped over the last three, and the Spoon locked up by a thumping sixteen.

So how the three races were won. The Trophy was — for one hole, on the 14th green — genuinely live, and Baker's name belongs in the record as the only man to draw level with Patterson at any point in TEG 9. The 15th took it back, the 16th and 17th confirmed it, and JP's three-shot lead became six. The Jacket survived a one-hole scare at Mullin's opening tee shot and was effectively settled by the turn; the seven-shot lead became ten and Jon Baker's quad at the 17th drew the final line under it. The Spoon, frankly, was never in doubt — 15 overnight, 16 at the close, Neumann's name on it from some distance out.
