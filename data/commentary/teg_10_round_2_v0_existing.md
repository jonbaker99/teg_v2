# Palmares Punishes the Patient {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 10 | Round 2 | 01/10/2017 | Palmares - Praia / Alvor</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> GW 48 | AB 45 | JB 45 | DM 42 | SN 37 | JP 35</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> JB +10 | DM +11 | GW +14 | AB +23 | JP +25 | SN +33</p>

There is a particular flavour of dread reserved for a brand-new TEG course on a brand-new TEG morning, and Palmares — Robert Trent Jones Jr.'s 27-hole confection above Lagos, the parkland Alvor stapled to the seaside Praia — served it up cold on the 1st tee. John Patterson, first into the breach, walked off the opening par-4 having taken **ten**. Not a misprint. A sextuple bogey before anyone had quite finished their coffee. It was the kind of hole that announces a layout's intentions, and Palmares duly spent the rest of the day extracting payment from someone, somewhere, on every nine. By close of play it had handed out two personal bests, three double-figure holes, and a Trophy race that has remembered it is a race.

Patterson's 10 was the day's overture and, in a sense, his entire round. Once the narrative was set he could not unset it, and he trudged home to finish the day's cards level with Neumann's 27 — anchored, once again, to the bottom of the Trophy and clutching the Spoon with the grim resignation of a man who increasingly suspects it has been welded to his hand.

At the same 1st, however, Jon Baker simply parred it, then birdied the par-5 2nd, then parred the par-3 3rd, and was suddenly **10 points in three holes** to the good. A flying start of the sort that makes the rest of the field check their pencils. Alongside him, Alex Baker was doing the same job in a quieter suit — bogey, bogey, par for 9 points, no fireworks, no fuss.

And then there was Mullin, who responded to all this early commotion by going completely, almost provocatively, calm. From the **4th** he parred the par-5, parred the par-3 5th, parred the par-4 6th, parred the par-4 7th, parred the par-3 8th — **five consecutive pars, fifteen points, metronome on, brain off**. It was the kind of run that makes a leader look unassailable right up until the moment it stops. Which, at the par-5 9th, it did: a double-bogey 7, a single shot conceded, and a small but audible reminder that the man at the top of both boards is in fact mortal.

By then Jon Baker's serenity was contagious. He answered the 9th with the fifth of **six straight pars from the 5th to the 10th — eighteen points on the spin** — a stretch of golf so untroubled it almost makes you forget the double-bogey 7 he'd posted at the par-5 4th to interrupt his own opening surge. Almost.

If the front nine belonged to Mullin's restraint and Jon Baker's calm, the inward stretch belonged emphatically to Gregg Williams. Quietly grinding through the early holes, Williams found a different gear from the 8th onwards. Par at the 8th. Par at the par-5 9th. **Birdie at the par-4 10th.** Par at the par-3 11th. Bogey at the par-4 12th. That is **18 points across holes 8–12**, the kind of five-hole burst that turns a tidy card into a thing of beauty. He then signed off par-par-bogey-bogey from the 15th for another 12 points and walked in with **48 — a personal best, and the round of the day**. From fourth overnight to joint-second by sundown; a statement, delivered without raising the voice.

Alex Baker, meanwhile, was assembling his own personal best in parallel, and for a while threatening better. Having banked those 9 points in the first three, he added another **10 across the 5th to 7th** (bogey, bogey, par) and looked every inch the man to catch Mullin. Then came the stumble: three straight double bogeys at **12, 13 and 14** — a 6 at the par-4 12th, a 7 at the par-5 13th, a 6 at the par-4 14th — six shots tipped over the side in the space of forty minutes. Enough to cost him sole second; not nearly enough to derail the bigger number. He came home in **45**, a personal best of his own, and the Trophy gap to Mullin is now five.

Which leaves Stuart Neumann, the day's pantomime villain, who had been quietly going about his business — the 9th through 12th yielded 14 points, par, bogey, par, par, perfectly respectable — until Palmares decided it had not yet had its pound of flesh. Across the **13th, 14th and 15th** he posted, in order, **10, 9 and 7**: a quintuple bogey at the par-5 13th, a quintuple bogey at the par-4 14th, a triple bogey at the par-4 15th. **Thirteen shots dropped in three holes.** For good measure he added another sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 18th, just to make the symmetry with Patterson's morning complete. A 27-point card, the worst of the day, and an extraordinary effort to wrest the Spoon from Patterson's grip that nevertheless — somehow — failed. Patterson scored worse early; Neumann scored worse late; the net effect is that the gap at the bottom has *widened* from 3 to 5. There is a lesson in there about timing.

The three races moved as follows. The Trophy lead held but breathed in sharply: Mullin's 42 was outscored by Williams (48), Alex Baker (45) and Jon Baker, and the cushion is down from eight to five with Williams and Alex Baker tied on his coat-tails. The Jacket lead barely flinched — 14 became 13, and with everyone else now 39-or-worse over par, this is rapidly becoming Mullin's to lose. And at the bottom, the Spoon flipped hands precisely nowhere, despite Neumann's most concerted efforts at grand larceny on the inward stretch.
