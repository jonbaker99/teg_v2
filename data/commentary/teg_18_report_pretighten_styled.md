The final scoreboard tells the story in one bewildering line: Gregg Williams, who had arrived in Girona with last year's Wooden Spoon still tucked under one arm, departed with the Green Jacket draped over the other. Won by fourteen. He has three of them now.

Pan one place along the page and the second inversion announces itself. Jon Baker, the defending Trophy champion, the man who only twelve months ago held aloft TEG's senior prize, is engraved in the small print as the holder of the Spoon. From the top of the tree to the bottom of the well in a single TEG.

And in the third column — the Trophy itself, won by 8 — sits a name that has decorated the Wooden Spoon engraving plate three times already, twice in the past three TEGs. Alex Baker. The handicap, it turns out, was paying attention.

Three competitions, three reigning holders displaced, three trophies handed across the table to the wrong-looking pair of hands. This is the Catalan Reshuffle.

## The familiar two-step

A fourth pilgrimage to Catalonia, and the customary Camiral choreography: two rounds on the Tour Course, two on the Stadium. Old records were, the bundle warned us, in danger. Both fell. The Tour Course gave up its all-time gross-worst in Round 2; the Stadium relinquished its course record in Round 3. One was set in awe, the other in something rather less flattering.

The structural pivot of the week was the course swap. The Jacket changed hands on the first day of Stadium golf and never wavered thereafter. The Spoon, more or less, did the same. Everything that looked settled after 36 holes was upended within two of the next.

## How the Trophy was won — Alex Baker's 67-hole lockdown

The narrative of the Trophy is that Alex Baker took outright command on the fifth hole of the tournament and stayed there for the next sixty-seven. Round-by-round margin at the close: 12, 11, 12, 8. The lead never wobbled inside single figures until the final card was signed.

That this should be the work of a man with three prior Wooden Spoons — and the holder of it in two of the last three TEGs — is the first reshuffle. That he should have done it while also writing himself into the Tour Course's history book for the worst gross score ever shot there is the second.

The Round 1 opener was 46 points of brisk efficiency. Holes 8 through 12 yielded 17 points; holes 14 through 16 a further 13, decorated with a birdie at the par-5 15th. That this same card also contained a quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 2nd — the easiest hole on the layout — and a 9 at the par-5 18th tells you everything about how Stableford launders disaster. The handicap took the bin bag out the back.

Round 2 escalated the experiment. A gross 106 on the Tour Course. The previous worst across 22 prior TEG visits had been 105; Alex took out the record by a single, hard-earned shot, with a quintuple-bogey 8 at the 16th and a sextuple-bogey 11 at the 18th. Between them, with the kind of insouciance that defines this week, he birdied the par-4 17th. The card returned 41 Stableford points. The advantage, somehow, stretched to eleven.

Round 3 on the Stadium produced the week's signature passage: a 12-shot bleed across holes 6–10 (three triples and two doubles) followed immediately by 21 points across holes 11 through 16. The cushion went back to twelve. Round 4 added a sextuple-bogey 10 at the 14th and an 8 at the 17th, and still the margin was 8.

A first Trophy since TEG 11. The patron saint of the Wooden Spoon has, at long last, accepted his promotion.

## How the Jacket was won — Williams, slow and then sudden

For 36 holes, the Jacket belonged to Jon Baker. By two after Round 1, by two after Round 2. The Tour Course threw routine early-round jockeying at everyone — Mullin drew level at the 3rd in R1, Alex Baker and Williams both drew level at the 16th — but Jon held the line at the close of both days.

Williams, meanwhile, was assembling something less ostentatious. Round 1 had two quadruple bogeys in it — a 9 at the par-5 7th and an 8 at the par-4 17th, the latter the third-hardest hole on the layout — but a birdie at the par-5 15th stopped a long stretch of bogey-or-worse from running away with him. Round 2 produced 9 points across holes 3–5 and 13 across holes 13–16. The kind of golf no one writes home about, which is exactly the kind of golf that wins Jackets.

The Stadium, when it arrived, settled matters with quiet decisiveness. Williams took the outright Gross lead at the par-4 2nd of Round 3 and was never headed across the next 34 holes. Four straight pars from the 2nd through the 5th piled up 12 points; the run ended with a double at the 6th. He closed the round with a score of 13 and a margin of 8.

Round 4 was the coronation: a personal-best 84 at the Stadium, 8 shots better than his last visit there, improving his Camiral best by the same. A round score of 12, a final margin of 14, and a third Green Jacket for a man who, this time last year, was the one being teased about the wrong piece of silverware.

## How the Spoon was lost — Jon Baker's two-hole undoing

The Wooden Spoon position changed hands ten times across the week. Round 1 left Jon at the bottom only by the narrowest margin and the unhappy accident of late timing; Round 2 saw it pass through Williams, Jon twice more and finally Mullin. Two days in, the slow puncture was visible but not yet terminal.

Then came the par-4 2nd at the Stadium in Round 3. A triple-bogey 7. It dropped him to the bottom of the Spoon race, and there — across the remaining 34 holes — he stayed.

Two holes later, on the par-4 4th, came a quintuple-bogey 9. Round 3 closed at 28 points, the week's lowest round to that moment. Round 4 contrived to undercut it conceptually if not numerically: a five-hole bleed of 10 shots across holes 7 through 11, every one a double bogey (7 at the par-5 7th, 5 at the par-3 8th, 6 at the par-4 9th — the hardest hole on the layout — 6 at the par-4 10th and 5 at the par-3 11th). He stopped the bleeding eventually with a birdie at the par-4 13th, but the books were closed.

29 points, 126 total. From Trophy holder to Spoon holder in a single TEG, an inversion as clean as anything this week.

## The Stadium gives up its record

Round 3 also produced the week's single defining individual achievement, and it was nobody mentioned above. David Mullin shot 84 at the Stadium — a new course record across 27 prior TEG visits, beating the previous mark by five clear shots, and a personal best by seven. Inside it, 10 points across holes 8 through 10: a par at the par-3 8th, a bogey at the par-4 9th (the hardest hole on the layout) and a birdie at the par-4 10th.

That this should be the same David Mullin who shot a sextuple-bogey 11 at the par-5 15th in Round 1, and dropped to the bottom of the Spoon race in the process, is the proper texture of the week. Camiral is a venue that rewards selective memory.

He settled, in the end, for Jacket runner-up at 14 back. The record will outlast the runner-up trophy.

## Patterson, stitching

If there is a quiet hero of the week, it is John Patterson, who in Round 1 contrived to bleed 22 shots across eight holes — the 3rd through the 10th — in a sequence that reads, on the page, like a man losing a fight with a hedge. Quadruple-bogey 8, triple-bogey 7 at the second-hardest hole on the layout, double-bogey 7, double-bogey 6, triple-bogey 8, triple-bogey 6, triple-bogey 7, double-bogey 7. Then he played the back nine in 34 Stableford points.

Round 2 produced a personal best at the Tour Course — 94 — with 16 points piled across holes 11–15, including a bogey at the par-4 13th, the hardest hole on the layout. Round 3 produced a personal best at the Stadium too — 93. And Round 4, after a slide of 7-7-bogey across the 14th, 15th and 16th, produced the gesture that locked down second: a birdie at the par-4 17th. Trophy runner-up on 161. Personal bests on both Camiral courses, banked for the road.

## The men, in brief

- **Alex Baker** — Wire-to-wire from the fifth hole of the week, leading the Trophy by 12, 11, 12 and 8 at successive closes. Won by 8 on 169. The handicap absorbed an 11, a 10, an 8 and two 9s, plus a 7 at the easiest hole on the Tour Course. First Trophy since TEG 11; the Spoon habit is, for now, broken.
- **Gregg Williams** — Reigning Spoon-holder to Jacket-holder in one TEG. Outright Gross lead from R3 H2, never headed. Sealed with a personal-best 84 at the Stadium, 8 better than his last visit there. A third Green Jacket, and the most quietly assembled of the trio.
- **Jon Baker** — Led the Jacket through 36 holes, then surrendered it at the par-4 2nd on Sunday Stadium day and slid into the Spoon position at the same hole. A 9 at R3 H4 and a five-hole, 10-shot bleed in R4 sealed it. Defending Trophy champion, departing Spoon-holder. 126.
- **David Mullin** — Wrote the line of the week with an 84 at the Stadium in R3 — new course record by five across 27 prior visits, personal best by seven. Settled for Jacket runner-up 14 back. An 11 at R1 H15 was the price of entry.
- **John Patterson** — 22 shots gone in eight holes on day one, then patient repair work: a back-nine 34 to close R1, personal bests on both Camiral courses, and a closing birdie at the par-4 17th in R4 to lock down Trophy second on 161.
