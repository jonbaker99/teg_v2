# The Wheel Turns in Catalonia

Look at the photograph. Gregg Williams, who twelve months ago was last seen clutching a Wooden Spoon, is wearing the Green Jacket. Jon Baker, who twelve months ago was the Trophy champion, is holding a Wooden Spoon and trying not to look at it. And the man with the actual Trophy — the silverware, the points total, the eight-shot margin — is Alex Baker, owner of three previous Wooden Spoons, two of them collected in the last three TEGs.

This is TEG 18. Every competitor on the podium was, twelve months ago, a cautionary tale. Catalonia, fourth visit, two courses, four rounds: the Tour, then the Stadium. Camiral set a course record this week and an anti-course-record this week, in the same field, on consecutive days. The week reorganised itself around inversions.

What follows is the unpicking of how each reversal arrived — beginning with the man who won the Trophy by playing some of the worst golf of the week.

## The Trophy: Alex Baker, In Spite of Everything

Some Trophies are won by sustained excellence. This one was won by sustained excellence interrupted by atrocity, and then more sustained excellence, and then more atrocity, all wrapped around a lead so durable nothing could quite shift it.

The decisive moment came on the Tour course on Thursday, at the 5th hole of the championship. Alex Baker had taken the outright Stableford lead at the 3rd, given it back to David Mullin at the 4th, and reclaimed it at the 5th. From that point onwards — across the next 67 holes of the tournament — he was never headed. Not once. Not after the quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 2nd in Round 1, the easiest hole on the course. Not after the 9 at the 18th to close that round. Not after Round 2's sextuple-bogey 11 at the 18th, his personal par-worst, and certainly not after the 8 at the par-3 16th the same day, on one of the course's gentler holes.

The lead was built in seizures. Round 1 holes 8 to 12: bogey, bogey, par, par, bogey for 17 Stableford points. Holes 14 to 16: another 13. He went to bed twelve clear. The next morning he assembled a 106 — the worst gross round ever played on the Tour course in TEG history, beating a previous worst of 105 that had stood across 22 visits — and somehow only lost one point of cushion. The 11 at the 18th was followed, immediately, defiantly, by a birdie at the 17th. Sorry, the 17th came first, then the 11. The stopping-of-the-bleed had already happened. The bleeding kept going anyway.

By Saturday his lead was back to twelve, courtesy of holes 11 to 16 on the Stadium: par, bogey, par, par, bogey, bogey, twenty-one points, the round of the week from a man who twenty minutes earlier had been hemorrhaging twelve shots across holes 6 to 10. Sunday produced a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 14th and a quadruple at the 17th, and still he won the Trophy by eight, on 169 points to John Patterson's 161. Three Wooden Spoons in the cabinet. Now a Trophy alongside them. The handicap did some work; the refusal to flinch did the rest.

## The Green Jacket: Williams, From the Bottom of the Table to the Top

For 36 holes the Jacket belonged to Jon Baker, the defending Trophy champion, at +2 — a position he had earned with a closing stretch on Friday of birdie-par-par-par-par across holes 14 to 18 that genuinely deserved a better fate than the one that came for it.

That fate was Gregg Williams, last year's Wooden Spoon holder, who arrived at the Stadium course on Saturday morning two strokes back and walked onto the par-4 2nd. He made a par. With that par he took outright Gross lead. He would not relinquish it.

The par at the 2nd was the first of four in a row — par-4, par-5, par-4, par-3 — yielding 12 Stableford points across holes 2 to 5. By the end of Saturday his two-shot deficit had become an eight-shot lead. On Sunday he produced a new personal best at the Stadium, an 84, matching the record Mullin had set the day before, and including a nine-point streak across holes 8 to 10: par, bogey at the par-4 9th — stroke index 1, the hardest hole on the course — par. The cushion stretched from 8 to 14. Final margin: 14 strokes, at +66 gross.

From defending Spoon-holder to a Jacket procession. The handover took place on the 2nd hole at the Stadium. Williams did not need to do anything extravagant. He merely had to stop being the worst golfer in TEG, which he managed with considerable distance to spare.

## The Wooden Spoon: Jon Baker, At The Same Hole, On The Same Day

The cruellest joke in TEG 18 — and there were several queueing — is that the Jacket and the Spoon were decided on the same hole, in the same round, by the same swing of the wheel.

The 2nd at the Stadium. Williams pars it and takes the Jacket. Jon Baker triple-bogeys it and drops to the bottom of the Wooden Spoon race. He never climbed out.

The defending Trophy champion had begun the week leading the Gross competition. He bottomed out briefly in Round 1 at the 18th, was overtaken by Mullin on Friday — there were ten changes at the foot of the table across the week, an unedifying conga line — and then, on Saturday morning at the 2nd, fell to last and stayed there. Two holes later, at the par-4 4th, he made a quintuple-bogey 9. Round of 28 Stableford. The hole that took the Jacket from him also delivered him the Spoon; one of life's tidier arrangements.

Sunday was a slower deterioration: ten shots dropped across holes 7 to 11, five consecutive double bogeys at the par-5 7th, par-3 8th, par-4 9th (SI 1 again, paying its second visit), par-4 10th, par-3 11th. A birdie at the 13th, by way of apology to no one in particular. Rounds of 30, 39, 28, 29 for 126 points. Eight shots worse on the Stadium than his last visit there — the exact inverse of the men ahead of him on the leaderboard. Williams improving by eight, Mullin by eight, Jon Baker worsening by the same amount, as though the points had been redistributed by committee.

## The Footnote That Will Outlast The Result

A word, before we move on, for David Mullin. On Saturday at the Stadium he shot 84 gross. The previous course record at the Stadium, across 27 prior TEG visits, was 89. He did not beat it; he removed it from circulation. Seven shots better than his own previous best on the course, eight superior to his last visit, and one of the cleaner moments of golf TEG has ever staged.

He finished Jacket runner-up by fourteen.

On the Tour course earlier in the week he had run up an 11 at the par-5 15th in Round 1 and back-to-back 8s in Round 2. He was ten shots worse on the Tour course than his last visit there. The record is his forever; the result belongs to other people. Nine majors-worth of trophies, perhaps, none of them this one.

## Patterson, Honourable Second

A note also for John Patterson, who finished Trophy runner-up by eight. He set personal bests on both Camiral courses — a 94 on the Tour course in Round 2, a 93 on the Stadium in Round 3 — and yet his rounds were structurally peculiar. Round 1 contained a 22-shot bleed across holes 3 to 10: quadruple, triple, double, double, triple, triple, triple, double. Round 2 produced an 11-shot hemorrhage across holes 6 to 10, but he answered it with 16 points across 11 to 15, including a par at the hardest hole on the course. He kept doing this. The pattern of an honourable runner-up: ruined for a stretch, rebuilt for a stretch, repeated.

## The men, in brief

- **Alex Baker** — Three prior Wooden Spoons. Now a Trophy, by eight. Wire-to-wire from the 5th hole of the week across the next 67. Set the new Tour course-worst at 106, also banked an 11, a 10 and two 8s, and won anyway. The most unanswerable response to one's own back-catalogue available in the sport.
- **Gregg Williams** — Arrived as reigning Wooden Spoon holder, left in a Green Jacket by fourteen. Took outright Gross lead with a par at the Stadium 2nd in Round 3 and never gave it back. New Stadium PB of 84 on Sunday, eight strokes better than his last visit. A procession.
- **Jon Baker** — Defending Trophy champion. Led the Jacket through 36 holes. Surrendered the Jacket and acquired the Spoon at the same hole on the same morning. Quintuple-bogey 9 at the Stadium 4th in Round 3; five straight doubles across Sunday's holes 7 to 11. Eight shots worse at the Stadium than the last time he was there. Began the week leading something, ended it leading nothing.
- **David Mullin** — Set the new Stadium course record at 84, demolishing a mark that had stood across 27 prior visits. Finished Jacket runner-up by fourteen. A record without a result; the footnote will outlast the result.
- **John Patterson** — Trophy runner-up by eight. PBs on both Camiral courses. A man whose card you could fold along the perforations: bleed, recovery, bleed, recovery. Second, honourably.
