# Mountain Sickness in Sintra

There is a particular face a TEG golfer pulls when, having sliced his second into the eucalyptus on a par-4 he has never seen before, he watches a playing partner do exactly the same thing thirty seconds later. By the time the fourth ball of the afternoon had been hacked sideways out of the Sintra undergrowth and onto the 16th green for a putt to save 8, that expression had become the unofficial mood of the round. Four players. Scores of 8 or worse. On a single par-4. Penha Longa, Robert Trent Jones Jr.'s mountain parkland set into the hills above Lisbon and reputed to be one of the most punishing courses in Portugal, had been given the keys to TEG for the afternoon, and TEG had handed them back with bleeding knuckles.

And yet — and this is the part that will require a stiff drink to process — the man who came off the mountain in the strongest position in the Trophy is the same man who made a 10 at the 12th. And the man who took control of the Green Jacket did so by, of all things, *not* doing anything interesting. Welcome to Penha Longa, where the leaderboard cares not for justice.

Begin, then, with **Gregg Williams**, whose round defies any normal accounting. The back nine started warmly enough and then, at the par-5 12th, ended in a quintuple-bogey 10 — the centrepiece of a three-hole stretch (11 to 13: double, 10, double) that cost nine shots in fifteen minutes of golf. Most days that's your card filed under "regrettable" and your evening spent staring into a glass of something. But Williams wasn't finished bleeding: 15 through 17 took another seven off him — double at the par-3 15th, triple at the 16th (he got off lightly), double at the par-3 17th. The remarkable, almost offensive fact is that he *still* posted the day's best Stableford haul. Not because he played particularly well in the stretches between the carnage, but because everyone else was busy posting worse. His Trophy lead, which was four overnight, is now eleven. The leaderboard's least-deserved victory, served with a smile.

The quietest narrative of the afternoon — and the most significant — belongs to **David Mullin**. He appears in precisely none of the disaster beats. No quads, no quints, no cold patches, no stretches at all really, just eighteen holes of refusing to find the trouble that everyone else seemed magnetised to. He returned +14 gross, the lowest of the day by a margin, and that overnight one-shot Jacket lead has now been stretched into a six-shot cushion over Jon Baker. On a course designed to embarrass people, refusing to be embarrassed turns out to be quite the competitive edge.

**Jon Baker**, similarly, declines to feature in any blow-up beat — a club to which membership was, on this evidence, distressingly easy. He posted the second-best gross of the day and is the only man within touching distance of both leaders, second in the Trophy (eleven back) and second in the Jacket (six back). Steady, unflashy, present on both leaderboards: the Baker brand.

Which brings us, with some reluctance, to **Alex Baker**, who had what can only be described as a comprehensive afternoon. The trouble started early and went barely noticed at the time: three consecutive doubles at 6, 7 and 8 — 7 at the par-5 6th, 5 at the par-3 7th, 7 at the par-5 8th — for six dropped shots in an early wobble that looked, with the benefit of an hour, like a warning. He failed to heed it. Holes 11 to 14 produced four more straight doubles (6, 7, 6, 6) for another eight shots, and then came the closing sequence that will live in TEG folklore: quad-bogey 8 at the 16th, double at the 17th, quad-bogey 9 at the par-5 18th. Ten shots over the final three holes. He has fallen from outright third in the Trophy into a tie with Mullin, and — more painfully — from fifth to last place in the Jacket, propping up a field that includes a man who made three craters of his own.

That man being **Stuart Neumann**, who managed to spread the damage about a bit: a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 10th, a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 12th, a quintuple-bogey 9 at the 16th. Three separate disasters. And yet — the Penha Longa logic again — Neumann closed the Spoon gap, not because he found anything resembling form but because the man immediately above him was busy finding things rather worse.

That man being **Henry Meller**, whose 11 to 14 stretch — double, triple, triple, double — bled ten shots, and who then signed off the mountain with his own quintuple-bogey 9 at the 16th, which by that point was less a golf hole and more a holding pen for nines. Fifth on Stableford he remains, courtesy entirely of Neumann being, just about, marginally worse.

The shape of the day, then: Williams' Trophy lead nearly tripled despite a 10 on his card; Mullin's Jacket lead grew from one to six on the strength of the cleanest scorecard in the building; and at the bottom, the Spoon gap halved not through anyone's revival but through Meller joining the queue at the 16th. Alex Baker's collapse from fifth to last in the Jacket is the round's headline reshuffle — a one-round freefall of the kind the mountain seems to specialise in.
