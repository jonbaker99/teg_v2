# The Catalonia Coronation

## A double bogey, and a coronation

The sixth at the Stadium is a par four like any other par four, until you stand on the tee two shots behind your brother with a Trophy you have led since Thursday afternoon slipping out of your hands. Jon Baker stood there on the final day, made a six, walked off, and discovered he was level. Alex Baker, with the same hole in front of him, could not better it. The Trophy that had been Alex's overnight — that had been his since a clipped par on the 13th at El Prat the previous afternoon — was suddenly nobody's, and twelve holes later it would be Jon's by six.

This is the story of how a man who had been runner-up for the Green Jacket in three of the previous four TEGs finally walked off with both pots in the same tournament; of how his brother and defending Trophy champion mounted a perfectly judged Saturday raid only to bleed it back through three separate three-hole stretches on Sunday; and of how an eight-time Green Jacket winner produced personal bests on both PGA Catalunya layouts and still finished the tournament holding the Wooden Spoon. Three acts, two course records, one coronation, and a Norman-designed pivot in the middle.

## Round 1 — Ninety, and Daylight

Thursday at the Stadium, TEG's third visit across two trips to the place. The venue has a history; the field has a history with it; Jon Baker, in particular, had walked it five times before without ever breaking 99.

He shot 90. Eighteen over, thirty-five Stableford points, and a new course record by four shots over anything anyone had managed here in five previous attempts. Outright lead in both competitions before the rest of the field had finished its first cigarette: three clear on the Trophy, two clear on the Jacket. The perennial bridesmaid had not just turned up to the wedding — he had taken the cake.

Behind him, the day's bookkeeping was being done by John Patterson, the reigning Spoon holder, who set about defending his title with the air of a man who didn't realise he had a choice. A nine at the 4th. Then, across 12 to 14, eleven shots dropped to par in three holes: triple at the par-5 12th, a quintuple 9 at the 13th, a triple at the 14th. The 14th, specifically, was the hole that tipped him to the bottom of the Spoon table, where he would spend the next two-and-a-bit rounds.

Gregg Williams contributed his own opening movement — triple, triple, double across the first three for eight shots dropped before the legs had warmed — and then settled into the long campaign of being fourteen shots better here than the last time he visited without it noticeably mattering.

## Round 2 — Another Course, Another Record

Friday, on the Tour. Jon Baker shot 84. Twelve over par, forty-one Stableford points, and — for the second day running — a new course record, this one by five shots over anything anyone had done here in five previous TEG visits. The Jacket lead, which had been two, became three. The Trophy lead, already three, was no longer being seriously contested.

There is a particular kind of round that quietly removes the suspense from a tournament without anyone quite noticing. This was one of them.

The 15th, a par five, did the day's wider damage. Patterson opened an 11 there — a sextuple bogey, zero points, the kind of number that earns its own quiet pause on the scorecard. Williams went round in 10 at the same hole. Patterson then signed for 103: a Tour course-worst across six visits, beating the previous low watermark by two. His front-nine bleed across 3 to 5 — double, triple, triple — had set the tone before the 15th confirmed it.

After 36 holes Jon Baker led both competitions, had broken two course records inside thirty hours, and was being followed around by the strong suspicion that this time he might actually finish what he had started.

## Round 3 — The Brother's Coup

Saturday at El Prat, on Greg Norman's Azul. A one-off detour, no priors, no records to break or chase — only the round in front of you and whatever it does to the table. What it did was tilt it.

Alex Baker, the defending Trophy champion, had spent two rounds in respectable but unremarkable orbit. He arrived at El Prat and went to work. He drew level with Jon at the 1st with a bogey. Then, having spent the front nine staying in touch, he produced a par run across 12, 13 and 14 — a par-3, a par-4, another par-4 — for nine points and, with the par at the 13th, the outright Trophy lead. Overnight, he was two clear.

The Jacket story ran on its own clock. David Mullin — eight-time Green Jacket champion, the man whose name has been on this trophy more than anyone else's — drew level with Jon at the par-4 3rd with a routine par. At the par-3 6th, with the tournament momentarily balanced, Mullin made seven and Jon made bogey, and that was the Jacket back in outright Baker hands. From there Jon went par-par-birdie across 7 to 9 for ten points, then promptly handed seven shots back across 11 to 13 — double at the 11th, double at the 12th (which, irritatingly, is the easiest hole on the course), triple at the 13th. The lead held, three clear, but the comfort had gone.

And the Spoon changed hands. Mullin's quadruple at the 6th began the slide; his bogey at the 12th — that same give-away par-3 — finished it. By the close of play, the eight-time Jacket champion was last on Stableford. Patterson, meanwhile, who made his customary contribution with a nine at the 16th, had been steadied by a quiet par run across 8 to 10 and was no longer at the bottom of anything. The Spoon, like a hot potato, had been passed.

Overnight, then: Alex Baker leading the Trophy by two, Jon Baker leading the Jacket by three, David Mullin at the foot of the Stableford table. Three competitions, three different stories, one round to play.

## Round 4 — The Stadium, Closing

Sunday, back where it started. The Stadium had given Jon his Thursday record; it would now stage the closing argument.

Mullin opened par-par-par for nine points across 1 to 3, and at the par-5 3rd he was level with Jon on the Jacket. The eight-time champion was not going quietly. But this was as close as he would get. Jon held him off through the next fifteen holes, signed for 35 points and nineteen over, and the wire-to-wire Gross win — never wider than three, never narrower than level — was sealed.

The Trophy was decided a few holes later, and on a hole nobody would have chosen.

Alex Baker had already bled seven shots across 4 to 6 — double, double at the par-3 5th, triple at the 6th — when he watched Jon match the 6th with a double bogey of his own. It was not a heroic shot or a turning birdie. It was a six that Alex, having dropped three at the same hole, could not punish. Level on Stableford. From there, Alex did not so much lose the Trophy as systematically surrender it. Triple, triple, double across 12 to 14 for another eight shots gone. A quadruple at the 9th, the hardest hole on the course, for good measure. Then, in case any doubt remained, double, quadruple 8, double across 16 to 18 — another eight shots, with the closing 18th the second-hardest hole on the course doing its sober share. Twenty-seven Stableford points: his worst TEG round on record, posted on the day he most needed not to post one.

Counterfactually — and you can run the numbers — the Trophy would have remained Alex's by some margin had any one of those three three-hole stretches not happened. All three happened. The margin was six.

Mullin, for his part, had his own closing imitation: triple at the par-5 15th (the easiest hole), double at the 16th, double at the 17th — seven shots leaked at the last possible moment — for 31 points. Enough to confirm last place on Stableford and the Spoon. His 91 at the Stadium was a personal best at the venue. His 84 on the Tour the day before had been a personal best there too. Both PGA Catalunya layouts, course PBs, and last on Stableford. A clean illustration of the difference between hitting the ball and harvesting the points.

Patterson, having spent four rounds posting numbers that on any other week would have settled it, escaped the Spoon by virtue of Mullin having been worse where it mattered. Two doubles and a triple across 7 to 9, three shots leaked across 12 to 14, and an 11 in his back pocket from the Tour — and he was not the last man standing. The reigning Spoon holder had defended his crown, in the end, by handing it on.

## How the three were decided

**The Trophy.** Jon Baker led from the 7th hole of Round 1 to the 12th hole of Round 3 — a lead built on the 90 at the Stadium and the 84 on the Tour. Alex Baker drew level at the 1st at El Prat, took outright at the 13th with a par capping a 12–14 par run, and went to bed two clear. At the Stadium 6th on Sunday, Jon drew level with a double bogey Alex could not match. Sixteen-shot bleeds across two later stretches did the rest. Final margin: six.

**The Green Jacket.** Wire-to-wire, but never settled. Two strokes after Round 1, three after Round 2, level briefly at the El Prat 3rd, retaken outright at the El Prat 6th, level briefly again at the Stadium 3rd on Sunday, and clear by three at +70 when the cards came in. Two course records inside thirty-six hours turned out to be exactly enough.

**The Wooden Spoon.** Patterson held the bottom from the 14th of Round 1 through the end of Round 2. The 6th and 12th at El Prat passed it to Mullin. The 15th to 17th at the Stadium on Sunday made sure it stayed there. The eight-time Jacket champion finishes the tournament on 125 Stableford points, holding a wooden spoon.

## The men, in brief

- **Jon Baker.** Two course records in thirty hours and the Jacket by three: the bridesmaid finally gets to keep the dress. The Trophy by six on top, for the double. Three near-misses on the Gross, and then this — a tournament that he led from the seventh hole of the opening round and never really let go, even when it briefly looked as though he had.
- **Alex Baker.** The defence had everything going for it through Saturday evening. He drew level at the El Prat 1st, took outright at the 13th, slept on a two-shot lead. Then 27 points on Sunday — his worst TEG round on record — across three separate three-hole bleeds and a quadruple at the hardest hole on the course. The brother's coup, undone by the brother himself.
- **David Mullin.** Personal bests on both PGA Catalunya layouts. Runner-up for the Jacket he has won eight times. Last on Stableford for a second career Spoon. There is a way of playing well enough to come second on Gross and last on net at the same tournament, and Mullin has just demonstrated it.
- **John Patterson.** A 9 at the Stadium 4th, a 9 at the Stadium 13th, an 11 at the Tour 15th, a 9 at the El Prat 16th, a 103 that is now the Tour course-worst, and somehow not the Spoon. The reigning champion saved by a three-hole par run at El Prat, which is the sort of sentence that only makes sense within TEG.
- **Gregg Williams.** Fourteen shots better at the Stadium than his last visit here, which on paper is progress and in practice was an 8 at the par-3 5th in Round 1, a 10 at the Tour 15th in Round 2, and eleven shots leaked across 8 to 11 on Sunday. The blow-ups kept coming. The improvement, somewhere underneath them, was real.
