# Coronation in Catalonia

On the first afternoon of the week, on the first course of the week, Jon Baker walked off the eighteenth green at PGA Catalunya Stadium having signed for 90. A course record. The previous mark of 94 had stood through five prior TEG visits without anybody laying a glove on it; Baker shaved four off it on a routing he had never seen sober before lunch. He had also, in TEG 6, gone round the same loop in 99.

This is the moment to which everything else in the week is, in retrospect, footnote. Baker arrived in Catalonia as the great Jacket nearly-man — runner-up in three of the last four TEGs, never the bride — and within a single round had put both the Trophy and the Jacket leads on his card and made the rest of the field look like minor characters in someone else's story. The week would still take four days to play. It had been decided in one.

What follows is the writing of it.

## Round 1 — Baker Breaks the Stadium

The Stadium course — Gallardo and Coles, generally spoken of as the best card in Spain — had spent five visits patiently rebuffing all comers. It blinked. Baker's 90 was good for 35 Stableford points and, after a routine bit of early jockeying through holes 3–7 between himself, David Mullin and John Patterson, both leads were his by the close. Trophy by three. Jacket by two.

The wreckage was, as is customary, supplied by Patterson. A quintuple at the par-4 4th. An 8 at the 10th. A second quintuple-bogey 9 at the 13th. Another 8 at the 17th. The truly malign passage came across 12–14, where he shipped eleven shots to par in three holes — triple, quintuple, triple — and dropped to the foot of the Spoon race on the 14th with one of them. Where else.

Gregg Williams opened triple, triple, double for eight dropped through three, then garnished the par-3 5th with a quintuple-bogey 8, settling in to what would become the week's defining personal motif.

## Round 2 — Eighty-Four, and a Side of 103

Onto the Tour course, the Stadium's slightly more forgiving sibling, where Baker promptly broke the second of the venue's two long-standing records. An 84. The previous mark, 89, had also stood across five visits; Baker took five off it and posted a 6-shot personal best over his own TEG 6 round on the same ground. Forty-one Stableford points, the round of the week to that point, with a par-par-par run at 9–11 worth nine of them on its own. Both leads stretched. The chasing pack, such as it was, looked queasy.

At the other end of the same scorecard pile, Patterson shot 103, a new course-worst at the Tour by two — the previous record-low ceiling, also of long standing, demolished from below in the same afternoon as the record above. A sextuple-bogey 11 at the par-5 15th was the headline; eight dropped across 3–5 (double, triple, triple) was the support act. The Tour course had waited five TEG visits for someone to find both edges of the envelope. It got both on the same day, in the same fourball.

Williams matched Patterson at the 15th with a quintuple of his own, then — and this is true — birdied the next-but-one. Mullin, meanwhile, was doing perfectly respectable work: an 85 for a 4-shot Tour PB over TEG 6, par-par-birdie at 8–10 for ten points. It is the sort of round that wins a Jacket in most weeks. Not this one. Alex Baker carried a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 12th into the clubhouse and watched the leaderboard from a polite distance.

## Round 3 — The King Falls

El Prat Azul — a Greg Norman card, new to TEG, the one-off pivot of the week — declined to follow the script.

Alex Baker, who had been keeping his powder dry, drew level with his namesake on the opening hole with a bogey, then took outright Trophy possession at the 13th with a par. A par-par-par stretch through 12–14 was worth nine points; Jon Baker, simultaneously, was posting his weakest round of the trip at 32 and bleeding seven shots over 11–13 (double, double, triple). The Trophy lead at the close belonged to Alex by two. Briefly, the bridesmaid story looked as though it might want another chapter of waiting.

The Jacket, though, held its shape. Mullin drew level on the third with a par. On the par-3 6th, Jon Baker bogeyed and Mullin failed to, and that — although nobody knew it yet — was the moment the Jacket was effectively settled. Baker followed with par-par-birdie at 7–9 for ten more. Mullin would draw level once more, fleetingly, on the third hole of the final round, and that would be that.

The real news of the afternoon was at the bottom of the page. Mullin — eight-time Jacket champion, back-to-back going in, the closest thing TEG has to a reigning monarch in any of its three competitions — ran up a quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 6th. Then, on the 12th, a par-3 marked SI 18, the easiest hole on the course, he made a bogey. It is the most banal possible way to fall to last, and it dropped him there for the first time all week. He would not climb off the bottom again. Patterson, who had been holding the basement faithfully through 36 holes and added a quintuple-bogey 9 at the 16th here for good measure, was, by the only mechanism available to him, spared.

Williams ran up a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 5th. The week's rhythm section, undisturbed.

## Round 4 — Baker, Twice Over

Back at the Stadium. Mullin opened par-par-par for nine and drew level with Baker for the Jacket lead at the third. The defending champion, for an hour, looked like the defending champion.

Then came the par-4 6th, where Jon Baker made a double bogey. This is the strange grammar of the week: he dropped two shots to par, and in doing so drew level with Alex Baker for the Trophy lead — a fact more revealing of Alex's afternoon than Jon's. From that hole onward, Jon Baker did not trail in either competition. He did, later, run up a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 13th, the kind of hole that in a different week is a haunting. Here it was a footnote. He posted 35 points and walked off with both trophies.

Alex Baker collapsed. Twenty-seven points, his worst TEG round on record. The damage came in slabs: seven dropped at 4–6 (double, double, triple); a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 9th, the hardest hole on the course; eight shots gone over 12–14 (triple, triple, double); and a closing eight-shot bleed at 16–18, the centrepiece a quadruple at the 17th, the coup de grâce a double at the par-4 18th, one of the hardest holes on the course. He had opened the front door, waved the burglar in, and offered him tea.

Mullin shot 91 — a PB at the Stadium over his TEG 6 94, the consolation a man takes home from a Spoon week — but inside it bled seven shots across 15–17. The triple at the 15th was the one to file away: a par-5 marked SI 18, the easiest hole on the course. Thirty-one points. Last. Done.

Williams, true to form, shipped eleven across 8–11, the heart of it a quintuple-bogey 9 at the 10th, and signed for 99 — fourteen shots better than his TEG 6 visit to the same course, which is the sort of statistic only TEG would record but is, on inspection, the largest course-history swing of the week by anybody. A small, private coronation.

## How it was decided

**Trophy.** Led from R1 to halfway through R3 by Jon Baker; held briefly by Alex Baker from the 13th of R3 through to the 5th of R4; reclaimed level on R4's 6th by Jon and never relinquished. Final margin: six points, on 143. The decisive moment was a Jon Baker double bogey, which is not the sentence anyone expected to write.

**Green Jacket.** Led from the third hole of the tournament to the last by Jon Baker, save for two brief level-pegs by Mullin. The moment was R3's 6th — a bogey on a par-3 on a course Baker had never seen, on a day he was otherwise quietly shedding shots — that gave him outright control he never gave back. He won by three at +70. The foundation, as everyone present will remember without prompting, was 90 and 84.

**Wooden Spoon.** Patterson held the bottom for 36 holes in the customary manner and posted a new Tour course-worst at 103 for the trouble. Mullin took it from him on the par-3 12th of R3 with a bogey at the easiest hole on the course, and confirmed it in R4 with a 7-shot stretch through 15–17 that included a triple at another SI 18. The reigning Jacket king walks home with 125 points and the Spoon. There is no countback in TEG, and even if there were, none was required.

## The men, in brief

- **Jon Baker** — The bridesmaid arrives at the chapel, takes the Trophy and the Jacket, breaks both course records at PGA Catalunya in 24 hours, and is, by lunchtime on day one, no longer interestingly nearly anything. Two near-misses made good, with a third thrown in for ballast.
- **David Mullin** — Eight-time Jacket champion, back-to-back going in, runner-up in the Jacket by three and last on the Trophy. A Stadium PB of 91 is the sort of mantelpiece consolation that doesn't really go with a Spoon. He took both home.
- **Alex Baker** — Defending Trophy champion, holder of the lead going into the final round, and author of a 27-point closing day — his worst on record — in which seven shots went at 4–6, eight at 12–14, a quad at the hardest hole on the course, and eight more at 16–18. Unlocked the door, waved the burglar in.
- **John Patterson** — Reigning Spoon holder. Did everything required of the role for 36 holes, including a new course-worst 103 at the Tour, then was spared by Mullin's R3 of all things. A quintuple-bogey 9 at the 16th of R3 suggests no hard feelings about the reprieve.
- **Gregg Williams** — Three-time Trophy winner, kept the family quintuple-per-round tradition alive (5th, 15th, 10th), and somehow, almost privately, posted a 99 at the Stadium — fourteen shots better than his last visit there, the biggest swing of the week by anybody. A man can improve in spite of himself.
