# Carnage on the Estoril Coast

There are days when a new course politely introduces itself, shakes hands, and offers a coffee. And there are days like this one, when Robert Trent Jones Sr.'s Quinta da Marinha — TEG's first crack at the Estoril Coast pines — opened the front door, smiled, and started swinging a length of lead piping. By sundown, **five of the six players had posted a quintuple bogey or worse**, somebody had carded a 10, somebody else had carded three nines in the space of nine holes, and the collective scorecard looked less like a round of golf than a casualty list translated from the Portuguese.

Out of this wreckage, improbably composed, walked Gregg Williams. **38 points. Four clear. Job done.**

Williams's round was not flawless — nothing was flawless today, and anything that looked flawless was lying — but it was the only card on the property that knew where it was going. The pivot was a four-hole burst from the 8th: a bogey at the par-4 8th, a par at the par-3 9th, a **birdie at the par-5 10th** and a bogey at the par-5 11th. Fourteen points in four holes, and the birdie at the 10th was the moment he took the outright Trophy lead and quietly refused to surrender it. He then did what everyone did at Quinta da Marinha — he bled. Doubles at the 12th, 13th and the par-3 14th, then a **quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 15th** dropped ten shots in four holes. On any other day this would be a calamity. Today it barely dented the lead.

Behind him, and quietly the round's second-best player on both sheets, was **Jon Baker on 34**. He drew level for the gross lead with a bogey at the par-5 6th and pottered along usefully until the par-4 13th, where a **quintuple-bogey 9** detonated under his card. To his credit he didn't sulk: bogey at the 15th, double at the par-3 16th, and then — splendidly, almost defiantly — a **birdie at the par-4 17th** to stop the rot before anything worse could happen.

**David Mullin** played the role of accountant-in-chief, posting 30 points but, more importantly, the day's best gross card at **20 over**. There was a three-hole wobble from the 6th — double bogeys at the par-5 6th, the par-3 7th and the par-4 8th, six shots gone in a sitting — but he recovered to draw level for the Jacket lead with a bogey at the 10th and then nudged ahead outright with a **par at the par-4 13th**, the kind of mundane scoring achievement that looked, in context, like a small miracle.

Which brings us, with a sigh, to **Alex Baker**. For five holes he was the leader-in-waiting and possibly the leader full stop: **five straight bogeys, 15 points, holes 1 through 5**. He looked utterly in command of the place. Then Quinta da Marinha read his card, raised an eyebrow, and went to work. A **quadruple-bogey 8 at the 8th**, a triple at the par-3 9th, a **quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 10th** — eleven shots gone in three holes. The closing stretch, when it came, was the kind of thing you cover children's eyes for: double at the 15th, triple at the par-3 16th, **quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 17th**, quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 18th. Fourteen shots shed in four holes. Final tally: 30 points with a **20/10 front-back split** that reads less like a round of golf than a stock-market crash.

**Henry Meller (29 pts)** had the dubious honour of setting the day's tone with the very first piece of carnage — a **quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 3rd** that briefly dropped him to the bottom of the Wooden Spoon race and served, in retrospect, as Quinta da Marinha's opening statement of intent. He answered handsomely with a **birdie at the par-3 5th**, the round's clearest little flash of defiance, and was holding things together passably until the 13th, where doubles at the 13th, the par-3 14th and the 15th leaked six shots and ended the contest.

And then, alas, **Stuart Neumann**. He inherited the Wooden Spoon from Meller at the par-4 4th with a triple bogey and never gave it back — though "never gave it back" undersells what happened next. From the par-3 7th to the 15th, over nine consecutive holes, Neumann **dropped 29 shots**. Three quintuple bogeys in the stretch. Double at the 7th, double at the 8th, triple at the par-3 9th, **quadruple-bogey 9** at the par-5 10th, **quadruple-bogey 9** at the par-5 11th, **quintuple-bogey 9** at the par-4 12th, double at the 13th, **quintuple-bogey 8** at the par-3 14th, double at the 15th. Final card: **17 points, 43 over gross — his worst TEG round to date**, and not by a small amount.

The Trophy lead changed hands precisely once today — at Williams's birdie on the 10th — and then sat tight. The Green Jacket, by contrast, was a hot potato: Jon Baker drew level at the 6th, Mullin drew level at the 10th, Williams drew level at the 11th, before Mullin's par at the 13th finally claimed it outright. The Spoon flipped from Meller to Neumann at the 4th and then, courtesy of that nine-hole bleed, opened into a chasm.
