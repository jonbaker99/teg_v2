# Patterson's Portuguese Procession

There are tournaments decided by attrition, tournaments decided by collapse, and tournaments decided in a single, unhinged afternoon by a man who has apparently decided he is now a different golfer. TEG 9, on its third pilgrimage to the Lisbon Coast, was emphatically the last sort. John PATTERSON arrived at Praia D'El Rey on the Saturday one point off the Trophy lead and a single ill-judged tee shot away from being remembered for a 10 at the 4th the day before. He left it ten clear, having posted 49 Stableford points — the second-best round in TEG history — and the rest of the week was paperwork.

It was a procession in stereo. While Patterson was rewriting the Trophy record book, David MULLIN was treating the Green Jacket as a leisurely stroll, having grabbed the gross lead on the 18th green of Round 1 and never thinking to give it back. And tucked away beneath all the bunting, Stuart NEUMANN had already sealed the Wooden Spoon by Friday tea-time, courtesy of a back nine at Seve's place that bled shots like a wounded matador. Three trophies, effectively settled inside the first 36 holes. The remaining 36 were for the scrapbook.

## Round 1 — Seve's Last Design Spits Out Five Different Leaders

Royal Óbidos, Seve Ballesteros's final design, is a course of theatrical water features, manicured menace and — on this evidence — chronic indecision about who should be leading the golf tournament being played on it. The Trophy lead changed hands at five of the first six holes. Jon BAKER drew level at the 2nd. MULLIN tied things up at the 3rd. Gregg WILLIAMS, gloriously, levelled at the 4th with a *double bogey* — the only time all week his name will appear next to the words "lead" without an exclamation mark of disbelief. PATTERSON nipped in front outright at the 5th. BAKER drew level again at the 6th. By then nobody on the leaderboard knew which way was up, and the leaderboard itself looked like it needed a lie down.

The damage being done elsewhere was considerable. PATTERSON ran up a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 4th — the sort of number that ought to end a tournament before it begins, and which somehow didn't. Alex BAKER went one worse with an eleven at the 14th, a septuple bogey of such bracing commitment it deserves its own plaque. WILLIAMS started with a quintuple at the 1st and added a nine at the par-3 15th for variety. PATTERSON himself dropped a quad at that same 15th.

Through it all, MULLIN simply played golf. He seized the gross lead outright at the 3rd, briefly conceded level status to Jon BAKER at the 6th, then — after a wobble that included a double at the 16th — calmly parred the par-5 18th to reclaim the Jacket outright as the sun went down. He would not let it go again.

Jon BAKER ended the day a point clear of PATTERSON on Stableford, despite a closing quadruple at the last. And at the bottom, the Spoon's quiet little plotline was already resolving itself. NEUMANN had dropped to last at the 11th, briefly clambered off the floor, then surrendered for good at the par-3 15th with a quintuple-bogey 8. From the 14th to the 18th he conceded sixteen shots in five holes — 23 points for the round, and a hole already dug too deep to climb out of. Nobody noticed at the time. Nobody needed to.

## Round 2 — Forty-Nine Shades of Patterson

Praia D'El Rey, all coastal dunes on the back nine and links-meets-parkland charm out the front, is a course designed to reward bold, clean ball-striking. PATTERSON arrived on Saturday and gave it precisely that. He bogeyed the 1st — three points, lead snatched outright from Jon BAKER. MULLIN parred the par-5 2nd to draw level. Game on, briefly.

Then this happened: bogey, double, triple, triple. PATTERSON haemorrhaged eight shots across holes 4 to 6, dropped to one over level after his earlier coup, and looked for all the world like a man whose Friday 10 was about to be joined by Saturday companions. A birdie at the 7th stopped the bleeding. And then, as if a switch had been thrown somewhere inside him, he simply stopped making mistakes.

From the 9th to the 13th: bogey, par, par, birdie, par. Seventeen points in five holes. From the 15th to the 18th, with Praia's coastal-dune theatre in full cry: par, bogey, par, bogey. Fourteen more. A 49-point round — the second-best in TEG history — built not on miraculous scoring but on the relentless absence of the bad number after the bad number had already been and gone. Jon BAKER, who had been quietly compiling his own steady card, ran into a quadruple at the par-3 14th and never quite picked himself up. Alex BAKER, to his enormous credit, posted a personal-best 44 of his own — and lost ten shots to Patterson anyway.

By close of play the Trophy gap was ten. MULLIN, with his customary +14 to par, stretched the Jacket lead to seven. The tournament, it must be said, was over. Everybody just had to keep playing it.

## Round 3 — Back to Royal Óbidos, Back to Earth

Seve's water-feature theatre had its revenge on Sunday, as it tends to. Patterson's 49 was followed by a much more mortal 34, the sort of round in which double-bogey 8s at the 12th and quad-bogey 9s at the 18th are processed as the cost of doing business when you're ten clear. The lead came down from ten to three. It was, briefly, possible to imagine a contest.

Jon BAKER did his best to provide one. Sixteen points in the first five holes — par, birdie, par, bogey, par — and suddenly the runner-up's engine was turning over. It promptly stalled. A triple-double-quad sequence through 10-11-12 put eight shots back on his card and the front-nine charge in the bin. He finished with 34 and, ten points further back, watched MULLIN survive what should by rights have been a disaster of his own: a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 10th. The Jacket leader simply absorbed it, played the rest in his usual orderly fashion, and walked off with the gross lead still seven.

NEUMANN, meanwhile, posted his best round of the week with 37 — including a 12-point burst through 10–13 — and remained, inevitably, six points behind everybody else. Some holes can be dug out of. The 14th-to-18th hole at Royal Óbidos on Friday was not one of them.

## Round 4 — Alex Briefly Levels, Then Alex Being Alex

The closing afternoon at Praia D'El Rey produced, for about four holes, the contest the week had refused to provide. Alex BAKER, who spent the tournament alternating personal-best rounds with calamities of operatic scale, arrived at the par-3 14th two shy of Patterson on Stableford. A double bogey there — five shots, two points — somehow drew him level. Patterson, having recovered from a quintuple at the 5th, had been generous enough to provide the opening. For a moment, with five to play, this thing was on.

Then, Alex being Alex: quadruple at the 15th, triple at the 16th, double at the 17th. Eleven shots dropped in four holes. The fleeting share of the lead vanished into the dunes, and PATTERSON, ever the metronome when it counted, sailed home with a 47 to win the Trophy by six, finishing on 168 — the highest total in TEG history.

Alex still posted a personal-best 44 (his second of the week — a tournament-in-microcosm if ever there was one) and took the Trophy runner-up spot on raw points. Jon BAKER, who started the day chasing MULLIN for the Jacket, ran up a quad at the 17th and conceded the gross by ten. MULLIN himself opened with a quadruple-bogey 8 at the 1st, calmly parred 7-8-9 in succession, and won the Jacket at +67, by ten, in a manner so unfussy that you almost forgot he'd been first across the line in every single round.

WILLIAMS, true to form, found a new flavour of sextuple — an 11 at the par-5 7th — to round out his collection (9 at the 15th in R1, 10 at the 9th in R2, 11 at the 7th in R4: a full set). And NEUMANN, after a week of being last from R1 H11 onwards, summoned one final flourish: 16 points across holes 11 to 15, par-par-birdie-par-bogey, the closing minor key on his 127-point Spoon. The Spoon itself had been settled days earlier, but it was nice to be reminded he could still play.

A procession, then, in three lanes: PATTERSON sprinting, MULLIN strolling, NEUMANN bringing up the rear with quiet dignity. Seve's water and Praia's dunes provided the scenery. The story, as ever, belonged to whoever turned up on Saturday afternoon and refused to make a bogey.
