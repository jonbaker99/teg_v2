# Patterson's 49: the day the Trophy race broke open {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 9 | Round 2 | 01/10/2016 | Praia D'El Rey</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> JP 49 | AB 44 | DM 39 | JB 37 | GW 36 | SN 30</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> DM +14 | JP +14 | JB +19 | AB +27 | GW +28 | SN +32</p>

Forty-nine Stableford points. The second-best round in TEG history. A one-shot deficit transmuted, over the course of a single afternoon on the Lisbon Coast, into a ten-point Trophy lead. That is the headline, and it is the sort of headline that ought to arrive with trumpets, bunting and a small civic reception. The trouble is that for the first six holes of John Patterson's Round 2 at Praia D'El Rey, there was no earthly indication that any of it was coming. None. Nada. The man was six over par through six, bleeding shots like a leaky radiator, and the round of his life was, at that point, indistinguishable from the nightmare version.

Let us linger over the horror, because it is important. Bogey, bogey, bogey — a sluggish but recognisable Patterson opening. Then a double at the 4th. Then — and this is where the wheels properly came off — back-to-back triple bogeys at the 5th and 6th. Eight shots dropped across three holes. The only consolation, if it can be called that, was that he had nicked the Trophy lead on the 1st via the dark arts of countback, only to hand it back on the 2nd when David Mullin parred the par-5 to draw level. Which is to say: Patterson was leading, then co-leading, then haemorrhaging, and the leaderboard at this stage looked exactly like everyone had expected it to look like at day's end. It just happened to be 12 holes early.

The hinge was the 7th. A birdie at the par-5 — the first time all morning the scorecard did something other than spit at him — and from that moment forward, Praia D'El Rey became Patterson's personal property. The 9th through the 13th yielded 17 points: bogey, par, par, birdie at the par-5 12th, par. The par at the par-3 11th drew him level with Mullin at the top of the Trophy chase for the second instance in the day, and this time he had no intention of giving the share back. The closing stretch from the 15th — par-bogey-par-bogey — was worth another 14 points, and that was that. Gross 14-over, net 49. The dunes of the back nine, which had been laid out as a kind of dramatic backdrop, ended up as merely the scenery for a coronation.

Mullin, meanwhile, did exactly what a Green Jacket leader is supposed to do, which is to say not very much, very competently. His own round had a wobble in the middle — four straight bogeys at the 6th, 7th, 8th and 9th, the sort of run that on another day might have got interesting — but a birdie at the par-5 10th plugged the leak, and from there he plodded home with the unhurried air of a man who knows the gross numbers are doing the work for him. The par at the 2nd had given him that fleeting share of the Trophy lead; the remainder of the day was tidy enough to push his Jacket margin over Jon Baker from two to seven. A quiet, professional, faintly boring afternoon, which on this evidence is the way he likes it.

Jon Baker, alas, had no such afternoon. He arrived as Trophy leader and departed ten points adrift, which is the sort of round that requires a strong drink and a long shower. The damage began early — double, double, triple at the 5th, 6th and 7th, seven shots gone before anyone had properly settled — and the middle suggested at least the bones of a regroup: three straight pars at 11, 12 and 13, a man digging in, a leader holding firm. Then came the 14th, a par-three. Jon made a 7. Quadruple bogey. And in the sort of synchronised swimming that only the Baker family can really pull off, Alex made the same 7 on the same hole. A birdie at the par-5 17th was a token, no more.

Which brings us, with some delicacy, to Alex Baker, whose 44 points would, on literally any other day in TEG history, be the lead item on the news. A personal best, no less. Built — and this is the strange thing — on a card that included two quadruple bogeys: a 9 at the par-5 10th and the matching 7 at the 14th. The engine room was holes 7-9, a 10-point burst of bogey-bogey-par that did most of the heavy lifting; between the quads, holes 10-12 cost him eight shots in three, and still he came home with 44. On the JP-free version of this scorecard, Alex is the story. He is not the story.

Gregg Williams's round was, with a single exception, the kind of cameo that defies easy retelling. The exception was a 10 at the par-4 9th. A sextuple bogey. The most lurid single number of the day in a competition with no shortage of contenders for that title, and a useful reminder that Praia D'El Rey will, if asked nicely, hand out double figures to anyone who fancies one.

And then Stuart Neumann, who is now bottom of the Spoon by fourteen, and who got there the hard way. A quad-bogey 8 at the par-4 5th. Another quad-bogey 8 at the par-4 9th. And — perhaps the round's most committed piece of work — three straight triple bogeys at 11, 12 and 13, nine shots gone in the space of half an hour. 53 points all in. Last by fourteen. The Spoon is not loosening its grip; it is tightening it.

The Trophy chase, which began the day separated by a single shot, ends it separated by ten. Jon Baker has gone from outright leader to a tie for third with Alex on 76; Mullin sits second on 77; Patterson is gone, up the road, alone. Mullin's Jacket margin has tripled-and-a-bit from two to seven, with Patterson keeping hold of third despite that 14-over gross. And at the bottom, the Neumann–Williams gap has near-doubled from eight to fourteen, a chasm now rather than a deficit.
