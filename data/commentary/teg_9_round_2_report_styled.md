# Patterson's Portuguese Resurrection {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 9 | Round 2 | 01/10/2016 | Praia D'El Rey</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> JP 49 | AB 44 | DM 39 | JB 37 | GW 36 | SN 30</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> DM +14 | JP +14 | JB +19 | AB +27 | GW +28 | SN +32</p>

Picture the scene at the 6th green at Praia D'El Rey. John Patterson has just signed for back-to-back triple-bogey sevens on a pair of par fours, three holes after a double at the 4th, six holes after opening with three sodden bogeys. Eight shots have evaporated in the space of three holes. He has the haunted look of a man composing his concession speech and wondering whether the airport bar opens early. The Trophy, briefly his at the 1st and surrendered when David Mullin drew level at the 2nd, has now wandered off to find more reliable company. By any reasonable reading he is cooked.

By sundown the scoreboard alongside his name reads **49 points** — the second-best round in TEG history. The one-point overnight deficit has been converted, in the space of twelve dune-blown holes, into a ten-point Trophy lead. This is the round that has to be explained, because nothing else about it makes sense.

The pivot came, modestly enough, at the par-5 7th. After the carnage of 4-6, a birdie there did nothing more glamorous than stop the bleeding — but stopping the hemorrhage turned out to be the entire point. From the 9th onwards Patterson simply ran the table. A bogey at the par-4 9th to get going; then pars at the par-5 10th, the par-3 11th and the par-4 13th, with a birdie at the par-5 12th sandwiched in for company — seventeen points across five holes, and at the 11th he had drawn level for the Trophy once again. The closing stretch took the result beyond reasonable argument: par, bogey, par, bogey through 15-18 for fourteen more points, the kind of unhurried, error-free golf that suggests a man who has remembered, mid-round, that he is rather good at this. The 49 was assembled from the wreckage of those triples at the 5th and 6th, which is what makes it the round of the trip so far, and arguably the round of any trip so far.

David Mullin had his own near-tragedy hidden inside what is, on paper, a perfectly respectable defence of the Green Jacket. Having drawn level for the Trophy with a par at the 2nd, he settled in to do the steady, low-handicap things that have kept him at the top of the Jacket race — and then promptly went bogey-bogey-bogey-bogey through the par-4 6th, par-5 7th, par-3 8th and par-4 9th. Four shots in four holes is the sort of quiet bleed that, in a less spectacular round elsewhere on the course, would have been the day's headline. As it was, a birdie at the par-5 10th calmed everything down and the rest of the afternoon did the job. Mullin's two-shot Jacket cushion over Jon Baker is now seven; the lead held, and meaningfully so.

Jon Baker began the day as Trophy leader, which feels almost quaint now. His own collapse came earlier than Patterson's and was less spectacularly recovered: double, double, triple across the par-4 5th, par-4 6th and par-5 7th — seven shots, gone. From there he composed himself with admirable discipline, parring the par-3 11th, the par-5 12th and the par-4 13th in succession for nine unobtrusive points, looking for all the world like the Trophy leader serenely steadying the ship. And then the par-3 14th — that exposed, wind-bothered short hole in the dunes — bit him for a quadruple-bogey seven, and the steady run was over. Bogeys at 15 and 16 and a birdie at the par-5 17th tidied the card, but the damage was done at both ends of the leaderboard: joint third in the Trophy, ground ceded in the Jacket.

Alex Baker's afternoon was the round's other oddity and a quiet personal triumph. Forty-four points — a personal best — is a fine number on any day; forty-four points containing **two** quadruple bogeys is borderline alchemy. The first came at the par-5 10th (a nine), the start of an eight-shot bleed across 10-12 (quad, double on the par-3 11th, double on the par-5 12th). The second was the matching quadruple-bogey seven at the par-3 14th, where, by some cruel scheduling, both Bakers found the same hole on the same afternoon to be precisely four shots too difficult. What rescued the card was a ten-point burst across 7-9 — bogey, bogey, par — that gave the round a floor when none of the rest of it seemed willing to. Into the Trophy chasing pack he climbs.

Gregg Williams will not enjoy the highlights reel: a sextuple-bogey **ten** at the par-4 9th is the kind of single-hole entry that organises the rest of the round around it whether you like it or not. A quiet afternoon, in the technical sense that everyone was busy looking elsewhere; further off the pace in both the Trophy and the Jacket.

And Stuart Neumann, anchored to the bottom of the Spoon race, did very little to lighten the load. Quadruple-bogey eights at the par-4 5th and the par-4 9th set the tone; the back-nine middle — triple, triple, triple at the par-3 11th, par-5 12th and par-4 13th, for nine shots dropped in three holes — sealed it. Six, eight, seven, in that order, on a stretch where most of the field was scoring at least respectably.

The Trophy race did not so much shift this round as somersault. Jon Baker began the day a point clear and ended it tied for third; Patterson's overnight deficit of one became a lead of ten, with Mullin and the two Bakers bunched behind him. In the Jacket, Mullin's two-shot cushion has stretched to seven on the back of Jon Baker's 14th-hole quadruple. And the Spoon race, already looking settled, is now becoming a procession — Neumann's deficit has doubled, from eight to fourteen.
