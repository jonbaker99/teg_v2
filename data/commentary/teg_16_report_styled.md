There are tournaments that turn on a single putt, and there are tournaments which, by lunchtime on the opening day, have quietly written their own endings and asked only that everyone keep walking for the next seventy-odd holes. TEG 16 — the fifth visit to the Lisbon Coast, four rounds across four very different courses — was emphatically the second sort.

Consider the leaderboards after Round 1 at Oitavos Dunes. Stuart Neumann led the Trophy. Gregg Williams led the Jacket. Alex Baker propped up both. Now consider the standings after Round 4 at Estoril, seventy-two holes and three Atlantic time zones of weather later. Stuart Neumann had won the Trophy. Gregg Williams had won the Jacket. Alex Baker had won — if that is the word — the Wooden Spoon. The identical three names at the extremities, undisturbed, as if the field had collectively agreed not to bother the principals.

What follows is the story of how four rounds at Oitavos, Troia, Penha Longa and Estoril, on courses by Arthur Hills, Robert Trent Jones Sr., Robert Trent Jones Jr. and Mackenzie Ross respectively, managed to change absolutely nothing at the top and the bottom of three competitions — and the considerable mess made in the middle while everyone else got on with the actual golf.

## The Trophy: Neumann, from gun to tape

Stuart Neumann's opening salvo at Oitavos was not the kind of thing that makes for great television. Bogey at the 1st, par at the 2nd, bogey at the short 3rd: nine Stableford points in three holes, a polite cough of competence on a links course that demanded little else. He finished the round on 39, five clear. Nobody quite registered, at that point, that the tournament was over.

Troia, the only new course of the week and TEG's belated introduction to RTJ Sr.'s championship layout, was the one chance for a fresh narrative. Stuart, dutifully, provided wobbles. A quadruple at the 16th. A four-hole purple patch — eleven points across the 10th, 11th and 12th, the birdie-par-par run that ought to have settled matters — ended at the 13th with a triple-bogey 7. He still posted 35. The Trophy lead, instead of shrinking, grew to seven.

Penha Longa, RTJ Jr.'s mountain parkland in the Sintra hills and the round where the field generally came apart, extracted two quads from him, at the 1st and the 14th, both par-4s, both 8s. He posted 39 anyway. The lead jumped to twelve. The competition, in any meaningful sense, stopped existing.

Estoril, then, was a coronation. Stuart's 43 was his best round of the week, built on a nine-point burst across holes 4 to 6 — bogey, par, bogey, on the two par-3s and the par-5 between them. There remained, even at this late stage, the obligatory blemish: a nine-shot bleed across the 7th, 8th and 9th, the centrepiece a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 8th. The margin, in the end, was 13 points over Gregg Williams. 156 plays 143. Wire to wire, never headed after the second hole of Round 1, the advantage growing in every round: 5, 7, 12, 13.

## The Green Jacket: Williams, even more so

If Stuart's Trophy was a procession, Gregg Williams's Green Jacket was a forced march in which the rest of the field declined to keep up. He led on gross from the first hole of Oitavos to the last hole of Estoril, a span of seventy-two consecutive holes at the head of the leaderboard. The three "lead changes" the data records are not lead changes at all but tied-leader moments, brief and never outright: David Mullin drawing level at Oitavos's 6th, Jon Baker drawing level at Troia's 11th and again at the 14th. None of them ever inched ahead. The round-by-round gaps — 3, 4, 14, 16 — tell their own story.

Penha Longa was where he buried it. The day began messily enough, with a triple-bogey 7 at the par-4 3rd and a double at the 4th, the kind of opening that, in another player's hands, becomes a 90. A birdie at the par-3 5th stopped the bleeding, and from the 13th he ran off three straight pars — par-4, par-4, par-3 — for nine points, the day's most adult piece of golf on a course that was busy extracting 8s, 9s and 10s from everyone else. The lead leapt from 4 to 14.

Estoril, by then, was for the cameras. Gregg's 11 over was his best round of the week, assembled on a diet of pars that bordered on the impolite: nine points across holes 2 to 4, then another nine across holes 6 to 8, two three-hole par parades on a parkland course that had been chewing up the rest of the field. The single quad at the par-4 14th, an 8, was the only blemish anyone could find. Final margin: 16 strokes. Final total: +66 gross. The fifth-best gross round-aggregate in TEG history. That David Mullin, who finished a distant second at +82, also finished runner-up in the Trophy to Gregg's runner-up self, is one of those statistical curiosities the Lisbon week threw up — Gregg leading one competition and second in the other, Mullin second in one and second in the other — and is nothing more sinister than handicaps doing their ordinary work.

## The Wooden Spoon: Baker, auditioning

Alex Baker's week began at the par-4 1st at Oitavos with a quintuple-bogey 9. In retrospect, the entire Wooden Spoon race was decided in roughly seven minutes on the opening hole of the tournament, before most of the field had finished their first coffee. He spent the rest of Round 1 in conversation with David Mullin about which of them might end the day last, the two of them swapping the position back and forth across the back nine. Alex resolved the question with the round's closing flourish: eleven shots dropped across the 16th, 17th and 18th — a double at the par-5, a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 17th, a triple at the last. He finished on 29, alone at the foot.

Troia was where he settled in. Two separate 13-shot bleeds: holes 4 to 9, a triple followed by five straight doubles; and holes 13 to 17, where a triple-bogey 7 at the par-4 13th was, statistically and emotionally, the decisive drop to the bottom of the Spoon race. From that point — the 13th hole of Round 2, the 31st hole of the week — Alex was bottom for the remaining 41 holes of the tournament. The position was never threatened again.

Penha Longa added a 12-shot bleed across holes 11 to 14, the centrepiece a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 12th. By then it scarcely registered. The week's defining number, however, was held back for Estoril. Holes 5 to 10: double, a quad 9 at the par-5 6th, double, double, double, double. Fourteen shots dropped in six holes. And then, two holes later, at the par-4 12th, the septuple-bogey 11 that the tournament will be remembered for. He signed for 33. Final total: 127 points, alone at the bottom, exactly where the leaderboards had placed him after Round 1, Round 2 and Round 3.

## Round 1 — Oitavos sets the template {.round1 .round}

The Atlantic links opener did its work briskly. Within eighteen holes of Arthur Hills's dunes, all three competitions had their eventual winner — and loser — installed in pole position. Stuart's 39, five clear on Stableford. Gregg's 18 over, three clear on gross. Alex's 29, last by some distance, the opening-hole 9 and the closing 11-shot bleed bookending the kind of day that asks searching questions about why one is paying green fees at all. Jon Baker, for his part, strung together three pars at the 14th, 15th and 16th — proof that Oitavos was playable — before a double at the 17th cheerfully ended the discussion.

## Round 2 — Neumann bends, does not break {.round2 .round}

Troia's introduction to TEG was meant to be the week's fresh narrative. It was duly squandered. Stuart wobbled visibly — the quad at the 16th, the triple that capped the H10-12 burst at the par-4 13th — and still extended his Trophy lead. Jon Baker drew level with Gregg twice, at the 11th and the 14th, and never went ahead. And Alex, methodically, spent two stretches of the round dropping 13 shots apiece, the triple at the 13th the moment the Spoon race effectively closed. RTJ Sr. asked the questions; the field, mostly, returned bogeys.

## Round 3 — Penha Longa breaks the field {.round3 .round}

The mountain parkland in the Sintra hills was the decisive round in both top competitions. Gregg's Jacket lead leapt from 4 to 14. Stuart's Trophy lead from 7 to 12. RTJ Jr.'s course extracted its tariff from almost everyone: a quintuple-bogey 10 from Jon Baker at the par-5 12th, inside an 11-shot bleed across the 11th, 12th and 13th; a quad 8 from David Mullin at the par-4 16th; two 8s from Stuart at the 1st and the 14th; and the 12-shot bleed from Alex. Against all of which, Gregg's three-par run at the 13th, 14th and 15th, and his recovery birdie at the 5th after a ragged opening, were the day's only sustained pieces of grown-up golf.

## Round 4 — Estoril: a coronation and an 11 {.round4 .round}

Mackenzie Ross's parkland, Portugal's oldest club, was the fitting venue for a procession. With every race effectively decided, Estoril offered the trophies, the colour, and one number for the ages. Gregg's 11 over, his best round of the week, built on the H2-4 and H6-8 par parades, sealed the Jacket at +66. Stuart's 43, his best round of the week, did likewise for the Trophy, despite a 9 at the par-4 8th inside a nine-shot bleed of his own. And Alex's 33, his last in the basement, contained the week's two defining figures — the 14 shots bled across the 5th to the 10th, and the septuple-bogey 11 at the 12th.

After Round 4, Stuart Neumann led the Trophy. Gregg Williams led the Jacket. They have, between them and Alex, been leading their respective competitions since the second hole of Round 1.

## The men, in brief

- **Stuart Neumann** — Wire to wire, 39-35-39-43, 156 points, the Trophy by 13. Opened with nine points across Oitavos's first three, absorbed the Troia quad and the triple-bogey end to the H10-12 purple patch, took two 8s at Penha Longa without flinching, and signed off with his best round of the week despite a quintuple at Estoril's 8th. A front-runner's masterclass, conducted at a pace that suggested mild boredom.
- **Gregg Williams** — Never headed on gross across 72 holes. 18-22-15-11 for +66, the Green Jacket by 16, the fifth-best gross in TEG history. Broke the field with the three-par run at Penha Longa's 13th-15th, sealed it at Estoril with two three-hole nine-point bursts. One quad at the 14th the only blemish anyone could lodge against him. Runner-up in the Trophy for good measure.
- **Alex Baker** — Bottom from the quintuple at the very first hole of the week to the septuple at the par-4 72nd. 127 points, alone at the foot, via a sextuple 10 at Oitavos's 17th, the seal-it triple at Troia's 13th, a 12-shot bleed at Penha Longa and a 14-shot bleed at Estoril. The Wooden Spoon as a piece of long-form performance art.
- **Jon Baker** — Twice drew level with Gregg on gross at Troia, at the 11th and the 14th, neither outright, neither held. Penha Longa did for him: the quintuple 10 at the par-5 12th inside an 11-shot bleed across the 11th, 12th and 13th. A quad at Estoril's 11th confirmed it.
- **David Mullin** — Runner-up in the Jacket at +82, sixteen behind. Flirted with the Spoon on Oitavos's back nine before steadying, drew level briefly on Stableford at the 5th of Round 1, took a quad at Penha Longa's 16th, and answered with a tidy nine-point burst across Estoril's 11th, 12th and 13th — exactly the kind of grown-up stretch the rest of his week kept threatening to produce.
