# Baker's Coronation

There is a particular look a man gives his card when it contains a 51 on it. Alex Baker, walking off the 18th green at Boavista on the final afternoon, was wearing it: the expression of someone who has just signed for the highest Stableford round in the recorded history of TEG and is trying, with limited success, to appear as though that sort of thing happens to him all the time.

It does not. It has never happened to anyone. And the man who finally achieved it was the one who had spent the previous two TEGs as Trophy runner-up — bridesmaid in TEG 8, bridesmaid in TEG 9, and frankly running out of weddings to attend in any other capacity. Third time, then. By eleven shots. On 184 Stableford points. With a Gross-round personal best of +17 strapped to his back for company.

To understand quite how improbable the closing scene was, you have to rewind to Wednesday morning at Boavista, where David Mullin had, by tea-time, made the rest of the tournament look ornamental.

## Round 1 — Mullin Reduces Boavista to Rubble

Howard Swan's parkland routing above the Atlantic has staged eleven prior TEG rounds, and across fifty visits its course record had stood at 82. Mullin shot 75. Not a typo; a seven-shot beheading of the prior mark, completed on a Wednesday, in shorts, against a backdrop of polite Algarvean breeze.

The 75 was good for +4 — the lowest Gross round ever played at TEG, full stop — and 49 Stableford points, the second-best Stableford return in the same long ledger. By close of play Mullin led the Trophy by eight and the Jacket by fourteen. He had taken the Jacket lead at the 6th and would not be headed for the remaining sixty-six holes, an outcome you could see coming from approximately the 6th tee.

Behind him, the Spoon contest was already being briskly auditioned. Stuart Neumann, the reigning two-time holder and a man desperate to pass the cup on, briefly assumed the bottom slot after the 1st. The hand-off came at the 4th, where John Patterson — defending Trophy champion, lest anyone forget — took possession of last place and, in a quiet, dignified way that nobody had really asked for, declined ever to surrender it. He held it through the next sixty-eight holes of the tournament. Patterson also signed for a quintuple-bogey 10 at the 8th, one of the hardest holes on the course. Neumann, not to be outdone in the matter of grand round-closing statements, made a sextuple 10 at the 18th.

## Round 2 — Palmares Throws Open Its Doors

Palmares, a Robert Trent Jones Jr routing of twenty-seven holes split between Praia/Alvor and Alvor/Lagos, was making its TEG debut. The field, generously, responded by playing some of its finest golf.

Jon Baker shot +10 on the Praia/Alvor combination — the third-best Gross round in TEG history. Gregg Williams posted a Stableford round personal best of 48 points (with a Gross personal best of +14 for good measure). Alex Baker, in what we will later recognise as a man clearing his throat, posted his own Stableford round personal best of 45 and a Gross-round personal best of +23. Mullin's eight-shot Trophy cushion was suddenly five.

Two players did not get the memo. Patterson opened with a sextuple-bogey 10 at the 1st, which is a difficult way to begin a fightback and an even more difficult way to commence a round. Neumann, whose body language at this stage suggested a man trying to give away an expensive watch nobody would accept, contrived to bleed thirteen shots across holes 13 to 15 — a quintuple 10 at the par-5 13th, a quintuple 9 at the 14th, a triple 7 at the 15th — and then signed off with another sextuple-bogey 10 at the 18th, for the symmetry.

## Round 3 — A Lead Changes Hands. Twice.

At Palmares Alvor/Lagos, Alex Baker delivered another Stableford round personal best — 47 points, +22 gross — and produced the single most consequential par of his life at the par-4 6th, where a routine four nudged him into outright Trophy lead for the first time in the tournament. He had pulled level with Mullin and now stepped past.

It is a measure of the day's drama, and of the inadvisability of triumphalism in any TEG round before the 72nd green, that he then surrendered the lead at the 13th. Williams, bogeying the par-5, briefly assumed outright command of the Trophy. He held it for, generously, the time it took everyone to walk to the 15th tee, where Baker drew level and retook the lead and was not headed across the remaining twenty-one holes of the championship.

Baker's sequence, lest it sound too clean, included a quintuple-bogey 10 of his own at the 13th — a detail of which he will speak less in later years.

Patterson, meanwhile, was producing the kind of five-hole stretch that lives in folklore. Across 11 to 15 he shipped nineteen shots: a triple at the 11th; a quintuple 8 at the par-3 12th (the easiest hole on the course, an irony delivered with characteristic Patterson timing); a triple 8 at the 13th; a quadruple 7 at the par-3 14th; a quadruple 9 at the 15th. He also contributed a quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 8th, for the avoidance of doubt.

By close of play, Baker led the Trophy by eleven. Mullin still led the Jacket by ten. A pattern was hardening.

## Round 4 — Fifty-One

Back to Boavista for the closing day, then, where on Mullin's record-setting Wednesday the course had looked playable only to Mullin. On Saturday, Baker pulled it off its hinges.

Fifty-one Stableford points. The best round in TEG history. +17 gross — also his Gross-round personal best, for the trilogy of round personal bests to go with the Stableford personal bests in R2 and R3. The decisive stretch came across holes 5 to 10: twenty-one points in six holes — bogey, par, par, bogey, par, par — the bogey at the 8th coming on one of the hardest holes on the course, the par at the 9th coming on the easiest, the whole sequence delivered in the manner of a man cashing in some long-deferred dividend.

Mullin shot +13, which on any other afternoon would be the story; here it was administrative, tidying the Jacket up by twelve shots on +51. Jon Baker, runner-up on the Jacket for the fourth time in five TEGs without ever winning the thing, posted a Gross-total personal best of +63. Williams posted a Gross-total personal best of +76 and finished Trophy runner-up by eleven, his half-hour atop the leaderboard at the 13th in R3 his sole taste of the lead across four days. Alex Baker's own Gross-total personal best came in at +90.

And Patterson, defending Trophy champion eight months ago, posted his worst round to date: 21 Stableford points, +40 gross, including a sextuple-bogey 11 at the 8th — the same hole at which he had made a 10 in R1, on the same course, although by now the hole and Patterson were, frankly, on first-name terms.

## How the Three Things Were Decided

**The Trophy** is best understood as a slow correction. Mullin's +8 cushion after R1 became +5 after R2; the takeover came at R3 H6, the brief Williams interregnum at R3 H13, the retake by R3 H15, and from that point onward Baker was simply running out the clock — twenty-one holes of safe procession in R3, then 51 points of close-out in R4. Bridesmaid in TEG 8. Bridesmaid in TEG 9. Bride, by eleven, in TEG 10.

**The Green Jacket** was effectively over at Wednesday teatime. Mullin's 75 at Boavista — that course record across fifty prior visits, the best Gross round in TEG history — opened a 14-shot lead he was never seriously asked to defend. Wire to wire. Never closer than ten. An eighth Jacket. The Trophy, for the fifth straight TEG, remained elsewhere — a curious thinning of a CV that nonetheless still has eight Jackets in its top drawer.

**The Wooden Spoon** was the longest single-occupancy tenancy in recent memory. Patterson took it from Neumann at R1 H4 at Boavista and held it for the next sixty-eight holes. Round scores of 29, 35, 27, 21 — the last of those a personal worst. A double-figure hole in every round: 10 at Boavista's 8th in R1, 10 at Palmares' 1st in R2, 10 at Palmares' 8th in R3, 11 at Boavista's 8th in R4. Champion to Spoon-holder in twelve months, sealed on 112 points.

Neumann, the back-to-back reigning holder, did escape — but the manner of escape will trouble him later. His best Boavista sequence, a 98, was identical to his last visit there and sixteen off his Boavista personal best; on a week of personal bests for almost everyone else, he was the only Boavista regular who failed to improve. Three quintuple-or-sextuple holes, a 13-shot bleed across three holes of R2, and a deliverance procured chiefly by another man's collapse. A reprieve, not an acquittal.

## The Men, In Brief

- **Alex Baker** — Twice the bridesmaid, finally the bride. Round personal bests in R2 (45), R3 (47) and R4 (51 — the highest Stableford round in TEG history), a Gross-total personal best of +90, and the Trophy by eleven on 184. Even the quintuple at Palmares' 13th in R3 will be edited out of the official version in due course.

- **David Mullin** — Eighth Green Jacket, secured at teatime on Wednesday and merely maintained thereafter; +51, by twelve; a new Boavista course record (75) that erased the prior mark by seven across fifty visits, and the best Gross round in TEG history while he was at it. The Trophy, however, has now eluded him for five straight TEGs — a fact he will be reminded of, courteously, for as long as anyone can be bothered.

- **Gregg Williams** — Stableford round personal best (48) at Palmares, Gross-round personal best (+14) at Palmares, Gross-total personal best of +76, a brief and unforgettable thirty minutes atop the Trophy at R3 H13. And still runner-up by eleven. There are days when posting personal bests in three different categories is not enough, and this was four of them in a row.

- **Jon Baker** — The third-best Gross round in TEG history at Palmares Praia/Alvor in R2 (+10), a new Boavista personal best of 86 in R4, a Gross-total personal best of +63 — and Jacket runner-up for the fourth time in five TEGs without ever having one in the wardrobe. The nearly-man of nearly-men.

- **John Patterson** — Defending Trophy champion in March; Wooden Spoon holder by Saturday. Bottom from R1 H4 to the 72nd green; last in all four rounds; a double-figure hole in each of them; a worst-ever 21-point closer to seal it on 112. A small private museum, immaculately curated.

- **Stuart Neumann** — Released from his back-to-back Spoon tenancy, though largely on a technicality. Sextuple 10 at Boavista's 18th in R1; quintuple 10 at Palmares' 13th and sextuple 10 at Palmares' 18th in R2; a 13-shot bleed across R2 holes 13–15; and a Boavista 98 identical to his last visit, in a week when everyone else at Boavista went lower. Escape, but not by his own hand.
