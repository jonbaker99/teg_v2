# Baker's Boavista Masterclass

The scoreboard at breakfast read as neatly as anything TEG has produced in years: Alex Baker 133, Gregg Williams 133, the Trophy hanging on eighteen holes of Algarve parkland. By sundown the gap was eleven points, the record book had a fresh entry at the top of it, and the only sensible thing to say about Boavista's twelfth TEG outing was a single number.

**Fifty-one.**

That is what Alex Baker shot — fifty-one Stableford points, the highest round in TEG history, gross seventeen over par on a course that spent the afternoon mugging everyone else who tried to play it. It is the sort of number that doesn't so much win a Trophy as confiscate it.

It did not, however, look inevitable at the start. Williams was the first mover, and for a brief golden moment the only mover. A double-bogey 7 at the par-5 3rd and a bogey at the par-3 4th had begun to nibble at his card, but he answered immediately at the par-4 5th with a birdie 3 — and there, for one solitary hole, the Trophy race was a tie at the summit once again. That was the last pulse it had. Williams was level for the lead at the 5th green and never level again.

Because from the 5th onward, Alex Baker simply went to work. Bogey at the 5th for three points. Par at the par-3 6th for four. Par at the par-4 7th for four. Bogey at the par-5 8th for three. Par at the par-4 9th for three. Par at the par-4 10th for four. Six holes, twenty-one points, and a Trophy race that had started the morning as a coin-flip turned — quietly, methodically, without any apparent need for theatrics — into a procession. There were no fireworks in this stretch, which is rather the point: it was just relentless, par-shaped, handicap-shredding competence. Baker had walked into the round tied. He walked off the 10th green untouchable.

Williams, to his credit, did not concede in spirit, but Boavista was no longer interested. The par-3 6th — where Baker had just collected his routine four points — bit Williams for a quadruple-bogey 7. Worse followed across 12, 13 and 14: double, double, double, six shots gone in three holes. A back-nine flurry of his own briefly threatened to make the margin respectable — nine points across 15-17, bogey-par-par, a tidy stretch that on any other afternoon would be the headline — before the par-4 18th finished him off with a quadruple-bogey 8. Forty points. On most days, plenty. On this one, eleven adrift.

While the Trophy was being rewritten in light, the Green Jacket was being sealed in dusk. David Mullin began the day ten gross strokes clear and ended it twelve gross strokes clear, which is about as much fuss as Mullin has ever made about anything. There was no flourish, no signature stretch, no need for one. Gross golf's most understated champion simply turned his ten into a twelve and went to find a beer. Jon Baker, for his part, claimed the runner-up spot in the Jacket race with a tidy 9-point burst across 15-17 — bogey, par, par, the very same notes Williams was humming a fairway away — to nail down second, twelve behind Mullin.

Spare a thought, then, for Stuart Neumann, who lost the round before most of us had finished our coffee. Double bogey at the 1st. A quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 2nd. A double-bogey 7 at the par-5 3rd. Eight shots gone in three holes is the sort of opening from which Boavista does not allow a recovery, and it duly did not allow one.

And then there is John Patterson, whose afternoon will be filed under "career-worst, please do not reopen". Twenty-one points. A sextuple-bogey 11 at the par-5 8th — the same hole where Alex Baker was collecting his metronomic three — a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 10th, and a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 18th to sign off. The Wooden Spoon, which had been hovering at a 14-point distance overnight, was secured by a yawning 32. There is losing a race, and there is losing it by a country mile, and then there is Boavista on Sunday.

The three races, then, all moved in the same direction but at very different speeds: the Trophy lurched from dead heat to landslide on the back of Baker's six-hole surge from the 5th; the Jacket margin crept from ten to twelve as Mullin did Mullin things; the Spoon gap more than doubled from 14 to 32 as Patterson's card unravelled. One round, three coronations, one record.
