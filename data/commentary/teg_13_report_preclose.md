# Baker Takes Kent

## In medias res: the 18th green, Prince's, late Friday afternoon

Gregg Williams has just drawn level on the Trophy. Not in front — level — but level is a place Williams has spent a not-inconsiderable portion of his career staring at the back of Jon Baker's head from, and after thirty-five holes of patient harrying around the Dunes and Himalayas loops, level will do for now. The Trophy, having looked like a procession after Thursday, is suddenly a contest.

Baker then holes for birdie at the last. A 3 at the par 4. Outright lead, restored.

This is, in retrospect, the entire tournament in a single putt: Williams getting his fingers to the trophy, Baker calmly removing them. It would happen one more time, at Littlestone the following lunchtime, and the answer would be louder.

---

By that point on Friday evening, of course, the Green Jacket had already been over for about twenty-six hours. Baker had arrived in Kent — TEG's first visit, four new links courses in four days, twenty player-course records all guaranteed to be set in the maiden voyage sense of the term — and posted a +13 at Royal Cinque Ports for a nine-shot Jacket lead by suppertime. He never let the cushion fall below seven. By Sunday evening it was eighteen. Zero lead changes across seventy-two holes. The Open venue at Deal had handed him the title on a tray and he had simply carried it, undropped, through Sandwich, across the Romney Marshes, and back again.

The Trophy was the live wire. The Spoon, meanwhile, was being passed around the field like a hot potato nobody wanted to be holding when the music stopped — and the man who ended up clutching it, David Mullin, would not arrive at last place until the tenth hole of the final round.

## Round 1 — Baker Lands Running at Deal

The Thursday round at Royal Cinque Ports, an Open venue making its TEG debut, set the template with brutal efficiency. Baker opened par-par-birdie at the first three for ten points and never looked rattled. 39 Stableford points, +13 Gross, lead in both competitions before tea.

The rest of the field, broadly, was being beaten up by the links. John Patterson — defending Trophy champion, lest we forget — assembled a card that read like a bailiff's inventory: a quadruple-bogey 8 at the 1st, another 8 at the 10th, a third 8 at the par-5 16th, and, garnishing the lot, a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 7th. Alex Baker contributed his own 8 at the 11th and bled nine shots across holes 4 to 7 — double, triple, double, double, a small tributary of carnage. Mullin opened triple-bogey, bogey, bogey, double before a birdie at the par-5 5th briefly suggested order.

The Wooden Spoon last-place spot, meanwhile, was being handed around like a contagious cold. Patterson had it at the 2nd. Williams at the 3rd. Mullin at the 4th. Williams again at the 5th. Alex Baker took it at the 12th. Patterson reclaimed it at the 16th and held on to the close. Six different occupancies in eighteen holes. A premonition, had anyone been minded to read one, that this particular sub-competition was going to be coy about declaring itself.

## Round 2 — Williams Knocks, Baker Answers

The Friday round at Prince's, Dunes out and Himalayas back, is where the Trophy briefly looked stealable. Williams, methodical to a fault, pieced together a strong run — pars at 11, 12 and 13 for nine points — and drew level on the Trophy at the 15th. Not outright; level. Baker had taken a bogey at the 4th — SI 2, one of the hardest holes on the course — and a double at the 5th, and had needed a birdie at the par-5 6th to stop the bleeding.

Then the 18th. Baker, birdie 3, par 4, outright lead restored. The round in totals: 36 Stableford points, +16 Gross, Jacket margin trimmed from nine to seven. Trimmed, never threatened.

The day's tally of trouble was again kept mostly by Patterson, who lost eleven shots to par across his opening four — 8 at the 1st, double at the 2nd, triple at the 3rd, double at the 4th — and added a second quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 17th. Mullin made his own 8 at the par-4 9th. Alex Baker closed with five consecutive double bogeys at 14 through 18, ten shots dropped in a closing march of perfect, dreadful regularity. Patterson was last on the Spoon table for the second night running.

## Round 3 — Baker's +8 Buries the Field at Littlestone

If there is a single round in this report that earns its capital letter, it is the Saturday at Littlestone — Purves, with later fingerprints from Braid and MacKenzie, the architect's signature shared with Royal St George's, which the players had presumably driven past on the way in.

Williams, undeterred, drew level on the Trophy again at the 1st. A par. Not outright; level. The third time in two days that Baker's name had been joined at the top of the column.

Baker's response, across the next ten holes, was the moment the Trophy left the building. A stretch of par-birdie-par-par at the 8th through 11th — the birdie at the par-3 9th, the par at the 10th coming on SI 1, the hardest hole on the course — was worth thirteen points on its own. The round in full: 44 Stableford points, +8 Gross. The second-best Gross round in the history of TEG. The Trophy gap restored to five, the Jacket lead ballooned to fourteen. The door, having been ajar at the 1st, was now closed, bolted, and the key thrown into the marsh.

Williams, it should be said, played beautifully. Fifteen points across holes 6 to 10 — four pars and a bogey at SI 1 — and another nine across 12 to 14. On almost any other Saturday this is a Trophy-winning afternoon. He happened to be playing on the Saturday Baker decided to post a number that goes in the record book.

And then there is Patterson, defending champion, whose week refuses to be summarised in fewer than two columns. In Column A, the Saturday: 49 Stableford points, the third-best round in TEG history, and a personal-best +12 Gross. In Column B, the same scorecard, which also contained a quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 17th and a slide to the bottom of the Spoon race at the 5th — a bogey 6 at the par-5 — because in a tournament where the Gross totals are this savage, even 49 points in a day can leave you going backwards in the wrong competition.

Alex Baker took an 8 at the par-4 2nd and inherited last place on the Spoon at the 9th. The Wooden Spoon, two days from home, was still refusing to settle.

## Round 4 — Mullin Finds the Bottom at the 10th

Sunday at Prince's, Shore out and Dunes back, was largely Jon Baker carrying a parcel across a road. +13 Gross, 39 Stableford points, three pars at 12-13-14 worth nine, a bogey at the 15th, a double at the 16th, a birdie at the par-3 17th to restore the bookkeeping. Trophy by 6 on 158 points. Jacket by 18 on +50. The double sealed.

The day's other headlines belonged to the supporting cast. Patterson, true to the two-column thesis, eagled the par-5 2nd — the only eagle of the entire tournament — and then put a quadruple-bogey 9 on his card at the par-5 15th, because of course he did. Alex Baker posted a personal-best +17 Gross, the timing of which, with the Spoon already lost to him on Saturday night, has a certain pure quality to it.

And David Mullin, defending Spoon holder, finally arrived at the destination he had been keeping at arm's length all tournament. Third after Thursday, third after Friday, fourth after Saturday — the reigning champion of last place stubbornly refusing to play the part. Then the 10th at Prince's Shore: a par 4, SI 3, one of the hardest holes on the course. Quintuple-bogey 9. Last place for the first time in seventy-two holes' worth of trying not to be. He did not climb back. Third Spoon in a row.

## How the three were decided

**The Trophy.** Baker led at every checkpoint — gaps of 6, 1, 5, 6 — but the gap of 1 is the story. Williams drew level at R2 H15 (not outright). Baker birdied the 18th to retake outright. Williams drew level at R3 H1 with a par (not outright). Baker posted +8 Gross at Littlestone. Two equalisers, both answered, the second answered with a record-grade round. Final margin: six points. Had any one of Baker's three answering moments — the birdie at Prince's last, the 8th-to-11th stretch at Littlestone, the +8 round entire — gone the other way, this is a different report. None of them did.

**The Green Jacket.** Decided on day one. Baker's nine-shot lead at Deal was never trimmed below seven, and his Littlestone round took it to fourteen with eighteen to play. Zero lead changes across the tournament. The counterfactual here is thin: there is no point in the four days at which any rival was within a single round's variance of him.

**The Wooden Spoon.** The genuinely live race. Patterson held last after R1 and R2 despite the Saturday 49. Alex Baker held it after R3. Mullin, who started Thursday in third, declined to occupy last place at any point until the tenth hole of the final round, at which point he committed to it with a quintuple-bogey 9 on SI 3. Final: 135 points, third consecutive Spoon, a sentence that ought to come with a small ceremony.

## The men, in brief

- **Jon Baker.** Two prior Trophies, two prior Jackets, back-to-back Jackets coming in, and now a Kent double that brooks no argument. Wire-to-wire Jacket by 18. Trophy by 6, with Williams answered at the 18th on Friday and answered again, comprehensively, with the second-best Gross round in TEG history on Saturday. The form player, confirmed.
- **Gregg Williams.** Three prior Trophies; second again. Drew level twice, led outright never. On any week Baker doesn't post +8 at Littlestone, this is his fourth Trophy. Baker posted +8 at Littlestone.
- **John Patterson.** Defending Trophy champion. The week in two columns. Column A: 49 Stableford points at Littlestone, third-best round in TEG history; a personal-best +12 Gross the same afternoon; the tournament's only eagle, at the par-5 2nd on Sunday. Column B: quadruple-bogey 8s at R1 holes 1, 10 and 16; a quintuple-bogey 9 at R1's 7th; 8s at R2 holes 1 and 17; a 7 at R3's 17th; a 9 at R4's 15th. A man holding two scorecards at once.
- **David Mullin.** Reigning Spoon holder, eight prior Jackets, two prior Trophies, and now a third Wooden Spoon in succession, sealed by a single hole on Sunday afternoon. From eight Jackets to four Spoons describes a career arc that no statistician would attempt to fit a line through.
- **Alex Baker.** A prior Trophy winner reduced this week to skirmishing for the wrong title. Held last place briefly at the 12th on Thursday and from the 9th onwards on Saturday. Closed with a personal-best +17 Gross on Sunday — a figure arriving, with impeccable timing, the moment it could no longer help him.
