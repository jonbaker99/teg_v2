# Baker's Algarve

## The 51

Boavista in the late-afternoon sun, the Atlantic doing its picture-postcard impression in the middle distance, and Alex Baker — twice a runner-up, once again the man most likely to finish second — walks off the 18th green at Howard Swan's parkland having signed for 51 Stableford points.

Fifty-one. The highest single-round tally in the history of TEG, full stop. A personal-best gross of +17 thrown in as an aside. A coronation, dressed as a Sunday card. The Trophy had been settled some hours earlier — settled, really, the previous afternoon at Palmares — but the manner of the settling was the kind of thing that makes a man rearrange the mantelpiece on the flight home.

The bridesmaid era, as everyone in the field had quietly come to think of it, ended in Portugal. To understand quite how, we have to rewind to Saturday morning, before anyone had unpacked.

## Round 1 — Mullin Settles the Jacket Before Lunch on Day Two

Boavista, TEG's eleventh visit to Howard Swan's parkland, and David Mullin opened TEG 10 by removing one of the three competitions from circulation. A round of +4 gross. Forty-nine points. The best Gross round in TEG history, and the second-best tally, posted in the same eighteen holes, before the sweat had cooled on anyone else's grips.

The Jacket race, three rounds still to play, was over. Mullin led the Gross by 14 and the Trophy by 8. Jon Baker, to his credit, briefly drew level on the Gross at the 9th — a tiny flicker of resistance, quickly smothered when Mullin reclaimed the lead and proceeded to treat the rest of the week as a regulatory exercise.

Elsewhere, the early outlines of two darker stories. Stuart Neumann, the reigning Wooden Spoon-holder arriving with back-to-back wooden spoons to defend, signed off his round with a 10 at the par-4 18th. And John Patterson — the defending champion, no less — ran up a quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 8th, SI 2, one of the hardest holes on the course. He dropped to last on the 4th. He would remain there for the next 68 holes.

## Round 2 — Palmares Debuts; Personal Bests Rain Down

TEG's first day at Robert Trent Jones Jr.'s Palmares, the Praia and Alvor nines, and the field — never having seen the place before — promptly used it to redraw its own ceilings.

Alex Baker: 45 points, +23 gross, a personal best on both counts. Gregg Williams: 48 points, +14 gross, personal bests on both. Jon Baker: +10 gross, which slotted in as the third-best Gross round in TEG history. Three men, three career-best rounds, all on a course none of them had stood on before lunch.

Mullin's Trophy cushion, until now an 8-point armchair, trimmed to 5. The Jacket race, of course, did not move at all.

At the other end of the leaderboard, the warnings of Round 1 escalated into something more architectural. Patterson opened his round with a 10 at the par-4 1st — a sextuple bogey, before the day had really started. Neumann then put on his own private exhibition across the 13th, 14th and 15th: a 10 at the par-5 13th, a 9 at the 14th, a 7 at the 15th. Thirteen shots bled in three holes. He signed off, as if to underline the theme, with another 10 at the 18th. And still — *still* — Patterson was below him in Stableford. The Spoon contest had, by the close of Saturday, the contours of a foregone conclusion.

## Round 3 — Baker Takes the Wheel at Palmares Alvor/Lagos

If the Jacket had been settled on Friday evening, the Trophy was settled on Sunday afternoon. A 5-shot deficit, converted into an 11-shot lead, inside a single round. That is the cold arithmetic. The lived experience was rather more interesting.

The outright lead changed hands at the 6th, when Baker — a par at the par-4 — moved ahead of Mullin for the first time all week. It shifted again at the 13th, where Gregg Williams, in the middle of the best four-day stretch of his TEG life, stole it back with a bogey at the par-5. And then it changed for the final time at the 15th, when Baker drew level and pulled clear, never to be headed again. He signed for 47 points and +22 gross. Another personal best on both metrics; the third of the week, in three rounds. (Lest the round be remembered as flawless, he also dropped a quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 13th of his own. The 51 was still a day away.)

The Patterson recital, meanwhile, took a baroque turn. Across the 11th through 15th he shed nineteen shots: a triple at the 11th, a quintuple 8 at the par-3 12th, a triple at the 13th, a quadruple 7 at the par-3 14th, a quadruple 9 at the 15th. He had earlier deposited another quintuple 10 at the par-5 8th — his second in a week at an 8th hole, and not, you may have begun to suspect, his final instance.

## Round 4 — 51 and Done

Back at Boavista, the bookend round, and the moment we opened with. Baker's 51 points are now in the books, but it is worth dwelling on the shape of them — because it isn't a player simply firing all the way through. It is a stretch.

Holes 5 to 10 at Boavista: bogey, par, par, bogey, par, par. Twenty-one Stableford points across six holes. The par at the 6th, the par at the 9th — SI 18, the easiest hole on the course, but still — and, most quietly impressive, a bogey at the par-5 8th, SI 2, one of the hardest. The same hole, by the way, on which Patterson had earlier in the week posted his quintuple 10. The same hole on which, this very afternoon, he posted a sextuple-bogey 11.

Patterson's final round was a personal worst: 21 points, +40 gross. The 11 at the 8th was the headline, but the whole card read as a kind of farewell tour of every par-5 in the Algarve. Mullin, untroubled, signed for the Jacket. Baker claimed the Trophy. Williams, who had played the week of his life, took second. The week, narratively, was complete.

## How the Trophy was won

Alex Baker, 184 Stableford points, won by 11 from Gregg Williams. The defining sequence: a 5-shot deficit after Round 2, converted into an 11-shot lead by the end of Round 3, extended to the final 11-shot margin by the record round at Boavista. The decisive moment was the 6th at Palmares Alvor/Lagos on Sunday — the par at the par-4 that put Baker outright in front for the first time. Williams's cameo at the 13th was the last time anyone but Baker held the lead in TEG 10. After two TEGs as the man in second, Baker stops finishing second.

## How the Green Jacket was won

David Mullin, +51 gross, won by 12 from Jon Baker — his eighth Green Jacket, and the fifth in succession. The Jacket was decided on Friday afternoon. His +4 opening round at Boavista — best Gross round in TEG history — opened a 14-shot cushion that the rest of the week was never asked to defend; he led after every round at +14, +13, +10, +12. Jon Baker, runner-up for the fourth time in five TEGs, did everything he could: a +10 round at Palmares Praia/Alvor that slots in as the third-best Gross round in TEG history, a personal-best Gross total of +63. None of it threatened the man at the top, because he had finished threatening anyone before Saturday breakfast.

## How the Wooden Spoon was lost

John Patterson, 112 Stableford points. Bottom from the 4th hole of Round 1 to the final putt of Round 4 — 68 consecutive holes at the foot of the leaderboard, an unhurried, almost stately reign at the bottom. The case for the prosecution is the par-5s: a 10 at Boavista's 8th in Round 1, a 10 at Palmares' 1st in Round 2 (a par-4, in fairness — he diversified), a 10 at Palmares' 8th in Round 3, an 11 at Boavista's 8th in Round 4. A man with a clear and recurring grievance against any hole numbered 8. Stuart Neumann, despite his own sextuples and the 13-shot bleed at Palmares, escapes the Spoon and ends the back-to-back streak — but only because, somewhere above him on every leaderboard, the defending Trophy champion was quietly hosting an exhibition of how to lose 50-odd shots without anyone making much of a fuss.

## The men, in brief

- **Alex Baker** — Trophy winner, by 11. Personal-best rounds in R2 (45), R3 (47) and R4 (51 — a TEG record). Personal-best gross rounds in three of four. Personal-best gross total of +90. Two TEGs as runner-up; now, finally, one as the man with the cup. The bridesmaid era ends in Portugal.
- **David Mullin** — Green Jacket no. 8, won by 12. The +4 at Boavista in Round 1 was the round of his life and arguably the round of TEG's life; it also rendered the Jacket race a formality before anyone had checked into their room. Briefly led the Trophy, too, before being out-Stablefordled by the man on his shoulder.
- **Gregg Williams** — Trophy runner-up, on the back of a week of personal bests at Palmares (48 pts, +14 gross, +76 gross total). Held the outright lead for precisely two holes of Round 3 before Baker remembered what he was here for. Outrun, not outplayed.
- **Jon Baker** — Jacket bridesmaid, fourth time in five TEGs. Personal-best gross total of +63, anchored by the +10 at Palmares Praia/Alvor — the third-best Gross round in TEG history. Drew level on the Gross at R1 hole 9; that was as close as anyone got to Mullin all week.
- **John Patterson** — Wooden Spoon, 12 months on from the Trophy. Bottom for 68 consecutive holes. A 10, a 10, a 10, an 11 — three of them on a par-5 8th. His worst round of TEG so far in the contest that mattered least, by which point his place on the bottom rung was a matter of public record.
- **Stuart Neumann** — Arrived with two consecutive Spoons; leaves with neither. Three sextuples, the 13-shot bleed at Palmares 13–15, two 10s at par-4 18ths — and the second-worst total in the field is, in this company, a kind of escape.
- **Henry Meller** — Mid-pack, quiet, untroubled by either the records or the recitals. In a week of personal bests and personal worsts, a man content to be neither is, on reflection, the field's most centred presence.
