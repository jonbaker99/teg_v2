# Carnage on the Estoril Coast {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 8 | Round 3 | 27/11/2015 | Quinta da Marinha</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> JB 35 | HM 34 | AB 32 | GW 29 | DM 26 | SN 20</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> JB +18 | HM +24 | DM +26 | GW +35 | AB +40 | SN +43</p>

The scoreboard does the talking, and what it says is unprintable in polite company. A Trophy lead of eleven, sliced to five. A Green Jacket lead changing hands at the 12th. A Wooden Spoon deficit stretched from a manageable eight to a yawning, almost editorial twenty-two. Robert Trent Jones Sr., from beyond the grave, took six grown men by the lapels at Quinta da Marinha and shook loose every coin in their pockets. Eights, nines, tens — and, just to round things off properly, an eleven. This was less a round of golf than a controlled demolition above the Estoril coast, with the elevated greens and that famously abundant bunkering doing precisely the job they were drawn up to do in 1984.

Let us start, as the carnage demands, with the overnight Trophy leader. **Gregg Williams** arrived at the turn with the air of a man whose biggest decision was which Portuguese white to order with dinner. An eleven-shot cushion. A procession. And then, in the space of four holes, the wheels, the axle and most of the chassis came off. A triple-bogey 7 at the par-4 8th was the polite cough. The double-bogey 5 at the par-3 9th was the throat-clearing. Then came the main address: a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 10th, followed — because why not — by a quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 11th. Fourteen shots, four holes, gone. For good measure he added another quad, an 8 at the par-4 13th. He still leads. His cushion shrinks from eleven to five, and he leads a man who didn't do anything daft all day, which is a rather different proposition.

That man is **Jon Baker**, the round's quiet beneficiary and, by close of play, the new Green Jacket leader. Baker's round will not feature in any anthology of attacking golf, but he did the one thing nobody else could manage at Quinta da Marinha: he kept the big figures off the card. He drew level with Mullin on Gross with a par 4 at the 12th — the exact hole, the exact moment — and from there he never trailed again. Five back in the Trophy, two clear on the Jacket. Everything to play for, and rather a lot of it now in his hands.

**David Mullin** began the day six clear on Gross and the bookmakers' choice for the Jacket; he ended it two adrift and nursing his worst TEG round to date — 26 Stableford points, a figure he will not be embroidering onto a cushion. The hinge was a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 12th, the precise hole at which the Jacket changed hands. Credit where due: Mullin gathered himself, drew level again at the par-4 15th with a par 4, and for a brief moment the lead was shared. He couldn't hold it. Two behind Baker at the close, and the procession he'd been leading now belongs to somebody else.

**Alex Baker** endured what can only be described as a grim middle stretch — and "stretch" is doing some heavy lifting there. Three triple bogeys in succession at the 3rd, 4th and 5th cost nine shots before he'd really got going. The par-4 8th then produced a sextuple-bogey 10, which is the sort of number that requires a small ceremony. The par-4 12th brought a quadruple-bogey 8, just to keep the theme consistent. The card's lone oasis was a birdie 3 at the par-4 17th, a moment of unexpected clarity that broke a long, dispiriting run of bogey-or-worse and reminded everyone he can, in fact, play.

**Henry Meller** stayed sensibly out of the headline races and instead curated a scorecard with admirable symmetry: a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 13th to open the conversation, and a quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 18th to close it. Bookended. Tidy, in its way.

Which leaves **Stuart Neumann**, and the Wooden Spoon, and the kind of opening five holes that one hopes is eventually laughed about over a drink at some great distance in time. A quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 1st set the tone. A double-bogey 5 at the par-3 2nd kept it going. A double-bogey 7 at the par-5 3rd, a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 4th and a triple-bogey 6 at the par-3 5th completed a five-hole haemorrhage of seventeen shots. The back nine, if anything, was worse in its individual moments: a quintuple-bogey 8 at the par-3 9th, and then — the day's signature score — a sextuple-bogey 11 at the par-5 10th. Eight back overnight; twenty-two back tonight. The engravers may begin sharpening their tools.

The three races moved as one might expect when only one man in six avoids a card-wrecker. The Trophy, a procession at breakfast, is a contest at dinner — Williams' lead shrinking from eleven to five, with Jon Baker the man closing. The Green Jacket changed hands for the first time, Baker drawing level at the 12th, Mullin briefly drawing level back at the 15th, and Baker pulling clear by two at the close. And the Wooden Spoon, eight adrift overnight, is now twenty-two adrift and, barring a final-round miracle of the sort Quinta da Marinha did not suggest is forthcoming, effectively engraved.
