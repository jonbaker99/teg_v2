# Procession in the Sun: Boavista, Records, and a 12

The 17th at Boavista is a par four. On the afternoon of 5 October 2010, Henry Meller, with the unhurried commitment of a man genuinely persuaded that the next shot would be the redemptive one, made it a par twelve. Eight over. The worst score relative to par recorded on any hole in TEG history.

While this was happening — while one TEG record was being engraved in real time on a single Algarve hole — Jon Baker was twelve holes into another. And David Mullin, paired somewhere in the procession behind, was constructing a third. By the time the three men handed their cards in, two trophies were, for all practical purposes, already decided, the record book was smoking gently, and Boavista's 17th had announced itself as the only character in this story with any genuine appetite for jeopardy.

What followed across the next three days was a coronation in stereo, accompanied by a single, grim sub-plot about who would be left holding the spoon. The headline margins — 18 in the Trophy, 12 in the Jacket — do not really capture it. The competitions never tightened. They only loosened.

## Round 1 — Two records, one par-twelve

Take Baker first, because the maths demands it. A net 63 on a Howard Swan parkland he was seeing for the fourth competitive day in his life. Nine under his handicap allowance. The lowest round in TEG history to that point, full stop, no qualifiers. He finished the day eight clear in the Trophy, after a single brief moment of company at the 6th when Mullin drew level — not led, but tied — before Baker resumed his quiet excavation of the field.

Mullin, meanwhile, was busy with a record of his own. A gross +17, the lowest Gross round ever recorded at TEG, and a net -1 that was itself the third-best round in the competition's history to that point. He moved into a share of the Jacket lead at the 4th and never gave it back. Two clear by sundown.

So: on the same afternoon, on the same course, the two most important pieces of paper in the tournament were already, in essence, written. The pursuit had not so much been waved off as quietly euthanised before tee-off on day two.

And on the 17th, Meller. The par-four 17th at Boavista — a hole that would acquire, over the course of the week, the gravitational pull of a small celestial body — took eight shots from him beyond what it was entitled to. The 12 dropped him to the bottom of the Wooden Spoon ledger at that very hole, and that is where he ended the round, looking at a four-day haul of golf with the spoon already in his eyeline.

## Round 2 — Meller's resurrection, briefly

If Round 1 was the record book burning, Round 2 was the chasers, briefly and movingly, attempting to chase themselves.

Meller — and one must pause here, because the man who had just written the worst hole in TEG history did, the very next day, produce one of its best rounds — shot a net -2 off a gross +18. At the time, the second-best round in TEG history. Also the second-best Gross round in TEG history. A man simultaneously authoring two entries near the top of the all-time list and one entry at the absolute summit of the all-time list of disasters. The card lifted him off the bottom of the Spoon standings; Stuart Neumann, after a bogey at the par-five 8th, took over at the foot.

Baker, undeterred by anyone else's heroics, added a net -2 of his own, then itself the second-best round in TEG history. The Trophy lead, eight overnight, became thirteen.

Mullin had what passes, in this tournament, for a wobble. A quintuple-bogey 9 at the 17th — Boavista's hungry par four, claiming its second victim of the week — and yet still a gross +21, and still an extension of the Jacket lead to five. The chase, the actual pursuit, was already conceptual.

## Round 3 — Mullin breaks his own record; Meller breaks himself

Three days in, Mullin broke his own TEG record. A gross +14 for a net -4, eclipsing the mark he had set on Tuesday afternoon. The third-best Net round in TEG history into the bargain. The Jacket lead grew to nine.

Baker, characteristically, declined to be outdone. A net -7, which displaced Meller's Round 2 as the second-best round in TEG history. The Trophy lead reached sixteen. The shape of his card had a stretch through the middle that was almost unfair: across holes 6 to 10 he gathered seventeen Stableford points off a sequence of bogey-par-bogey-par-par. Bogeys on a par three and a par five, pars on three par fours. A man cleaning up.

Behind these two, lesser earthquakes. Neumann posted a gross +18, the third-best Gross round in TEG history at the time, the foundation of his eventual Jacket runner-up finish. Gregg Williams shot a net -4 — the third-best round in TEG history then standing — which was a curious thing for him to produce, given that his week elsewhere was a kind of touring exhibition of the quadruple bogey, scattered liberally across both nines and all four rounds.

And Meller. Oh, Meller. A triple at the par-four 1st sent him to the bottom. Williams briefly relieved him there with a quadruple at the par-three 2nd. Then, at the par-five 3rd, Meller made a quintuple-bogey 10, and the Spoon returned to him with the heavy, ceremonial finality of an inheritance. He stayed bottom for the rest of the tournament. The Spoon's lead had changed hands nine times across the week; the tenth move never came.

## Round 4 — The coronation, and one last 17th-hole offering

There was nothing left to play for at the top, which Boavista, generously, recognised by serving up one final sacrifice on the 17th. Stuart Neumann, the man quietly assembling a Jacket runner-up finish, made a sextuple-bogey 10. The hole had now devoured Meller, Mullin and Neumann in successive rounds — a recurring character with a recurring trick.

Baker shot level par to seal the Trophy at -18, winning by eighteen, the only meaningful uncertainty being whether the margin would be a round number. It was.

Mullin shot a gross +20, which included a quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-three 11th, and took the Green Jacket at +72 — twelve clear of Neumann. The Jacket margins, round by round, had gone 2, 5, 9, 12. A line drawn with a ruler.

Meller signed for +16 net vs par and collected the Wooden Spoon.

## How it was actually won

**The Trophy.** Baker led after every round of the tournament. The only lead change occurred at hole 6 of Round 1, when Mullin briefly drew level. Margins thereafter: 8, 13, 16, 18. A procession, but — and this is the part worth saying — a brilliant one. Three of the rounds were, at the time they were played, top-two-ever material. The Trophy was won by a man playing the best four days of golf anyone had played at TEG, against opposition that was not, in any meaningful sense, opposition.

**The Green Jacket.** Mullin led from hole 4 of Round 1 to the final green, never headed once. He twice set the TEG record for lowest Gross round, the second time at his own expense. He survived a 9 at the 17th in Round 2 and a 7 at the 11th in Round 4 without the lead ever genuinely contracting. Margins: 2, 5, 9, 12. A Jacket won not by a flourish but by the steady refusal of the chasers to chase.

**The Wooden Spoon.** The bottom of the standings changed hands nine times, all of them in the first three rounds, most of them involving Meller. He fell to last with his 12 at the 17th in Round 1, escaped via the second-best round in TEG history in Round 2, and returned to last for keeps via the 10 at the 3rd in Round 3. A wild ride, with a thoroughly settled destination.

## The men, in brief

- **Jon Baker** — Won the Trophy by eighteen, in the most emphatic fashion the format allows. -9, -2, -7, level. The first of those a TEG record at the time; the next two each second-best-ever at the time of posting. Within all this brilliance he found room for a quadruple at the par-four 5th and a quintuple at the par-four 18th in Round 3 — proof, of a sort, that he was a person.

- **David Mullin** — Twice broke the TEG record for the lowest Gross round in the space of three days, led the Jacket wire-to-wire, and won by twelve. Also took a 9 at the 17th and a 7 at the 11th and the Trophy runner-up spot, the last of these the natural consequence of the first two.

- **Henry Meller** — The week's most volatile scorecard, by a distance. A 12 at the 17th in Round 1, the second-best round in TEG history in Round 2, a 10 at the 3rd in Round 3, the Wooden Spoon at the end of it. Some men have weeks; Meller had a small anthology.

- **Stuart Neumann** — Runner-up for the Jacket, twelve back, the position built on a gross +18 in Round 3 that was the third-best Gross round in TEG history at the time. Undone slightly — though not at all in the standings — by a 10 at the 17th in the final round.

- **Gregg Williams** — Produced a net -4 in Round 3, the third-best round in TEG history at the time of writing. Around it, a connoisseur's collection of quadruple bogeys: at the par-three 6th and par-four 18th in Round 1, the par-four 10th in Round 2, the par-three 2nd in Round 3, and the par-five 3rd, par-four 7th and par-four 12th in Round 4. A career in miniature.
