# Palmares Pulls Both Ways {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 10 | Round 2 | 01/10/2017 | Palmares - Praia / Alvor</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> GW 48 | AB 45 | JB 45 | DM 42 | SN 37 | JP 35</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> JB +10 | DM +11 | GW +14 | AB +23 | JP +25 | SN +33</p>

Palmares, on its TEG debut, produced something close to a Rorschach test. On the same warm Algarve afternoon that Gregg Williams and Alex Baker were filling in personal-best Stableford cards in the kind of dazed, double-checking handwriting reserved for career rounds, John Patterson and Stuart Neumann were busy carding three separate scores of nine or ten between them. Robert Trent Jones Jr's links-parkland mashup, it turns out, rewards rhythm and punishes its absence with the enthusiasm of a man being paid by the stroke. One course. Every kind of round.

**David Mullin** held the fort, as he tends to, with the air of a man who knows exactly which holes he is allowed to lose shots on. The serene centrepiece was a five-hole run from the 4th — the par-5 4th, the par-3 5th, the par-4 6th, the par-4 7th, the par-3 8th — fifteen Stableford points harvested with metronomic disinterest. The only blot came at the par-5 9th, where a double-bogey 7 reminded everyone, possibly including Mullin, that even leaders can be nicked here. It was not the day's best round — Alex Baker outscored him by three — but it was efficient enough to stay atop the Trophy and nudge the Jacket gap outwards by a stroke. As ever, you have to actually beat him to beat him.

**Alex Baker** very nearly did. His personal-best 45 points was built on a fast start — nine points from the opening three holes (bogey, bogey, par) — and a second surge of ten across holes 5–7, the four-pointer at the par-4 7th the kind of thing Alex normally only allows himself in dreams. Then came the middle stretch that he will replay on the flight home: three consecutive double bogeys at the 12th, 13th and 14th, six shots haemorrhaged in three holes. It cost him second position in the Jacket — he slipped to fourth on gross — but the Stableford raid was real enough to close the Trophy gap to Mullin from eight to five. Suddenly the leaderboard has a pulse.

**Gregg Williams** played the round of his TEG life. Forty-eight points. Gross +14. Both numbers personal bests, both requiring a second look in the column. The engine room was a remarkable 18-point stretch from the 8th to the 12th — level at the par-3 8th, level at the par-5 9th, *birdie* at the par-4 10th, level at the par-3 11th, bogey at the par-4 12th — and he closed in the same vein, twelve more points across 15–18, finishing with bogeys at the par-5 17th and par-4 18th that felt almost lazy in context. The reward: level with Alex Baker on 86 in the Trophy, clear into third in the Jacket, and the rarefied private satisfaction of knowing you have just performed as well as you currently know how.

**Jon Baker** had his own quietly admirable afternoon, even if the headline 45 in gross terms felt slightly less than the constituent parts. A birdie at the par-5 2nd had him purring early, ten points banked across the first three. Then a double-bogey 7 at the par-5 4th snapped the start in two. He responded with the kind of patience the course seemed to be asking for — six consecutive pars from the 5th through the 10th, eighteen points, no fuss — and that's largely what kept him second in the Jacket, even as Mullin's margin stretched from 14 to 13.

And then, the carnage. **Stuart Neumann** had actually been playing himself into contention — a tidy 14-point run from the 9th to the 12th (par, bogey, par, par) suggested a man finding his feet on debut. What followed was one of the more vivid back-nine collapses TEG has logged. A quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 13th. A quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 14th. A triple-bogey 7 at the par-4 15th. Thirteen shots dropped across three holes — not a stretch, a haemorrhage — and then, for the souvenir programme, a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 18th to sign off. Last in the Jacket, second-bottom in the Trophy, 22 adrift and still slightly shell-shocked.

Which leaves **John Patterson**, whose round was effectively over by the time the rest of us had finished our coffee. A sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 1st is not a start so much as an ending. He never recovered, and remains rooted to the foot of the Trophy table — the Spoon a problem now actively widening rather than narrowing.

The day's race-shifts were modest but real. The Trophy lead was *halved* without changing hands — Mullin's eight became five, and Williams gate-crashed second alongside Alex Baker on 86. The Jacket stretched the other way by a single stroke, 14 to 13 over Jon Baker, while Williams elbowed his way clear into third and Alex Baker slid to fourth. And at the bottom, the Spoon gap between Patterson and Neumann widened from three to five — the 10 at Patterson's opening hole and Neumann's back-nine implosion effectively cancelling each other out at the wrong end of the table.
