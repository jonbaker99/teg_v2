# Carnage on the Coast {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 8 | Round 3 | 27/11/2015 | Quinta da Marinha</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> JB 35 | HM 34 | AB 32 | GW 29 | DM 26 | SN 20</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> JB +18 | HM +24 | DM +26 | GW +35 | AB +40 | SN +43</p>

There are rounds in which one man falls apart and the rest watch politely from the fairway. Round 3 at Quinta da Marinha was not one of those rounds. Robert Trent Jones Sr.'s elevated targets and forest of bunkers — on TEG's second visit to the Lisbon coast — set about the field with the indiscriminate enthusiasm of a man with a strimmer and a grudge. Five of the six players posted at least one quadruple-bogey-or-worse. Between them they ran up four quintuple-bogeys-or-worse. Neumann, in a piece of statistical landgrab that deserves its own plaque, carded all four of those himself.

And yet, somehow, the leaderboards did move. They moved meaningfully, in fact. The Trophy cushion more than halved, the Green Jacket changed hands at a precise hole at a precise moment, and the Wooden Spoon hardened from a position of mild discomfort into something approaching permanence. All while the actual golf was, by common consent, a war crime.

**Stuart Neumann** set the tempo before most people had loosened their grip. A quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 1st was followed by a two-stroke at the par-3 2nd, another two-stroke at the 3rd, a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 4th and, for variety, a triple-bogey 6 at the par-3 5th. That is seventeen shots leaked across the opening five holes, which is the sort of arithmetic that tends to settle a Spoon race before the second coffee. He wasn't done. A quintuple-bogey 8 at the par-3 9th and a sextuple-bogey 11 at the par-5 10th bled a further 13 shots across 9–11. Final tally: 20 points, four quintuples-or-worse, and a gap to the field that swelled from 8 to 22. The Spoon is no longer a contest; it is a fixture.

**Alex Baker**, perhaps inspired, perhaps merely caught in the same weather front, contrived his own greatest hits package. Triple-bogey at the 3rd, triple at the 4th, triple at the 5th — nine shots gone in a tidy little three-hole bundle. A bogey and a two-stroke offered a brief intermission before the headline act: a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 8th, the centrepiece of a 13-shot bleed across 7–10. A quadruple-bogey 8 was added at the par-4 12th for completeness. Mercifully, a birdie at the par-4 17th finally broke what had become a near-archaeological streak of bogey-or-worse holes. 32 points, which under the circumstances counts as a sort of victory.

Which brings us to the day's hinge. **Gregg Williams** had arrived in Cascais with an 11-shot Trophy cushion — the kind of buffer that allows a man to order the second bottle. He spent most of the front nine confirming the wisdom of that order, and then the back nine arrived. Holes 8 through 11 went: triple-bogey 7, two-stroke 5, quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 10th, quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 11th. That is fourteen shots bled in the space of four holes, including a 10 and a 9 inside the same scorecard square. A quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-4 13th was an aftershock rather than a fresh disaster. He signed for 29 points. The Trophy lead survived; the comfort did not.

**David Mullin** began the day six clear in the Green Jacket race and ended it two behind, which is the sort of swing that requires a specific, locatable moment. That moment was the par-4 12th, where a quadruple-bogey 8 played itself out in real time and Jon Baker, walking the same fairway, drew level on gross at precisely that hole. Mullin's 26 points are his worst round to date — a phrase no leader ever wants attached to the day he loses the coat. He did not recover the advantage.

**Jon Baker** is the day's quiet winner, which is the only kind of winner worth being on an afternoon like this one. No headline blow-up, no card-killer hole, no narrative pyrotechnics — just 35 points, the round of the day, and a smooth glide past Mullin at the 12th to take the Green Jacket lead and stretch it to two by the close. He also took six shots out of Williams's Trophy cushion without appearing to break sweat. On a day when everyone else was bleeding, Baker simply walked between the wounded.

**Henry Meller** contributed his quota to the day's tally — quadruple-bogey 8s at the par-4 13th and the par-4 18th, bookending the back nine with a pair of identical disasters — but stayed well clear of the Spoon weather. 34 points, the second-best round of the day, and a reminder that on a course like this one, surviving counts.

The Trophy lead held but more than halved — 11 became 5, and Jon Baker is now within a single good round of Williams with one to play. The Green Jacket flipped at the 12th, as cleanly as these things ever flip, with Mullin's six-shot overnight lead converted into a two-shot deficit over a single afternoon. And the Spoon, as discussed, is now a piece of civil engineering: 22 shots, four quintuples, and no realistic path back for Neumann.
