# Sandwich, Signed and Sealed: Mullin Survives the Sextuple

There is a particular kind of silence that descends on a links green when a man who is three behind and charging — who has, in the previous hour, made the leader look not just beatable but actively breakable — picks his ball out of the cup and writes down a 10. It is the silence of a tournament ending mid-sentence. It happened on the 16th green at Prince's on Sunday afternoon, and everything else that occurred during Round 4 of TEG 14 either led Alex Baker to that hole or watched, slack-jawed, as he left it.

To understand the 16th, you have to understand what preceded it. Baker began the day eight adrift of David Mullin and announced his intentions on the first tee with the subtlety of a foghorn. Par, par, par at the 1st, 2nd and 3rd — ten Stableford points in three holes, which is the sort of opening that makes a leader check his shoelaces. A double-bogey 6 at the 4th interrupted the procession but didn't blunt the threat; Baker simply kept walking towards Mullin with his sleeves rolled up. By the time he stood on the 13th tee, the contest had quietly become a contest again, and then he went and made it a proper one: par at the 13th, par at the 14th, par at the 15th — two par 4s and a par 5 dispatched for eleven more points — and the Trophy, which had spent the entire week tucked under Mullin's arm, was suddenly out in the open air.

What made all this matter, of course, was that Mullin had cracked the door open himself. The leader's afternoon turned on the 10th, a par 4 he played in 8 for a quadruple bogey and a blank on the card. One hole, four shots dropped, and the eight-shot cushion that had looked impregnable at breakfast was suddenly something a charging Baker could plausibly chew through. For the next few holes the Trophy genuinely flickered. Mullin's lead shrank. Mullin's lead wobbled. Mullin's lead held — but only because Prince's 16th had other plans for the chaser.

A 10. On a par 4. Sextuple bogey, zero points, and with one stroke of the pencil the tournament was over. Mullin had not so much closed Baker out as watched him walk into a lamp post. It is the rarest and cruellest of golfing exits: the man playing the round of the day, eliminated by the round of the day. The Trophy was Mullin's by two, won not by sealing it but by being the one still upright when the dust settled.

The day's other long surge belonged, improbably, to Jon Baker. Arriving 13 behind in the Spoon race and presumably resigned to the cutlery drawer, he picked the 12th tee as the moment to remember he could play. Birdie at the par-5 12th. Birdie at the par-4 13th. Pars at the 14th and 15th. Fourteen Stableford points across four holes, two under gross, and for a flickering moment the Wooden Spoon was a live competition rather than a foregone conclusion. Then Prince's 16th, which had already decided the Trophy, turned its attention to the Spoon: a double-bogey 6 to halt the run. The 18th delivered the coup de grâce — a quadruple-bogey 8 to close the round and confirm what the overnight gap had always implied. Cold comfort, but real comfort, is that Jon's gross card across the day was strong enough to vault him past Gregg Williams into second on the Green Jacket. Spoon in one hand, silver in the other.

Which brings us, inevitably, to Gregg Williams, the day's quietest man. No headline beat, no foghorn opening, no four-hole burst, no sextuple anything — just a steady slide from second to third on the Trophy, second to third on the Green Jacket, and a Spoon margin that has shrivelled from comfortable to merely sufficient. Twelve points clear of bottom is twelve points clear of bottom, but it is not the cushion he set out with.

And so to Mullin, the champion. He completes the double — Trophy by 2, Green Jacket by 7 — having spent the afternoon with a quadruple bogey on his card and a man making birdies in his rear-view mirror. It was not, in the end, a coronation. It was a survival. But the record book does not record the manner, only the margin, and the margin says: champion of TEG 14, both ways.

The shape of the day, then: the Trophy gap closed from eight to two and only because Prince's 16th intervened; the Green Jacket runner-up slot changed hands as Jon Baker's gross card lifted him over Williams; and the Wooden Spoon, briefly a contest through that glorious 12–15 stretch, was reclaimed by the same closing holes that decided everything else.
