# Brother, Can You Spare a Lead?

There are golf courses where the first hole is a gentle handshake, and there are golf courses where it is a slightly awkward conversation about money. El Prat's Azul, on TEG's second-ever visit to Catalonia and the course's debut on the schedule, fell somewhere in between — a Greg Norman parkland on which precisely nobody in the field had a single shred of local knowledge to lean on. Which is presumably why, by the time Alex Baker holed out for a bogey 5 at the par-4 1st, the Trophy lead was already loose in its moorings. A bogey, in Barcelona, was apparently enough to draw level with his brother. Jon's two-shot cushion, lugged carefully through customs and onto the first tee, had not survived the walk to the second.

Two holes later the day's other race tilted as well. David Mullin parred the par-4 3rd, and by the simple maths of a quiet hole he had drawn level with Jon Baker at the top of the Green Jacket standings. For about twenty minutes, in the Catalan sunshine, both of TEG's headline races were tied. It was the calm bit. It did not last.

The opening stretch then revealed its teeth. Gregg Williams, who would otherwise have had a perfectly serviceable day, signed for an 8 at the par-4 5th — a quadruple bogey that did for any back-nine ambitions he might have been nursing and which, in the standings sense, rather defined his round by omission. One hole later it was Mullin's turn. The par-3 6th yielded him a 7, another quadruple, and the first proper crack in a card that would keep cracking. Jon Baker, playing the same hole, made a quiet bogey 4 — and because Mullin's level-pegging cameo at the 3rd had now been firmly retracted, that single dropped shot was enough to restore Jon's outright Green Jacket lead. The wobble had wobbled, briefly, and then settled.

From there Jon went into full coronation mode. Par at the 7th, par at the 8th, birdie 4 at the par-5 9th: ten Stableford points across three holes, the kind of burst that in real time looks unanswerable and that, in retrospect, makes everything that follows feel crueller. Behind him, John Patterson was quietly assembling a nine-point sequence of his own across 8-10 — bogey, bogey, bogey at a par-4, par-5 and par-5, three Stableford points each — the unglamorous handicap-golfer's version of a charge, and the engine that would, by the close of play, lever him off the bottom of the Spoon table.

And then 11. And then 12. And then 13.

Jon Baker's Trophy lead did not so much erode at the start of the back nine as fall down the stairs. Double-bogey 6 at the par-4 11th. Double-bogey 5 at the par-3 12th. Triple-bogey 7 at the par-4 13th. Seven shots over par in three holes — and the timing could scarcely have been worse, because Alex was right there, playing the same stretch with the unhurried air of a man who has noticed something. Par at the 12th. Par at the 13th. Par at the 14th. Nine Stableford points, returned with interest. The par at the 13th was the one that did it: a routine four on a par-4 that, on any other afternoon, would barely register, but which on this one carried Alex Baker into the outright Trophy lead for the first time. Same two-shot gap as the morning, opposite direction. Fraternal, and final-ish.

Mullin, meanwhile, had been quietly descending. His bogey 4 at the par-3 12th was the moment, in pure standings terms, that he slid beneath John Patterson and took up sole occupancy of the Wooden Spoon — a position Patterson had held overnight and would, briefly, escape. Briefly, because the Azul had one more joke to tell: Patterson signed for a 9 at the par-4 16th, a quintuple bogey, the kind of number that reminds everyone the climb out is not the same as being out.

The Trophy lead changed hands today; the Jacket lead survived a scare; the Spoon found a new tenant. Jon Baker began the round two clear of his brother and ends it two behind — the gap preserved, the direction reversed, and the same surname on top. The three-shot Jacket margin over Mullin is unchanged from overnight, Mullin's cameo at the 3rd notwithstanding. And Mullin himself now trails the field by exactly the five that Patterson did 24 hours ago. Nothing in TEG ever really gets resolved; it just rotates.
