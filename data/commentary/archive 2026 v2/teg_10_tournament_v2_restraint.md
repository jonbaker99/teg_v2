There is a moment, somewhere around the 10th green at Boavista on a Sunday afternoon, when the arithmetic stops feeling like arithmetic and starts feeling like history. Alex Baker had played six holes — the 5th through the 10th — and pocketed 21 Stableford points. Twenty-one. Bogey, par, par, bogey, par, par, in that order, on a card that already had Gregg Williams drawing level at the start of it and would, by the close, leave Williams disappearing into the Atlantic spray eleven points adrift. Baker would sign for 51. Nobody in TEG history had ever signed for more.

To understand quite how improbable a coronation that was, we have to rewind four days, to a different Algarve afternoon, and a different man with the scoreboard at his feet.

## Round 1 — Mullin Lights Up Boavista with a 49 {.round1 .round}

TEG's fourth Algarve pilgrimage opened on familiar ground — the eleventh time the tour had set foot on Boavista's clifftop fairways — and David Mullin made the most of his local knowledge. Forty-nine points. Plus four gross. The second-best round in TEG history at the time of writing, and an eight-point gap on the field by sundown. From the 6th hole of the tournament onwards he was top of the Trophy AND top of the gross, the latter a perch he would still be occupying 66 holes later.

Behind him, almost invisibly, sat Alex Baker on 41. A perfectly respectable second place which would have been a perfectly remarkable second place had he not also carded a quadruple-bogey 7 at the par-3 2nd and a quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 8th. The phrase "out of it" was deployed. So was "eight back". So, presumably, was a quiet word with himself.

The afternoon's other contributions belonged to the cellar. John Patterson found a quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 8th, slid to the bottom of the Trophy at the 4th hole of the tournament, and — though no one knew it yet — booked his accommodation there for the duration. Stuart Neumann, meanwhile, completed his round with a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 18th, the first instalment in a four-day study of how to lose 16 shots over the closing stretch at Boavista. He had managed exactly that across holes 15 to 18.

## Round 2 — Palmares Debuts, Personal Bests Follow {.round2 .round}

To Palmares, then — Robert Trent Jones Jr.'s links-parkland hybrid, the Praia and Alvor nines making their TEG debut. Unfamiliar turf has a habit of producing one of two things: bewilderment, or a personal best. Day two produced both.

Gregg Williams went round in a career-best 48 points (+14 gross), the round of a lifetime arriving with no warning and remarkably little fuss. Alex Baker, perhaps having had the requisite quiet word, posted his own personal best of 45 (+23). Mullin held, but his eight-shot lead had been pruned to five, and the field was beginning to look less like a procession.

The bewilderment, inevitably, was downstairs. Patterson opened his round with a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 1st — a way of saying good morning that rather set the tenor of the day. Neumann's afternoon was more thermodynamically alarming still: from the 13th to the 15th he leaked 13 shots in three holes, a quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 13th, a quintuple-bogey 9 at the par-4 14th and a triple-bogey 7 at the par-4 15th. He then, for symmetry, signed off with a sextuple-bogey 10 at the par-4 18th — a different course from the previous day's venue, but resulting in precisely the same numeral on the scorecard. Consistency is a virtue.

## Round 3 — Baker Takes the Wheel {.round3 .round}

Palmares again, this time the Alvor and Lagos nines, and the tournament's pivot. Baker posted yet another personal-best 47 points — his third career-best score in three days, in case anyone was still labelling him "out of it" — and at the par-4 6th took the outright Trophy lead for the first time. The Mullin era, it turned out, had lasted 60 holes.

It was not, however, quite a clean inheritance. Williams nosed in front at the par-5 13th, briefly the outright leader of TEG 12 in his own right, before Baker reclaimed matters by the 15th. By close of play the margin stood at eleven, which felt, depending on your vantage point, either decisive or precisely the sort of cushion that gets demolished by a Williams 48 on the final day.

The card was not unblemished. Baker found a quintuple-bogey 10 of his own at the par-5 13th — proof, if any were needed, that you can post 47 Stableford points with a 10 on it and still be the best player in the tournament. Patterson, true to fixture, ran up a quintuple-bogey 10 at the par-5 8th. The 8th hole at Palmares Alvor was, by this point, a Patterson postcode.

## Round 4 — Fifty-One: The Best Round TEG Has Ever Seen {.round4 .round}

Back to Boavista, then, and back to where we came in.

Williams, who had spent the week threatening to do something extraordinary, did it: a birdie at the par-4 5th drew him level with Baker at the top of the Trophy. The contest was, briefly, a contest.

What happened next is the bit that goes in the record book. Baker's next six holes, in order: bogey at the par-4 5th, par at the par-3 6th, par at the par-4 7th, bogey at the par-5 8th, par at the par-4 9th, par at the par-4 10th. In points: 3, 4, 4, 3, 3, 4. Twenty-one points in six holes. Williams could not respond because nobody could have responded. By the time Baker walked off the 10th green the tournament was, in any meaningful sense, finished.

He kept going to 51. Plus 17 gross, 51 Stableford, the highest single round in TEG history. The final margin in the Trophy was eleven, on 184 to 173.

Mullin, perfectly serene in his parallel competition, signed for +13 to wrap up the Green Jacket by 12 from Jon Baker — a margin that had been 14 after two rounds and was never genuinely under threat, despite a +23 wobble at Palmares Alvor/Lagos that included a quadruple at the closing hole. Jon Baker, meanwhile, had been quietly excellent throughout: at one point during the Palmares debut he played the 5th through the 10th in 18 consecutive pars, which was the cleanest stretch of golf nobody mentioned because Williams was busy posting 48 in the same group.

And then there was Patterson. A closing 21 — a personal worst, to round off a week of milestones — including a sextuple-bogey 11 at the par-5 8th. The 8th had now given him 10, blank, 10, 11 across four rounds. He was bottom from the 4th hole of the tournament to the 72nd. Sixty-eight holes of uninterrupted residency.

## How they were won

**The Trophy.** Mullin led by eight after R1 and five after R2. Baker took the outright lead at the 6th of R3, led by eleven that night, was drawn level by Williams at the 5th of R4, and then played the next six holes in 21 points. 184 plays 173.

**The Green Jacket.** Mullin led the gross from the 6th of R1 to the 72nd of R4, was drawn level once (by Jon Baker at the 9th of R1) and never headed. Rounds of +4, +11, +23, +13 for +51, winning by 12.

**The Wooden Spoon.** Patterson, bottom from the 4th hole of the tournament to the last. Rounds of 29, 35, 27, 21. Tens at the 8th in R1, the 1st in R2, the 8th in R3, and the 11 at the 8th in R4. Total: 112.

## The men, in brief

- **Alex Baker** — Eight back, then five, then in front. Three personal bests in three days (45, 47, 51), the last of them the highest Stableford round TEG has ever produced. Trophy winner by eleven. A coronation, in instalments.
- **David Mullin** — Lit the fuse with 49 at Boavista, led the Trophy for two rounds and the gross for all four. Surrendered the Stableford on Sunday at Palmares but kept the Green Jacket by 12. A consolation prize most players would frame.
- **Gregg Williams** — A career-best 48 at Palmares, the outright Trophy lead at the 13th of R3, level pegging at the 5th of the final round. Then ran into a six-hole burst nobody could have lived with. Second, eleven back, with no obvious regret.
- **Jon Baker** — Drew level for the gross lead at the 9th of R1, then settled into Mullin's slipstream and never left it. Eighteen consecutive pars on the Palmares debut. Runner-up for the Jacket by 12, and quietly the second-best ball-striker of the week.
- **John Patterson** — Bottom from the 4th of R1 to the 72nd of R4. A 10 at the 8th, a 10 at the 1st, a 10 at the 8th, an 11 at the 8th. A closing personal-worst 21. Sixty-eight holes of uninterrupted last place, and 112 points to show for it.
- **Stuart Neumann** — A walking Boavista cautionary tale plus a Palmares one. Three tens (two of them at an 18th, on two different courses), 16 shots dropped over the closing four of R1, and 13 more given back in three holes at Palmares Praia. The Algarve owes him nothing.
