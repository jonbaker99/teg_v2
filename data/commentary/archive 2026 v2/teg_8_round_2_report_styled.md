# Sintra Hills, Heavy Going {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 8 | Round 2 | 26/11/2015 | Penha Longa</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> GW 41 | AB 36 | DM 36 | JB 34 | SN 31 | HM 27</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> DM +14 | JB +19 | GW +24 | SN +27 | HM +30 | AB +34</p>

By the time the leaders reached the 16th tee at Penha Longa — a par four of no obvious malice on the card — the round was already a small humanitarian crisis. Three of the field would walk off it with an 8 or worse. Triple, quad, quint: a steady drumbeat of disaster on a single hole. It was the moment the round revealed its true character, but it was not the only such moment, and to understand it properly we have to start much earlier, at the foot of the mountain.

Penha Longa's Atlantic Course is new ground for TEG — Robert Trent Jones Jr.'s dramatic hillside layout, on only our second visit to the Lisbon Coast — and from the first tee it was clear the terrain itself was going to be the dominant force. The course doesn't so much ask questions as set traps. The front nine kept its powder largely dry. The back nine emptied the magazine.

The first warning shot came early, and it came from Alex Baker. Through the 6th, 7th and 8th — par five, par three, par five — he went 7-5-7: three double bogeys, six shots gone in the space of about forty minutes. At the time it looked like a mid-round wobble, the sort of thing a man dusts himself down from. With hindsight it was a tremor before the earthquake.

Williams, meanwhile, sauntered to the turn with his Trophy cushion intact and the air of a man who'd done his homework. Then came the 11th. A double-bogey 6 at the par four was nothing to lose sleep over. But the 12th — a par five — produced a **quintuple-bogey 10**, the kind of number that doesn't fit comfortably on a scorecard or in the memory. The 13th followed: another par four, another double-bogey 6. Nine shots gone in three holes. He bled more on the way home — double-bogey 5 at the par-three 15th, triple-bogey 7 at the 16th, double-bogey 5 at the 17th, another seven shots in three holes — and yet, somehow, in the round's most quietly absurd statistic, his **41 Stableford was the best round in the field**. On Penha Longa's terms, hanging on counted as triumph.

Meller was on the same stretch of mountain at roughly the same time, and got the same treatment, only worse. From the 11th to the 14th he carded 6-8-7-6 — double, triple, triple, double — **ten shots dropped in four holes**. For good measure he added a **quintuple-bogey 9 at the 16th**. His **27 Stableford was the worst round of the day**, and it slid him further off the Trophy pace.

By now the 16th was developing a reputation, and Alex Baker arrived to confirm it. He'd already followed his front-nine warning with four consecutive double bogeys at 11-12-13-14 — 6-7-6-6, eight shots — and then the closing trio went off in his hands like a faulty firework: a **quadruple-bogey 8 at the 16th**, a double-bogey 5 at the 17th, and a **quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-five 18th**. Ten shots gone in three holes to finish. **Gross +34** for the round, the worst of the day, and a slide from fifth to dead last in the Jacket standings.

Neumann's afternoon was a tour of the course's three nastiest holes and little else: a **quadruple-bogey 8 at the par-four 10th**, a **quadruple-bogey 9 at the 12th**, and — inevitably — a **quintuple-bogey 9 at the 16th**. Three blow-ups, 31 Stableford, and still anchored to the foot of the Trophy.

Which leaves the two men who actually played the course rather than wrestled it. Jon Baker put together a round with **no blow-ups recorded** — a phrase that in this company reads like sainthood. 34 Stableford, +19 gross, and on a day when everyone else was bailing water he quietly climbed to clear second in both the Trophy and the Jacket. He did nothing spectacular. He didn't need to.

And then Mullin. The round's true winner, in the sense that matters: **the only player in the field to navigate the back nine without a major blow-up**. **Gross +14**, the lowest score of the day, 36 Stableford to accompany it. While the rest of the field was carving 8s and 9s and 10s into the Sintra hillside, Mullin simply… didn't.

The Trophy race didn't change hands — Williams still leads — but the texture of it transformed entirely. A four-shot cushion became eleven, and not because the leader was clean. He shipped a 10 at the 12th and seven over 15-17, and still walked away with the day's best card. The Jacket, by contrast, did shift in character if not in name: Mullin turned a one-shot edge into a six-shot advantage in a single afternoon, the kind of move that starts to look like a verdict. At the bottom, the Spoon paradoxically tightened — Neumann is still last, but Meller's misery dragged him eight closer, where he had been twelve clear at dawn.
