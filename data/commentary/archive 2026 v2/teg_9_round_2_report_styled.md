# Patterson's 49: A Praia D'El Rey Masterclass {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 9 | Round 2 | 01/10/2016 | Praia D'El Rey</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> JP 49 | AB 44 | DM 39 | JB 37 | GW 36 | SN 30</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> DM +14 | JP +14 | JB +19 | AB +27 | GW +28 | SN +32</p>

There is a particular kind of silence that descends on a golfer who has just made triple-bogey at the 5th and then, with grim inevitability, triple-bogey at the 6th. Through nine holes at Praia D'El Rey, John Patterson embodied that golfer: eight shots dropped in a three-hole stretch, the Trophy lead he had carried into the day already dispatched, and the body language of a man calculating how early one can reasonably order a beer. He was, on any reasonable reading of the morning, the day's likeliest casualty. What followed instead was the second-best round in TEG history — a 49-point demolition built almost entirely on the back nine, a round so emphatic that it took a single afternoon to turn a one-point Trophy deficit into a ten-point lead. Praia D'El Rey, in its third TEG outing, served up its usual Cabell Robinson cocktail of parkland calm and coastal teeth; the fangs, predictably, bit hardest on the back, where most of the day's stories — both the masterpiece and the massacres — were written.

Patterson's morning, then, unfolded in something close to slow motion. A bogey at the 1st was enough on its own to nudge him into outright Trophy lead, a status he held for precisely the length of time it took David Mullin to par the par-5 2nd and draw level at the top. The pleasantries dispensed with, Patterson then went about systematically undoing himself: bogey, bogey, bogey to open, a double-bogey 6 at the 4th, and then those back-to-back triples at the par-4 5th and par-4 6th. Eight shots dropped from the 4th to the 6th. He stopped the bleeding the only way he knows how — a birdie at the par-5 7th — but at the turn there was still no hint that anything resembling a comeback, let alone a record assault, was in the post.

Mullin, having drawn level at the 2nd, then conspired to throw away the moment with a four-bogey trudge of his own. From the 6th he reeled off a 5, a 6 at the par-5 7th, a 4 at the par-3 8th and a 5 at the 9th — four bogeys, four shots gone, the Trophy lead drifting back to Patterson by default. To his credit Mullin steadied with a birdie 4 at the par-5 10th, and crucially never let the Green Jacket lead — his since Round 1 — out of his grip. By day's end the gross margin, just two overnight, had stretched to seven.

Jon Baker, the overnight Trophy leader and therefore the man with most to lose, lost most of it through the middle of the front. A double-bogey 6 at the 5th, a double-bogey 6 at the 6th and a triple-bogey 8 at the par-5 7th — seven shots in three holes, the kind of stretch from which a leaderboard position rarely recovers. To his great credit he then assembled the back nine's tidiest passage anywhere: par-par-par at the 11th, 12th and 13th (3-5-4), nine points and a hint that the round might yet be salvaged. Whereupon the par-3 14th took a quadruple-bogey 7 off him in a single swing of the wrist. A birdie 4 at the par-5 17th was honourable but no longer relevant: from overnight leader to joint third, eleven adrift.

The 9th, meanwhile, was the front nine's wreckage hole, and two men in particular wish it had been quietly removed from the routing. Gregg Williams put a sextuple-bogey 10 on the par-4 9th — a blank, no points, the kind of number that lingers in a scorecard like a smell in a fridge. Stuart Neumann matched the day's mood with a quadruple-bogey 8 there, his second quad of the morning after an identical 8 at the par-4 5th. Williams's round was otherwise a study in losing ground in every direction at once without ever quite slipping into Spoon range — small mercies. Neumann was not so fortunate. From the 11th he produced perhaps the day's bleakest sequence: triple-triple-triple, a 6 at the par-3 11th, an 8 at the par-5 12th, a 7 at the 13th. Nine shots gone in three holes, on top of everything else, and the Spoon gap — eight after Round 1 — yawning open to fourteen.

Alex Baker, in the middle of all this, was quietly assembling the round of his life. From the 7th he went bogey-bogey-par (a 6 at the par-5 7th, a 4 at the par-3 8th, a 4 at the par-4 9th) for ten Stableford points in three holes — the day's first sustained rhythm, and the platform for what would become a career-best 44. The back nine then tried very hard to derail him. A quadruple-bogey 9 at the par-5 10th, a double-bogey 5 at the par-3 11th and a double-bogey 7 at the par-5 12th meant eight shots dropped through 10-12; a second quad, a 7 at the par-3 14th, should reasonably have finished the job. That he still came in for 44 points — a personal best — is a small monument to the strange arithmetic of Stableford, where blow-ups are merely capped and strong holes still pay. A round of remarkable resilience on a brutal day.

Which leaves the main event. From the 9th — the very hole that had buried Williams and Neumann — Patterson played bogey-par-par-birdie-par: 5 at the 9th, 5 at the par-5 10th, 3 at the par-3 11th, 4 at the par-5 12th, 4 at the 13th. Seventeen points in five holes. The par at the 11th drew him level for the Trophy lead; the birdie at the 12th pulled him clear. And then, refusing to let the round become merely a recovery, he tacked on fourteen more across the closing four: par at the 15th, bogey 5 at the 16th, par 5 at the par-5 17th, bogey 5 at the 18th. Forty-nine points. Gross 14-over. The second-best round in TEG history, assembled from the most unpromising front nine imaginable, and — by the unforgiving logic of Stableford — quite sufficient to lift him from third overnight into a commanding Trophy lead.

The races, then, shifted hard. The Trophy was not so much rearranged as detonated: Mullin drew level briefly at the 2nd, Patterson equalized at the 11th, and from the 12th onward it was a procession, the overnight leader Jon Baker now eleven back and demoted to joint third with Alex Baker. The Green Jacket, by contrast, simply extended: Mullin's two-shot gross margin became seven, with Patterson's 14-over contribution sliding him up to third on that ladder too. And the Spoon, alas, hardened into something close to a vice — Neumann's deficit doubling from eight to fourteen, Williams's 10 at the 9th nowhere near enough to drag himself into the conversation.
