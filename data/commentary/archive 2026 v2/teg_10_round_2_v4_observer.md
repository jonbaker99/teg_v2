# Palmares Pulls the Field Apart {.round-report-title}

<p class="dateline">TEG 10 | Round 2 | 01/10/2017 | Palmares - Praia / Alvor</p>

<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Stableford:</span> GW 48 | AB 45 | JB 45 | DM 42 | SN 37 | JP 35</p>
<p class="round-scores"><span class="round-scores-header">Round Gross:</span> JB +10 | DM +11 | GW +14 | AB +23 | JP +25 | SN +33</p>

A fresh course on TEG's fourth visit to the Algarve, Palmares — Robert Trent Jones Jr.'s parkland-meets-links debutant — did exactly what new layouts tend to do: it stretched the field at both ends. The same eighteen holes that produced two personal-best cards at the top also yielded three separate double-figure scores at the bottom. By the time the players were back in the clubhouse the spread had widened in every direction at once, and the only man holding his ground in the middle of it all was, predictably, David Mullin.

**Gregg Williams** played the round of the day, and not by a small margin. His 48-point personal best — gross +14, for those keeping a parallel ledger — was built around a five-hole engine room from the 8th through the 12th worth a frankly absurd 18 points: pars at the par-3 8th and par-5 9th, a birdie at the par-4 10th, a par at the 11th, a bogey at the 12th. Having essentially won the round in those five swings, he then did the harder thing, which is not unwin it. Pars at the 15th and 16th and bogeys at the par-5 17th and par-4 18th delivered 12 more points across the closing stretch and lifted him into a tie for second in the Trophy on 86.

**Alex Baker** matched the personal-best theme with a 45 of his own, gross +23, and the shape of it was textbook front-loading. He opened 1–3 for nine points — bogey, bogey, par — and added another 10 across the 5th to 7th: bogey at the par-3 5th, bogey at the par-4 6th, par at the par-4 7th. The wheels then made a brief and noisy bid for freedom through 12–14, three consecutive double bogeys at the par-4 12th, par-5 13th and par-4 14th costing him six shots and any chance of outright second. He landed on 86 alongside Williams regardless, and the Trophy gap to Mullin — eight overnight — closed to five.

**David Mullin** did what Mullin does, which is appear barely to be playing the same tournament as everyone else. The middle of his card was a five-par run from the 4th to the 8th — par-5, par-3, par-4, par-4, par-3 — worth 15 points and the kind of stretch that quietly settles an afternoon. A double-bogey 7 at the par-5 9th briefly broke the spell, but by then the work was done. Trophy cushion: five. Gross lead over Jon Baker: down only from 14 to 13. A lead held, not a lead challenged.

**Jon Baker** had every right to feel hard done by. He opened 1–3 for 10 points — par, birdie at the par-5 2nd, par at the par-3 3rd — endured a double-bogey 7 at the par-5 4th, and then put together six straight pars from the 5th through the 10th across par-3, par-4, par-4, par-3, par-5 and par-4 for 18 points. On most days that is a round that climbs the leaderboard. On this one it was overtaken by two personal bests, and he slipped to fourth in the Trophy on 82.

**Stuart Neumann** found a four-hole purple patch from the 9th through the 12th worth 14 points — par at the par-5 9th, bogey at the 10th, pars at the 11th and 12th — which is when one starts to think there might be a round in here somewhere. The thought lasted three holes. The 13th to 15th bled 13 shots: a 10 at the par-5 13th, a 9 at the par-4 14th, a 7 at the par-4 15th. As if to make the day properly memorable rather than merely bad, he then signed off with a 10 at the par-4 18th.

**John Patterson** opened with a 10 at the par-4 1st. There is not a great deal more to say. The round did not recover from it, and never really threatened to; he remains anchored to the foot of the Trophy on 64, and the Wooden Spoon gap to Neumann — three overnight — duly stretched to five.

The Trophy is now a contest again: Mullin's eight-shot lead has been trimmed to five, with Williams and Alex Baker leapfrogging Jon Baker into a tie for second on 86 and the chasing order genuinely reshuffled for the first time. The Green Jacket, by contrast, barely moved — Mullin's +11 to Jon Baker's +10 nudged the gap from 14 to 13 and changed nothing of substance. And at the bottom, Patterson's 10 at the 1st did the day's quiet structural work, pulling him a further two clear of Neumann in the race nobody is running.
