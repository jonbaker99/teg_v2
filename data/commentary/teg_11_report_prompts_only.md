# The Double and the Downfall

For three of the last four TEGs, Jon Baker had stood at the prize-giving with the wrong sort of smile — Jacket runner-up, again. David Mullin, meanwhile, had spent those same years treating the Jacket as a kind of subscription service: two on the spin, eight in total. Catalonia turned the picture inside out. Jon left wearing both the Jacket and the Trophy. Mullin departed holding the Spoon. The bridesmaid kept the bouquet and threw the garter as well; the serial groom was sent to the kitchen with the washing-up.

That this happened across four rounds — two at the PGA Catalunya Stadium, one at its Tour sibling, and a debut on Greg Norman's Azul at El Prat — is the report. The shape of the inversion is the story.

## Round 1 — Stadium Opening: Jon Baker Sets the Tone

Spain's premier course, on TEG's second visit. Jon Baker opened with 35 Stableford and +18 gross, and from that moment to the final hole he never trailed in either competition. He led the Trophy by three and the Jacket by two by sundown — the kind of opening evening that gets filed under "promising" and only later, in hindsight, looks like an annexation.

The bottom of the field announced itself with equal directness. John Patterson, the reigning Spoon holder, picked up where he left off: a nine at the par-4 4th, then an 11-shot bleed across 12–14 — a triple at the par-5 12th, a nine at the par-4 13th, a triple at the 14th. By the time he holed out at 14, the Spoon's defending champion was already last. Gregg Williams contributed an eight at the par-3 5th, because that is what Gregg Williams does.

## Round 2 — 41 on the Tour: Jon Baker Stretches Out

The Tour course is, in theory, the gentler sibling. In practice it has a par-5 15th that ate the field. Patterson's contribution there was an 11 — six over par, no further questions — and Gregg Williams, playing in the same group and presumably with a front-row seat, answered with a 10 of his own. Two men, one hole, twenty-one shots between them.

While the 15th was conducting its inquest, Jon Baker was elsewhere doing something quietly extraordinary: 41 Stableford points, the highest round of the week, anchored by three pars in a row at 9–11 for nine points. He led the Trophy by two and the Jacket by three. Alex Baker, the defending Trophy champion, was still well inside touching distance, though a nine at the par-4 12th hinted that touching distance might come at a price.

## Round 3 — El Prat Flip: Alex Leads, Mullin Sinks

A new course for TEG, and the engine of every inversion that mattered. Greg Norman's Azul, in Barcelona, took both the Trophy race and the Spoon race and shook them by the ankles.

Alex Baker, defending champion, played as a defending champion ought. He drew level with Jon on the Trophy at the first, a bogey at the par-4 1st on one of the harder holes on the course. Then, on the back nine, he put together a stretch of three straight pars at 12–14 for nine points and went outright ahead at the 13th. Two clear overnight. The bridesmaid, for the first time in the week, was in second.

Jon had not been idle. A 10-point burst of his own at 7–9 — par, par at SI 1, birdie at the par-5 9th — had taken outright control of the Jacket at the 6th with a bogey at the par-3, and he held the gross lead overnight by three. But the Trophy, the one he wanted, had slipped.

For David Mullin, the Azul was where the eight-time Jacket holder discovered that history grants no exemptions. A quadruple bogey at the par-3 6th was bad. A bogey at the par-3 12th — stroke index 18, the easiest hole on the course, the hole on which one is supposed to bank a par and move on — was worse, because it dropped him below Patterson into the Spoon slot for the first time all week. Mullin had arrived in Catalonia defending eight Jackets. He left the 12th green on Saturday afternoon defending nothing.

## Round 4 — The Bridesmaid Walks Off with Both Bouquets

Back on the Stadium. Three things had to happen for the symmetry to land, and all three did.

First, Jon Baker had to catch Alex. He did so at the 6th, with a double at the par-4 — not the sort of stroke that normally features in a coronation, but a Stableford lead is a Stableford lead, and Alex was already coming apart on the same hole with a triple of his own. From that moment Jon did not trail again.

Second, Alex's defence had to collapse, and collapse it did, in three separate movements. Holes 4–6: a double, a double, a triple — seven shots gone. Holes 12–14: a triple, a triple, a double — eight more. Holes 16–18, including a quadruple at the par-4 17th and a double at the 18th (SI 2): another eight. The total damage was 27 Stableford, a personal worst, and a final margin of six. The defending Trophy champion had defended bravely for three rounds and then watched the fourth round happen to him.

Third, Mullin had to fail to escape the Spoon. He very nearly didn't fail. He drew level on the Jacket at the par-5 3rd with a par, the last hopeful gesture of his week, then ran into the closing stretch: a 7-shot bleed at 15–17 — a triple at the par-5 15th (which on this course is the easiest hole), a double at the 16th, a double at the 17th. That sealed both questions at once: Jon's Jacket, by three at +70; and the Spoon, on 125, to Mullin.

Gregg Williams, true to character, bled 11 shots of his own at 8–11 — a double at the par-3 8th, a double at the par-4 9th (SI 1), a nine at the par-4 10th, a double at the 11th — and finished mid-pack as ever, having supplied a collapse to every scoring round you'd want one in.

## How it was decided

**The Trophy.** Jon led from R1, was caught at R3 H1, lost the lead at R3 H13, drew level again at R4 H6, and won by six. The decisive moment was the double at the par-4 6th on Sunday — not for the score itself, but for what Alex was doing on the same hole.

**The Jacket.** Wire to wire on gross. Mullin twice drew level — at R3 H3 and R4 H3 — without ever taking the lead outright after R1. The decisive blow was Jon's bogey at the par-3 6th on the Azul, which reclaimed outright control on Saturday and was never relinquished. Final margin: three, at +70. Jon Baker's first Jacket, after three runner-up finishes in four TEGs.

**The Spoon.** Patterson carried the bottom slot from R1 H14 through to R3 H12, the 11 at the Tour 15th its centrepiece exhibit. Mullin took it from him with a bogey at the easiest hole on the Azul and never gave it back. The closing seven-shot bleed at the Stadium 15–17 was the last nail.

## The men, in brief

- **Jon Baker** — The bridesmaid, finally. Three runner-up Jacket finishes in four TEGs evaporated in a wire-to-wire Jacket and a Trophy reclaimed at a Stadium par-4 on the back nine of the last day. Won both. Said little.
- **Alex Baker** — Led the Trophy by two overnight and led no further. The personal-worst 27 on Sunday came in three separate instalments, each more thorough than the last; runner-up by six, and the defence is officially over.
- **David Mullin** — Arrived on back-to-back Jackets and eight in total; left with the Spoon. Pushed Jon to within three on gross, which is no consolation at all, because the bogey at the easiest hole on the Azul did what the quadruple at the par-3 6th could not, and the rest of the week could not undo it.
- **John Patterson** — Reigning Spoon holder, carried the bottom slot through two rounds — the 11 at the Tour 15th its signature exhibit — and escaped, improbably, because someone else contrived to be worse on the Saturday. Hand it on with relief.
- **Gregg Williams** — A collapse in every round it mattered: an eight at the Stadium par-3 5th, a 10 in lockstep with Patterson at the Tour 15th, a nine at the Stadium par-4 10th. Mid-pack as ever, and the colour man throughout.
